Fifty years after the Sixth Special Session, the Progressive International has declared a strategy of \'rebuilding the Non-Aligned movement\', and \'renovating the NIEO\'. However, key questions remain about the praxis of the original. The draft declarations of the programme of action and declaration on the establishment of a NIEO were brought to the UN by the leaders of 95 states, caucused as the G77. There proposals and strategies, therefore, were inherently plural. This begs the question of how such a \'common front\' had even been possible. This article offers a partial answer by exploring the strategic imaginaries that underlined the project. It first establishes that the Leninist ideas of Kwame Nkrumah and the liberal ideas of Raúl Prebisch converged at the level of world order. Then, by drawing on the work of Mohammed Bedjaoui, it shows that these contradictory tendencies were successfully synthesised in practice.

What would a successful NIEO have looked like? Clearly, this can\'t be known, because the core proposals of the NIEO were never tested in practice. Still, as C.L.R. James showed in The Black Jacobins, imaginary \'what if?\' scenarios are foundational to all strategic decision making. To decide how to act, people imagine what would happen if each option was taken. To understand the strategic decision making of the NIEO, then, is to consider the project\'s strategic imaginaries. How did G77 elites imagine a successful NIEO? A full answer to this question is beyond the scope of this article. Here, I consider the strategies of the NIEO more broadly, by focusing on on its intellectual foundations. The Declaration and Programme of Action drew on two distinct, but intertwined, praxes: Kwame Nkrumah\'s critique of neo-colonialism and Raúl Prebisch\'s work on the declining terms of trade. These organic intellectuals agreed on the need for liberal reforms to world order. However, they did so for entirely different reasons. Whereas Nkrumah imagined NIEO-style reforms as useful for global socialism, Prebisch sought a global capitalism that worked as its neo-classical boosters already believed it did. This strategic pluralism allowed the G77 to forge global agreement amongst ideologically diverse state elites. In this sense, the NIEO was a truly Non-Aligned \'common front\'.^1^{#fnref1 .footnote-ref role="doc-noteref"}
