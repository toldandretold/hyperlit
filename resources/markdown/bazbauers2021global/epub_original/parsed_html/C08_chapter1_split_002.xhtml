4.2.1 Key developments at the World Bank

Clinton-appointed President James Wolfensohn oversaw the World Bank’s transition to the post-Washington Consensus and expanded the poverty-reduction mission (see Table 4.2). Under Wolfensohn, the phrase ‘Our dream is a world free of poverty’ was grandiosely carved into the entrance of the Bank’s refurbished headquarters in 1996 (Marshall 2008, 54). The Bank retained poverty reduction as its overarching mission since, even during the presidencies of conservative US Republican Party appointees Paul Wolfowitz and Robert Zoellick. Still, as Cammack (2004, 197) argued, Wolfensohn’s Bank promoted the ‘sustainable reproduction of capitalism’ by ensuring micro-level incentive structures were in line with neoliberal macroeconomic ones. Wolfensohn further attempted to maintain the Bank’s hegemony by shaping developmental thought through programmes like the Knowledge Bank initiative in 1996, designed to make the Bank the leading provider of development expertise, and the World Bank ‘Doing Business’ series starting in 2004, which reported on the ease of doing business in states across the globe and has been very influential (Banerjee et al. 2006). He also renamed structural adjustment loans as development policy loans in 2004 to distance the Bank from the most ideological aspects of the Washington Consensus (Bazbauers 2018). The Bank also helped launch the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) initiative for debt relief in 1996, although debts to the Bank were exempt until the 2005 Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative, to protect its preferred creditor status and credit rating. Despite progress, the conditions on debt relief advocated neoliberal prescriptions like limiting public expenditure and prioritising debt servicing over other needs.
World Bank lending spiked in 1998 and 1999 in response to the Asian Financial Crisis from an annual average of $22.03 billion in 1990–5 to $27.8 billion but rapidly declined to $15.3 billion in 2000. Yet, MDBs need to lend, so they steadily grew again to reach $24.7 billion in 2008, with the GFC then causing a further spike in loans. The relative stability of the 1999–2007 interregnum was not great for the IMF or Bank as their customers were reducing debt or able to borrow with few conditions and at competitive rates from other provides. Indeed, many countries were building foreign reserves explicitly to avoid having to turn to the IMF in the future. Still, the Bank remained vitally important to many smaller and poorer countries whose access to private finance was limited.
Co-financing expanded World Bank lending. This was mostly with official bilateral and multilateral partners. Between 1996 and 1999, co-financing ranged between 33.9% and 40.1% of the value of bank loans. As Bracking (2009) argued, these linkages highlight that there is not just an MDB system, but rather a system of interlinked development finance institutions. Still, an indication that MDF and the MDBs declined in influence over the 2000s was that by 2007 Bank co-financing was equal to just 25.5% of operations. Bank lending to the financial sector increased during this period through all three of its lending arms, particularly the IFC. Leading up to the GFC, the Bank was quite exposed to asset- and mortgage-backed securities, though the IADB was far more exposed (Kellerman 2019).

4.2.2 Key developments in other MDBs

The AsDB took an active response to the Asian Financial Crisis, but some of its IMF-style policies led to backlash. It adopted poverty reduction as its mission in 1999, predating the World Bank. It was partly the result of pressure from the US Treasury, which forced the Asian MDB to put a much greater share of its funds into social and environmental projects than it had in the past (Babb 2009). The AsDB and most of the RDBs adopted and expanded safeguard mechanisms to protect project affected people from harm, which has helped ensure loans benefit local communities, though many problem projects are still funded. The RDBs increased inter-agency harmonisation too and engaged with new financial instruments following the World Bank’s lead.
The post-Washington Consensus era was a solid period for many of the sub-regional and specialised development banks. The smaller MDBs that would become influential during the 2010s began their expansion circa 2000, the CDB and CAF being two examples of this change in fortune, following organisational restructurings implemented during the 1990s. The focus on the private sector expanded in many smaller MDBs, for example, the AFESD’s Board of Governors approved a revision of the MDB’s Articles of Agreement in 1997 to better support the Arab private sector (AFESD 1997).
In Europe, the CEB broadened its mandate during the post-Washington Consensus era, through the 1997 Strasbourg Summit that advocated the CEB incorporate ‘strengthening European social cohesion’ into its mandate and the 2005 Warsaw Summit that called for building a free, democratic, and more inclusive European society; such changes reflected the greater focus on good governance in the post-Washington Consensus period. The EIB became the EIB Group during this period with the incorporation of sibling institutions addressing private sector financing and partnerships, reflecting the expansion of the World Bank into the World Bank Group with the IFC and MIGA.
In Africa, the misfortunes of the EADB continued into the 2000s, not as the result of the economic decline of its members but due to severe mismanagement of finances, leading to several high-ranking officials, including Director General Godfrey Tumusiime, being expelled; it posted losses of millions of dollars. In contrast, BOAD increased its private sector financing, along with a few other smaller MDBs. BOAD originally lent to regional member states but, from 2002, it provided loans to the private sector for development projects of regional significance plus lines of credit to small and medium private enterprises. This can be interpreted as the MDBs following the lead of the World Bank towards more private sector financing products in line with the post-Washington Consensus approach of market-led development.

4.2.3 New MDBs

Just two new MDBs were established between 1998 and 2009 and there was one unsuccessful proposal. The first new MDB, a trade and development bank, was established in 1996 by the Economic Cooperation Organisation (ECO) regional grouping comprised of mainly central Asian states. The ECO Trade and Development Bank (ETDB) was founded by three of the ECO states – Iran, Pakistan, and Turkey with Azerbaijan, Afghanistan, and the Kyrgyz Republic joining later. The ETDB aims simply to expand intra-regional trade and increase economic development. The second new MDB, the Eurasian Development Bank (EDB), was created in 2006 as another post-communist MDB mandated to develop and strengthen market economies, although its member states – the Russian Federation, Kazakhstan, Belarus, Armenia, Tajikistan, and Kyrgyzstan – remain far from model market economies.
The unsuccessful proposal was for a Banco del Sur (or Bank of the South), the idea for which was first raised in 2005 between Venezuela and Brazil. It was an expression of Neostructuralism and an indicator of new activism in the Global South. Its proposers were concerned that the South was still depositing large amounts of resources in Global North financial institutions and they wanted to create a new regional financial architecture (Pérez 2009–10, Rosales 2013). Discussions occurred in 2006–7 between Venezuela and Argentina, and they were joined by Brazil and Ecuador. In 2009, an agreement to establish the MDB was signed with eight initial members. In responding to the demands of the different founding member countries, the functions proposed for the MDB were:
to finance development projects in key sectors of the economy, aimed at improving competitiveness, scientific-technological development, infrastructure, creation and provision of services, intraregional productive complementarity, and the maximization of value added to raw materials produced in the countries of the region.
(cited in Rosales 2013, 31)
Its proposed structure seems rather like a standard MDB: a proposed initial capitalisation of $20 billion, capital raised on bond markets, and weighted voting for large projects and structures matters but one country, one vote for simple operational matters. Its constituent agreement entered into force in 2011 but adequate capital was not paid-in to make it operational. Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro attempted to revise it in April 2015, but it was not capitalised (Mares and Trinkunas 2016). The proposed Banco del Sur was a clear manifestation of Latin American Neostructuralism, and thus part of the broader post-Washington Consensus discourse. It also reopened discussion about the need for public development financing, which the NDB and AIIB continued in the 2010s.

4.3 Retroliberalism and the rise of debt: 2010–20

The progressive changes of the post-Washington Consensus era had come and largely gone by the early 2010s, with the focus turning to the retroliberal – or beyond aid – agenda. The quantity of resources delivered as opposed to the quality of aid effectiveness came to define MDB cooperation documents. The retroliberal agenda first emerged in the early 2000s and began as a slow political reconfiguration away from grant aid. As early as 2002, the International Conference on the Financing of Development in Monterrey made it clear that poor countries were responsible for their own development and that the private sector was expected to be the key course of finance going forward (Saith 2006). And, yet the retroliberal era did not fully emerge until around 2010, catalysed by the upheaval of the GFC.
Ideationally, it was hoped that the 2007–8 GFC would contribute to a broadening of development discourse. And, for a moment in the wake of the crisis, there was a brief flirtation with unorthodox policies, in particular Keynesian stimulus spending and financial controls in order to bail out capitalists but this was followed by a rapid return to austerity politics (Ortiz et al. 2020). The minor changes to regulations such as reforms to the Basel banking rules did not address the systemic risk that had been created by the liberalisation of financial flows, and financialisation revived after the crisis (Gabor 2020, Goldin et al. 2014). Mainstream donors and the MDBs maintained a broadly post-Washington Consensus mindset, but now with an increase emphasis on infrastructure, nationalism, and financialisation – promoting private investment and loans over grant aid. Thus, following Murray and Overton (2016), we label the period from 2010 to 2020 as the retroliberal era.
The term retroliberalism underscores that the policy response to the GFC benefited capital by socialising its losses and leaving labour income to pay for this through public debt. The practice of neoliberalism has always differed from the theory, with centre left and right governments adapting to serve business interests. In the current era, this means a return to a more state-centric and nationalist discourse focussing on the benefits that development aid and finance can provide to private and state firms rather than to poverty reduction. Retroliberalism has also marginalised the SDGs; they may get lip service, but they are no longer at the centre of development thinking. Significantly, the dramatic rise of China and its development strategy has put infrastructure again at the centre of development finance, a resurgence that broadly aligns with the modernisation mentality the World Bank pursued over its first two decades.
China’s approach to rapid industrialisation differs radically from the minimalist state agenda of the Washington Consensus and China’s influence on development has grown – by 2019, they were the third largest World Bank shareholder and were central to the formation of the NDB and AIIB. This has led scholars like Jakupec and Kelly (2019) and Lin and Wang (2017) to argue that China has created space for the Global South to adopt developmentalist approaches. However, like Yağcı (2016, 43), we argue that while China’s experience may be informative for debates about developmentalism, China itself is not concerned with ‘enlarging space for developing countries’ trade relations and industrialisation efforts.’ Rather, the contemporary inegalitarian capital order provides the foundations for China’s development, which they support along with the associated global economic governance system (Bond 2015, Robinson 2015).
The expansion of China’s mutual aid activities since 2008, notably the Belt and Road Initiative of 2013, was largely in response to domestic stagnation. Its aid promotes retroliberal policies – supporting open markets and broadly neoliberal fiscal and monetary policy, but with increased investment in infrastructure and less interference in state affairs including around human rights. Further, China’s policies support their investors, whether they are seeking to source raw materials, outsource elements of low-wage manufacturing, or gain construction contracts outside China using Chinese inputs and labour. Indeed, it is not unwarranted to say that they are often ‘kicking away the ladder for developing countries’ (Chang 2002). A key issue here is China’s extensive use of debt instruments, which previously had been very cautious about. During the 2010s, however, China’s approach became a retroliberal one – largely neoliberal policies with a good dash of infrastructure spending, all wrapped up in debt.
China’s expansion of debt relations has led the MDBs to expand their loan portfolios and the Global North to expand existing, and create new, bilateral development finance mechanisms, such as the US International Development Finance Corporation or Australia’s Infrastructure Financing Facility for the Pacific. Private sector debt has rapidly grown too over the past decade, and in 2018 global debt reached a record high of 230% of GDP. Since 2010, debt in EMDEs ‘has risen 54 percentage points of GDP to a historic peak of about 170% of GDP in 2018’ (Kose et al. 2020, xix). China itself has also rapidly expanded debt, but they have the advantage of most of their being owed to domestic sources.
Key contributors to the 2010s debt wave are the usual culprits of low interest rates and financialisation, as well as ‘a rise of regional banks, growing appetite for local currency bonds, and increased demand for EMDE debt from the expanding non-bank financial sector’ (Kose et al. 2020, xxi). The expansion in EMDE debt between 2010 and 2014 was predominantly private sector debt, but from 2014 to 2018 that reversed with publicly guaranteed debt making up the majority. In low-income countries, both public and private debt has grown and there has been a shift away from concessional finance and towards private creditors, although smaller sub-Saharan African states are still primarily reliant on official creditors (Bonizzi, Laskaridis, and Toporowski 2019). According to Kose et al. (2020, 10), this ‘wave of debt buildup has been unprecedented in its size, speed, and reach’ in EMDEs. It is also more global than previous debt build-up waves occurring since the 1970s, though debt in the Global North has been stable and there have been declines in debt in the Middle East, Eastern Europe, and Central Asia.

4.3.1 Key developments at the World Bank

World Bank President Jim Yong Kim, who led the MDB from 2012 to 2019, with his public health background, seemed to offer a progressive approach to development (see Table 4.3). By the time Kim left the Bank, however, the MDB had doubled down on financialisation. At the same time, networks between the MDBs and DFIs continued to grow. The International Development Finance Club was established by the IMF and Bank in 2011 to help DFIs set agendas on development finance and share know-how. It has six SRDBs and the IsDB as members of its private sector arm, along with Global North and South national development banks and one Exim bank.

World Bank commitments reached an all-time high in 2010 of $58.7 billion, and then dropped substantially to $29 billion in 2012. Yet, this lending interregnum was short. As the investment climate deteriorated mid-decade, states increased public spending, and President Kim announced his ‘billions to trillions’ agenda in 2013 that aimed to leverage Bank lending to shift from billions of dollars in investments to trillions. Between 2017 and 2019, average annual Bank commitments were $44.8 billion and IBRD gross disbursements were $18.5 billion and IDA gross disbursements totalled $14.9 billion (World Bank 2019).
The ‘billions to trillions’ agenda was followed by Maximising Finance for Development (MFD) in 2017, which sought to leverage official development finance to attract private investment but with a focus on ‘innovative’ financial instruments. The World Bank’s 2018 large shareholding increase (detailed in Chapter 5) was core to this, though it was also likely partly driven by competition with the new MDBs and Chinese development finance. The increase substantially expanded Bank Group lending capacity and is linked to the IDA raising funds on money markets for the first time in its history. With the Trump regime in power in the US, it seems that the shareholder increase came at the price of Kim’s resignation.
The MFD initiatives shows that, despite the havoc wreaked by financialisation in the GFC, the World Bank has become a proponent of it being extended to aid and development finance; the ‘overall strategy is to policy-engineer a shift towards securities-based financial systems and thus render DECs [developing and emerging countries] more amenable to the forces of financialised capitalism’ (Gabor 2018, 16). While the overall strategy is clear, the precise methods for implementation are less so. One method is issuing new kinds of bonds, such as green bonds, IDA bonds, the world’s first blockchain bond, and Indonesian women’s empowerment bonds (World Bank 2019). A second method is the ‘cascade approach’ that identifies imperfections that may be stopping private capital from investing in projects. If addressing the imperfection is not enough, subsidies and guarantees may be needed to leverage private investment and only when such reforms are still not attracting private investment can public investment be considered (Gabor 2018). It thus combines an ideological preference for private investment over public regardless of profitability or efficiency, with a preference for financialised capitalism over traditional banking. For example, the Bank issued catastrophe bonds in 2016 through the Pandemic Emergency Financing Facility (PEFF), a mechanism pioneered by insurance companies to pay high interest rates on the basis that investors may lose some of or all their money. The Bank’s PEFF bond had a complex set of triggers before they could be paid out, leading to an expectation that they would not be. But, the COVID-19 crisis met all the triggers and so investors lost their principal (Mackenzie 2020). The Bank created PEFF bonds to build a new market in securitised risk but given most investors are loss adverse, they will be less likely to purchase them again in the future (Mackenzie 2020). More to the point, such innovative financial instruments may have a strong structure, ‘but from almost any angle, they look fundamentally ill-suited to protecting vulnerable people from diseases, disaster, or climate change’ (Mackenzie 2020).
With Trump appointee David Malpass as the incumbent president (see Table 4.3), the Bank became more concerned with the growth of debt, which the IMF had been warning about for a few years. Yet, the COVID-19 pandemic has left no option but for a big increase in lending as bilateral donors are not increasing grant resources. The Bank has said they will provide $160 billion in frontloaded funds to help states respond to the crisis and other MDBs will private $80 billion. They are also frontloading grants through their COVID-19 Fast Track Facility, but more loans will follow.
In 2020, the Bank Group’s ambitious mission was to end extreme poverty by decreasing the percentage of people living on less than $1.90 a day to no more than 3% by 2030 and to promote shared prosperity by fostering the income growth of the bottom 40% of people in every country. But, just like the MDGs, this formulation is an accounting illusion that narrowly focusses on inequality by promoting income growth. This brings us back to the centrality of the Bank’s original purposes, which can be summarised as facilitating investment in productive purposes and promoting private investment and trade.

4.3.2 Key developments in other MDBs

The late 2000s and 2010s saw an across-the-board improvement in the organisational functioning and loan portfolios of the regional, sub-regional, and specialised development banks, particularly among a few smaller MDBs that had struggled during the 1980s and 1990s. Such improvements coincided with organisational restructurings, an expansion of capital availability, and new leadership. In the case of the RDBs, however, most of which had also lent counter-cyclically during the 2007–8 GFC, they had a larger decline in lending in the early 2010s than the World Bank, which has been attributed to downward pressure from credit rating agencies (Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli 2018).
In the case of the SRDBs, successful reform occurred across several continents. In Africa, the EADB went from severe financial mismanagement during the 2000s to significant organisational restructuring and the recovery of outstanding loans under Director General Vivienne Yeda, who was appointed in 2009. As a result, Yeda received several financial and banking awards, including the African Banker of the Year Award in 2014. At the TDB, President Adamassu Tadesse, who received the same African Banker Award in 2019, spearheaded a dramatic reformation of the African SRDB, including growing equity capital and annual profits four-fold between 2012 and 2019, attracting 15 new shareholders, and receiving the TDB’s first investment grade rating from Moody’s in 2017 (Africa Fintech Summit 2019). The TDB’s reform process began in 2000 and the overall result is that it is now substantially larger than the four African SRDBs that were created before it (Humphrey 2019). In Latin America, CAF came to rival the IADB and exceed World Bank Latin American and Caribbean commitments during the 2010s, which is indicative of the change in fortunes and expansion of operations of so many MDBs (Humphrey 2014, 2016).
In Europe, the NDF, after almost being dissolved in the 2000s, instituted a series of institutional reforms in 2009, shifting its mandate from soft loans for social and economic development to grants for climate change interventions in low-income countries (Amland 2018, Normann 2019). This was an attempt to not only satisfy the competing interests of its Nordic shareholders but also to better find its niche within MDF (Wihtol 2013). The CEB, its relevance revitalised in the 1990s with the collapse of the Soviet Union and reintegration of people from the former Soviet bloc into capitalist Europe, renewed its pertinence once again in response to the refugee crisis affecting Europe since the mid-2010s.
In Eastern Europe and Central Asia, the IIB underwent considerable restructuring in 2012. Following two decades of stagnation in the wake of the Soviet Union’s collapse, new management entered the former Soviet-led MDB to make it a modern-day development bank. A revised agreement in 2014 saw it shift to a much more capitalist agenda of ‘promoting economic growth, increasing the competitiveness of national economies, expanding trade and economic ties, and maximising investment interaction opportunities’ (IIB 2018, 1). The IIB also entered debt capital markets in 2014, the first time in its history, and began issuing bonds in Russia, Slovakia, and Romania.
In a range of ways, the 2010s saw a closer alignment between the MDBs as to what a development bank ‘should’ look like. In addition to broad harmonisation efforts, another example is the increasingly close alignment on climate finance following the 2015 Paris Agreement (see Chapter 10) and further many MDBs followed the World Bank’s lead in increasing their financial innovation engagement.

4.3.3 New MDBs

Two new MDBs emerged during the retroliberal era, the NDB and AIIB, and they were designed as traditional development banks. The NDB (2015, 1) adopts neutral language in its purposes, aiming to ‘mobilize resources for infrastructure and sustainable development projects in BRICS and other emerging economies and developing countries,’ and the AIIB (2015, 2), largely duplicating the AsDB’s Charter, has a strong profit focus prioritising ‘infrastructure and other productive sectors,’ despite being founded by an ostensibly socialist state. The absence of references to poverty reduction in the NDB and AIIB’s founding documents speaks to them being business-link banks, at odds with the SDGs; the new MDBs conform to the ‘GDP-centred, Northern-development-model approach’ (Dossani 2014).
Promoting private capital is remarkably prominent in the AIIB’s Articles of Agreement, giving the MDB responsibility for its promotion in the region. The Articles suggest that, as argued above, the practice of this China-led development financier is not much friendly to the state than the old traditional MDBs. The NDB is a little more conspicuous in its purposes, referring to ‘support’ for public and private projects. The similarities of purposes and inclusion of facilitating private investment demonstrates that the new MDBs aim to promote debt-based, capitalist expansion in the same way as the old ones have.

4.4 Conclusion

There are so many dimensions to the MDBs discussed in Chapters 3 and 4 that it is hard to offer simple conclusions. The chapters highlight the connections between MDB creation, geopolitics, economic theory, and debt. They demonstrate that there is a fair degree of coherence around the aims and objectives of the MDBs; almost all exhibit a Keynesian influence, which is not surprising given their logic is the Keynesian idea that markets will not always ensure adequate levels of investment. Some are more statist, others more pragmatic, and the EBRD brought in a neoliberal tone, but, in the end, they are all focussed on promoting market-based economic growth in line with banking principles. There is a remarkable consensus around the need for more and more debt to promote economic growth, despite there still being no orderly default and restructuring mechanism for states at the international level.
Many of the MDBs started out with strong regional missions but few have been able to live up to them in practice; not many governments want to be burdened by joint loans that they may not get an equal share of benefits from. This demonstrates the dominance of the state in development discourse and it permeates the analysis and lending strategies of the MDBs too. The MDBs have helped naturalise the idea that states are actual and effective units that can control development challenges at the same time as their banking and neoliberal logics lead them to want to constrain the state and promote the benefits of markets. This is the tension at the heart of the MDBs.
The durability of the MDB model and the MDBs themselves are further factors that leap out of the analysis – ‘once established MDBs do not disappear, but rather transform and evolve’ (Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli 2018, 21). The BDEGL is the only suspended MDB we have found and even it still exists on paper. Still, as the chapter demonstrates, many MDBs have faced challenges. Nonetheless, they are substantial financial actors. The 23 MDBs studied by Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli (2018, 49) had a total portfolio (loans, equity investments, and debt securities) of $1.13 trillion in 2016 or 1.6% of world GDP, which was a decline from earlier in the decade. In addition, states have turned to the idea of creating new MDBs time and again when faced with obstacles, which suggests they consider productive investment of the trillions of dollars that now float around the world is an ongoing challenge. States seem to like the influence that MDBs provide and, as Chapter 5 shows, once established MDBs are quite cheap for them. The next chapter focusses on the structure of the MDBs and it emphasises how their structure as banks has been a long-underestimated influence. Indeed, Kapur, Lewis, and Webb (1997, 1111) note that at times the World Bank’s ‘financial and development personas’ have been ‘more competitive than complementary.’

5 The structure of multilateral development banks

This chapter analyses the structure of the MDBs, positioning the World Bank as the archetype and exploring how the other MDBs are similar or different. The chapter starts with voting systems and capital subscriptions and how these have shaped Bank operations. Next, the Bank’s governance structure is outlined, analysing the relative influence of its Governors, Directors, and senior management. The chapter then turns to the Bank’s lending system – types of loans, conditionality, procurement, and interest rates. Table 5.2 systematically compares the Bank’s structures to those of other MDBs, highlighting organisational convergences and divergences.

5.1 The structure of the World Bank

The MDBs are all structured like private banks, following the model established by the IBRD. They have shareholders, predominantly states, contributing capital in return for shares and voting rights. An analysis of vote allocations, capitalisation, governance structures, and lending capacity may seem quite technical, but it is critical to understand the MDBs as organisations to comprehend their strengths and limitations and thus their role in development finance.

5.1.1 Voting rights

The negotiators at the 1944 Bretton Woods conference did not spend much time debating the IBRD’s organisational structure – Article V of the Bank’s establishing agreement is ‘nearly an exact copy of the [IMF’s] Articles XII and XIII’ (Mason and Asher 1973, 29). Article V outlines the Bank’s organisation and management, including its governance structure, system of voting rights, and location of offices. The voting rights were the ‘realist’ heart at the centre of post-war international economic organisation building at Bretton Woods. Closely following Harry Dexter White’s 1942 draft (Oliver 1971), IBRD voting rights were linked to the number of shares held – each member had 250 basic votes plus one additional vote for each share of stock held. The size of each country’s capital subscription was originally based on relative economy size and later subscriptions to IDA were added to encourage donations to the Bank’s concessional arm. Further, there is little incentive for developing countries to have large subscriptions because they are independent of borrowing rights; this is in contrast to the IMF, where subscriptions are linked to drawing rights (Mason and Asher 1973). This means that many borrowing members have little influence over the Bank, an issue identified as a severe democratic deficit in the MDB.
Basic votes are a compromise between equal representation and power-based voting, and some MDBs have more basic votes per member than others. In the IBRD’s case, capital increases were not matched by an expansion of basic votes, so their value has significantly declined (Leech and Leech 2006). Basic votes were later capped at a low 5.55% of total votes but generally have been below that. In some of the other MDBs, basic votes were set as a percentage of total votes from the outset. For example, basic votes are 20% of total votes in the AsDB, while they are 12% in the AIIB, with founding AIIB members getting an additional 600 votes giving them greater weight, though the value of these will decline as the capital stock increases. Several MDBs also have different formulas for regional and non-regional members, generally with the aim of increasing the power of developing regional member countries.
The largest shareholders in the IBRD – those countries that provide the main guarantee behind the Bank’s bond issues and loans on capital markets – were originally those from the Global North, namely the US and Western European countries. With the refusal of the Soviet Union to ratify the Bretton Woods agreement, the US had 35.07% of votes in 1946 and the US and European countries combined controlled 74% (Mason and Asher 1973, 800–02). Even with an influx of new members in the 1960s, the US and Europeans still held 61% in June 1971. Japan pushed to increase its shareholding in the early 1970s (Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997). After the creation of the IDA add-on in 1960, adjustments to Bank shareholding reflected not just the member’s economic weight but also contributions to the Bank’s concessional arm. In practice, however, political factors have always weighed in too.
China and other emerging economies began campaigns in the 1990s to gain more influence in the Bretton Woods institutions and other international organisations, to better reflect geopolitical changes. The Bank undertook what was described as major ‘voice’ reforms after a call from the UN International Conference on Financing for Development held in Monterrey in 2002. The revised quota framework was said to be 75% based on the size of state’s GDP, IDA contributions were given 20% weighting, and 5% weighting was given to country’s borrowings. The formulas were never formally published however, and countries did not have to agree to reductions, so the basis of decision-making remains unclear (Vestergaard 2010). Shareholding changes introduced in 2008 and 2010 produced only a 4.5% transfer from the Global North to developing and transition countries and much less for low-income countries (Vestergaard 2010)! By 2020, the top six shareholders in the IBRD were the US, whose 15.44% shareholding grants it veto power over certain key decisions, followed by Japan (7.75%), China (4.78%), Germany (4.08%), France (3.8%), and the UK (3.8%). Resolving over- and under-representation is difficult for the Bank and the rapid growth of emerging economies exacerbates this. Nevertheless, it can be said that: (i) high-income countries as a group remain over-represented; (ii) China is significantly under-represented, as is India; and (iii) low-income countries are over-represented relative to their GDP but still have just a tiny fraction of voting power (Vestergaard 2010).
The IBRD’s governing structure is made up of a Board of Governors, with representatives appointed from each member country, a Board of Directors, and a President nominally elected by the Executive Directors. Almost all MDBs follow this structure, or a close variant thereof, e.g., the EADB has a Director General in lieu of a President, although the role is analogous. Governors are generally appointed by ministries of finance, though sometimes by Treasury or state departments or central banks – they rarely come from national aid agencies. The World Bank’s Board of Governors is the supreme decision-making body, but its formal responsibilities are limited to admitting and suspending members, adjudicating appeals from the Directors on interpretations of the Bank’s Articles, and increasing and decreasing the capital stock. Most formal power including setting strategic directions and approving lending operations is vested in the Directors, but in practice the President and senior management have substantial control. Directors are elected for two-year terms and are paid by the Bank, not member governments. There were originally 12 Directors, but this grew to 24 in 1992 following an expansion of country membership, and in 2010 an additional Director for sub-Saharan Africa was added, bringing the total to 25. Larger boards can lead to better representation but can also complicate decision-making and coordination (Babb 2009).
The largest shareholders get to appoint Directors. For many decades, this meant the US, the UK, Germany, France, and Japan, but today also includes China. Other countries generally pool their votes in relatively stable constituencies, with directorships usually held by the largest constituency members. These elections do not precisely follow the IBRD’s Articles (Strand and Retzl 2016). Directors cast the number of votes that they control or have obtained at their election and voting for all IBRD decisions is 50% of votes cast, with a few key exceptions. The first is that capital increases require 75% of voting power, but the specification that members have the right to an equal subscription when there is a capital increase means that no country can have its shareholdings reduced without agreeing, so capital increases effectively require unanimous approval (Vestergaard 2010). Second, amending the Articles of Agreement requires three-fifths of members and 85% of voting power, increased from 80% in 1989, allowing the US to retain its veto. Finally, unanimous agreement is required to modify clauses on the right to withdraw from the IBRD, rights to subscribe to increases in the authorised capital stock, and limitations on liability.
Earlier Executive Director constituencies were sometimes based on historical or colonial relationships and there were often groups with a member from the Global North who dominated. Australia is an interesting example, as it formed a group in 1953 with the Union of South Africa, Vietnam joined in 1957, and New Zealand in 1965. By 1971, the group was without Vietnam. Then, in 2003, Australia was paired with New Zealand, Cambodia, the Republic of Korea, Mongolia, Palau, and seven Pacific Island nations. In 2020, the group was almost the same, except the seven Pacific states had expanded to ten and the group’s 3.45% voting power expanded to 3.99%. Contra the Australian example, most groups rarely change composition (Strand and Retzl 2016). The Russian Federation has a Director elected based on their shareholding, though Syria joined their constituency. Equally, Saudi Arabia is a single-member constituency. These two single-seat groups ‘mean that the remaining voting groups are larger than they otherwise would be, thus potentially reducing the formal voice of members represented by elected’ Directors (Strand and Retzl 2016, 420). Most other Directors are elected based on geographical groupings, though there are mixed constituencies, including the Philippines with several Latin American states and Switzerland with Eastern European and Central Asian states. Sub-Saharan Africa is represented by three groups, one with Angola, Nigeria, and South Africa and two others with over 20 states in each, which remain by far the largest groupings despite reforms enacted to address inequitable representation (Strand and Retzl 2016, 429). The Board is the same for the IBRD, IDA, and IFC though voting power differs, for example, the US holds only 10.06% shareholding in the IDA.
Almost all other MDBs operate along similar structures, with voting power linked to shareholding and allocated relative to their economic size. They can have higher or lower percentages of basic votes and some grant regional membership voting privileges over non-regional ones. The newly created NDB is the only MDB with equal voting power in 2020, but this may change when and if other countries are admitted. Further, some MDBs have financial institutions as shareholders in addition to countries. For example, the BOAD has eight regional member countries, the Central Bank of West African States, the AfDB and the EIB, three non-regional member states, and three financial institutions acting on behalf of Germany, India, and China. The admission of such eclectic members enabled BOAD to raise funds on bond markets, which it was previously unable to do due to capital and credit rating limitations of its regional member countries.
The shareholding system reflects a corporate governance model and is subject to similar vagaries with many MDBs having a dominant shareholder or group of shareholders, which creates problems around hegemonic power. Voting is usually pursued by consensus, but this often means appointed Directors set outcomes for their constituencies (Leech and Leech 2006). As Strand and Retzl (2016, 419) note, this is an expression of the power of major shareholders and weighted voting casts ‘long shadows over negotiations.’ There are no rules about whose interests elected Directors represent, which becomes particularly tricky in large constituencies (Leech and Leech 2006, Woods 2006), and quality control can be problematic in the absence of an independent evaluation office that reports to the Board (Ben-Artzi 2016).

5.1.2 Capital subscriptions

As shareholder banks, four factors shape MDB lending capacity: first, their size or total capitalisation; second, the amount of capital that is paid-in and the amount callable; third, the creditworthiness – or perceptions thereof – of the largest shareholders; and, fourth, their debt-to-lending ratios. When first established, the IBRD held $10 billion capital stock, which was not enough to meet Europe’s post-war reconstruction needs; hence, it was quickly superseded by the Marshall Plan. The cash-strapped British successfully argued to reduce the amount of paid-in capital from the 50% of total capital that White proposed to 20% (Gardner 1980, Mason and Asher 1973). Of the paid-in capital, only 2% had to be paid in US dollars or gold with the remainder in the member’s currency. This meant in the early years, when the US dollar was the only convertible currency, the Bank’s loanable funds were limited to contributions in dollars or gold (Mason and Asher 1973). As Engel (2010, 29) argued, the ‘small initial capital stock and even smaller paid-in component made the Bank reliant on financial markets for capital, which profoundly shaped its subsequent operations.’ Add to this a conservative debt-to-lending ratio adopted by IBRD management and clearly the Bank established a cautious business model that prioritised banking principles (Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997). This model was emulated in the regional, sub-regional, and specialised development banks, many of which function more as banks than development agencies (Ben-Artzi 2016), with their loan policies, financial management, and country risk assessment designed to ensure bond market approvals (Humphrey 2016).
For the IBRD in its early years, support from financial markets meant gaining allies on Wall Street (Caufield 1998). The preference for bankers as IBRD President (see Chapters 3 and 4) and in other key management roles was meant to gain investor trust. The corollary was that very few staff had development backgrounds (Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997). Still, in the post-war years, caution around international bonds persisted, thus raising capital was difficult for the Bank. When established in the mid-2010s, the NDB and AIIB followed the pattern of the early MDBs, with senior staff often coming across from finance sector firms like Goldman Sachs (Engel and Bazbauers 2018).
Credit rating agencies (CRAs) were a major influence on the MDBs from the outset because the ranking of MDB bonds were a major factor in their saleability. There are four key elements to CRA ratings of MDBs (Ben-Artzi 2016, Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli 2018, Humphrey 2014, Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997). First, they examine if the MDB has preferred creditor status. Second, CRAs assess the soundness of the countries and projects that MDBs lend to and for. In terms of the countries lent to, this encourages the MDBs to have a substantial share of their loan portfolio with credit-worthy middle-income countries. The World Bank has come under criticism for its lending to high middle-income countries, particularly its lending to China (see Behar 2012). In terms of the soundness of individual loans, this led the IBRD to prioritise project lending in its early years, despite a mandate to finance programme lending (Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997). As early Bank staffer Victor Urquidi (1996, 48) said, the ‘Bank’s “bottom line” in judging a project was the “times earned column” on a spreadsheet.’ However, it has always been perceptions of project quality rather than reality that matters because of the substantial gap between when projects are approved and when they are completed and their outcomes are evaluated (Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997). The third way CRAs judge MDBs is the stability of their major shareholders. In other words: do they have rich industrialised member states? This is because they are the ones that would realistically be able to contribute callable capital should an MDB ever need it. The consequence is that MDBs with fewer wealthy member states have reduced access to financial markets. The AfDB, for example, admitted non-African shareholders to improve its capital base (English and Mule 1996). Fourth, CRAs use a range of financial ratios to determine MDB risk ratings, particularly equity-to-loan ratios to determine capital adequacy. Almost all MDBs have capital adequacy well above those of commercial banks and there are not many reasons for this, apart from protecting wealthy members from capital call-ins (Humphrey 2014). The IBRD gained its AAA credit rating from the major US CRAs in 1959 and kept it ever since. After the 2007–8 GFC, CRAs enacted new evaluation methodologies equating the MDBs to commercial banks, even though they are effectively non-profit organisations. Consequently, Humphrey (2017, 283) argues, ‘to retain their AAA bond rating, major MDBs are (i) restricting their overall capacity to make use of their balance sheet for development projects and (ii) limiting lending to some borrowing countries facing economic difficulties.’ This impacts the poorest countries that already have difficulty accessing credit the most because the MDBs are powerful signallers to private finance. Such limits could prove problematic when the next financial crisis arrives, particularly as grant finance was hollowed out over the 2010s. Former MDB staffers note how the CRAs are ‘strategy makers and even regulators’ despite having ‘very little depth of comprehension’ about MDBs (Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli 2018, 58). CRAs regularly put demands on the MDBs, including reducing their portfolio risk and sector and country diversification. As a result of their inordinate impact, the BRICS started talks in 2015 about establishing alternatives to the US-based CRAs, but with little success. The reliance on international banking and money markets has thus created a system that priorities banking norms over development ideals.
The World Bank has increased its lending capacity in two ways over the years: first, by increasing its capital reserves and, second, by increasing its capital base. The first general capital increase (GCI) was 14 years after the Bank was established in 1959, which doubled its capitalisation; it was to ensure that increased lending did not result in too great a deterioration of its equity-to-loan ratio and was strongly supported by the US. Other shareholders have also bargained intensely to increase their capital share, as Japan did in the 1980s in the pursuit of becoming the second largest IBRD shareholder (Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997).
Changes to relative IBRD shareholdings are done through Selective Capital Increases (SCIs). These generally follow IMF quota reviews and see countries offered additional shares to reflect their changed international positions, although political factors are always in the mix. This was the case in 1976 when OPEC members, whose economic influence increased after the 1973 oil shock, were offered larger shareholdings than their economies warranted as then President McNamara saw them as a potential source of new funds (Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997). The second oil shock of 1979 reduced US resistance to a new GCI, which was agreed at $40 billion. Following US resistance to capital increases during the mid-1980s, there was a large $78.9 billion GCI in 1988 that shored up the Bank’s equity-to-loan ratio for quite some time. An influx of new members including Switzerland and former Soviet states led to further capital increases so that by 1995 the IBRD’s capital was $184 billion.
It took over two decades for the next GCI because, after the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, there was a long period of relative global economic growth and stability with private financial flows on the rise. The GFC brought that to an end, and led to a $58 billion GCI under President Zoellick. This 2010 GCI was dependent on World Bank governance reforms, as were the large 2018 GCI and SCI President Kim oversaw shortly before stepping down. The 2018 capital increase was for both the IBRD’s paid-in ($7.5 billion) and callable ($52.6 billion) capital, with paid-in increases always harder to obtain. The Trump administration was critical of the Bank’s ongoing lending to China and, in return for supporting the capital increase, obtained commitments to operational reforms and loan pricing changes designed to reduce loans to China, but resulted in higher interest rate charges to all wealthier IBRD borrowers (Mayeda and Shankleman 2018).
Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli (2018, 38–9) examined 25 MDBs calculating their subscribed capital increased by an average of 5.7% per annum between 2007 and 2016 reaching $1.4 trillion, which in 2016 was equal to 1.8% of world GDP. Further, almost all MDBs retain earnings and this on average equalled 30.7% of total subscribed capital by 2016 (Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli 2018, 41). The very low costs of gaining influence by creating an MDB is likely, we think, a key reason that there are now 30 MDBs. With all the discussion about the recent establishment of the AIIB and NDB, this factor was overlooked.

5.1.3 Governance

The IBRD’s Articles of Agreement state that the Executive Directors select the Bank President. In practice, however, an informal agreement between the US and European countries means that the US proposes a presidential candidate for the Directors to approve and the Europeans propose the IMF Managing Director. A major campaign by several states, NGOs, and others started in the early 2000s pushed for a more merit-based selection process, this was epitomised by the 2012 Open Letter to all World Bank Governors signed by 59 leading development NGOs and published by the Bretton Woods Project, an NGO which critically monitors World Bank and IMF activities. But it remains the case that ‘at the end of the day he’ – and Bank Presidents have only even been men – ‘is only accountable to the President of the United States and the US Congress’ (Vestergaard 2010, 11).
All World Bank Presidents have been US citizens, though two were born overseas: James Wolfensohn in Australia and Jim Yong Kim in South Korea. The US has tended to select appointees without development backgrounds and, up to Wolfensohn, the majority were bankers, demonstrating again that it is a bank as much as, if not more so than, a development agency. Since Wolfensohn, Republican administrations have adopted a more partisan approach, with the three Republican appointees – Paul Wolfowitz, Robert Zoellick, and David Malpass – all having previously served as Republican political appointees. The last two Democratic appointees did not, however; Wolfensohn was a banker and Kim a public health specialist. This suggests that Republicans have taken a far more politicised approach to the Bank than Democrats. Still, it is not uncommon for MDB presidents to come from the political world and strong political ties may help in negotiating capital increases and replenishments (Ben-Artzi 2016).
The World Bank President guides day-to-day decisions, ‘under the direction of the Executive Directors’ (World Bank 2012). Bank Presidents are a key influence in shaping its strategic direction, as was shown in Chapters 3 and 4; they ‘were not only the source of power in the institution; they were also the ones the staff looked to for signals that would indicate the Bank’s primary mission and its operational priorities in a changing world’ (Kraske 1996, vii). Still, as with all MDBs, there are tensions between the Boards of Governors and Directors and senior management and staff, which can arise from different priorities around politics, development, and bank finances and who prioritises what can change over time (Ben-Artzi 2016).
The location of an MDB’s headquarters is political too. In the case of the IMF and World Bank, they were to ‘be located in the territory of the member holding the greatest number of shares’ (World Bank 2012). The US chose to headquarter the IBRD in Washington, DC, so it would be under close governmental influence. Subsequent MDBs mostly show a similar pattern of politicking, with head office locations being a decision by major shareholders or a backlash against them. As examples, the IADB is in Washington DC, while the AsDB is in Manila, the Philippines, to reduce member concerns about Japanese influence.
Debates about reform to World Bank governance have arisen at least once a decade both within and outside the MDB, leading to a never-ending cycle of restructuring. In terms of reform, many commentators have made the important point that MDB governance should be as sound as the MDBs expect the governance of their borrowers to be. Some outside the organisation call for reform (see inter alia Ahluwalia et al. 2016, Stiglitz 2006, Woods 2006), while others call for a more radical rethinking of international economic governance (Amin 2014, Bello, Kinley, and Elinson 1982, Cammack 2002, George and Sabelli 1994). And, yet, at the end of the day, despite regular restructuring, the Bank’s governance as delineated by its Articles of Agreement remains largely unchanged since the 1940s, a template emulated by almost all other MDBs and clearly evident in both the NDB and AIIB.

5.1.4 Loans

The World Bank offers three main loan types: investment project finance, development policy loans (DPLs), and programme-for-results lending. Investment project finance for physical and social infrastructure is the Bank’s staple lending instrument and can be approved as an IBRD loan, IDA credit, or as a guarantee. Investments can be in any sector and can only be financed by the Bank or co-financed with other development agencies either as loans or grants. DPLs provide budget support to governments based on commitment to a set of policy or institutional reforms. These originated from what was called non-project and programme lending, which comprised an average of 6% of Bank lending up until the late 1970s (Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997, 487). In the 1980s, the Bank launched structural adjustment loans (SALs) and sectoral adjustment loans (SECALs), which were conditional on macroeconomic changes. Adjustment lending peaked at around 30% of loans in the 1980s then declined in the 1990s. DPLs replaced SALs and SECALs in 2004 and made up roughly one-quarter of financing between 2005 and 2015, largely due to a spike in overall lending and emergency responses to the GFC (World Bank 2020). This is the most controversial lending instrument as it involves the Bank interfering in a country’s macroeconomic policies. Compared to the coercive reputation of SALs and SECALs, DPLs are meant to be borrower-owned and -led, but the degree to which this is the case is questionable. Programme-for-results lending, previously known as programme lending, focusses investment on specific sectors. Funding is disbursed when pre-agreed results are achieved. This third instrument is focussed on lending to ‘high-performing’ countries. In addition, the Bank offers tailored loan programmes, but the three above loan types receive the bulk of annual approvals. The Bank also has several trust funds and grant facilities with different donors and these finances are often allocated along with a Bank loan. To develop loans, the Bank offers extensive technical assistance services (see Bazbauers 2018 for a detailed history).
Harry Dexter White’s first IBRD draft went into detail about lending operations (Oliver 1971), which likely reflected his concern to ensure the exploitative lending of the inter-war years was not repeated. Further, White judged that the below-market interest rates on offer would produce a good demand for loans, which has not in fact generally been the case for the IBRD. The IDA, however, tends to be oversubscribed because of its highly concessional terms. Many of White’s ideas are inscribed in the Bank’s Articles of Agreement and into those of subsequent MDBs. Loan conditions are specified in Article III, Section 4 and they start with the requirement that states guarantee loan repayments made in their territory regardless of who it is to. Next, to make sure Bank funds did not compete with private banks, the IBRD had to be satisfied that the loan could not be secured from the market. Section 4 continues in outlining that loans and guarantees, except in special circumstances, must ‘be for the purpose of specific projects of reconstruction and development’ (World Bank 2012). Further, interest rates must be ‘reasonable’ and, to ensure the productive use of loans, a ‘competent committee’ must study each proposal and complete a written report on it (Mason and Asher 1973). Debt was also a major concern of post-war planners, so the IBRD was tasked with making prudent assessments of the borrower’s capacity to repay. And, so, the aim was to create a cautious lending programme based on market ideals and framed by (Western) experts (Engel 2010).
Article 4, Point 10 of the IBRD’s establishing agreement on political non-interference gives ‘complete discretion’ for terms and conditions, meaning that an action is non-political if the Bank says it is (Mason and Asher 1973, 27). The artificial distinction between politics and economics in Point 10 has allowed the Bank to promote clearly political economic policies and prescriptions. Even loan conditions have been political, given that they grew with the rise of neoliberal SALs and SECALs in the 1980s and then declined in part because of their lack of efficacy.
Conditionality is the product of a three-way interaction between the Bank, shareholders, and borrowers, with more powerful borrowing countries being less subject to conditionality; influential shareholders only come into play if they have particular support for, or concerns about, loans to a borrower (Hernandez 2017). The availability of finance from other sources – the private sector and other donors – is also a factor shaping the level and impact of conditionality. Focussing on the availability from other states, Hernandez (2017) found that increased lending from China was associated with a decline in Bank conditionality on loans in 54 African states. While Hernandez (2017) regards conditions positively as a way of increasing aid effectiveness and protecting resources, progressive scholars and NGOs have argued that they often undermine the state and harm the well-being of the poor (Bello 1994, Cammack 2002, Carroll 2010, SAPRIN 2004).
The World Bank’s terminology for loan conditions has changed over the decades, thus if you are reading loan documents available from the Bank’s project database, you might see terms like ‘pre-requisites,’ ‘policy action,’ ‘triggers,’ and ‘challenges.’ Conditions can be targeted at the macro- and mid-levels and the project level. DPLs focus on macro- and mid-level conditionality. In investment loans, conditions relate to the sector the loan is in. For example, the West Bengal Major Irrigation and Flood Management Project approved in 2019 called some of its conditions its ‘theory of change’ and included a condition of outsourcing operations and maintenance ‘to irrigation contractors on a performance basis’ (World Bank 2019, 7). This demonstrates the Bank’s commitment to private sector provision of public services funded by its loans. Investment loan conditions can have even broader implications for a sector, for example, a loan for a power plant in Vietnam required not only a private provider to build and operate the plant, but also legislation to promote the expansion of private providers in the sector, despite decades of problems in privatised electricity (Engel 2010).
Projects are evaluated by their expected rates of return, though the country’s overall creditworthiness is also a factor (Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997, Urquidi 1996). As early as 1949, due in part to the low level and poor quality of loan applications, the Bank established a system of technical assistance to develop, assess, and supervise projects (Bazbauers 2018). It also assisted with broader state macroeconomic planning and management. Both were done with the assistance of ‘fly-in, fly-out’ Western experts. Within two decades, the Bank had built four technical assistance pillars, a structure emulated by several other MDBs. These pillars included technical assistance embedded within projects to support implementation, stand-alone technical assistance project that often guided capacity development, survey missions that reviewed a recipient’s development status to propose future lending schedules, and professional training institutes delivering courses and seminars to senior public servants (Bazbauers 2018).
The World Bank requires projects to be managed in accordance with Bank-set accounting, auditing, and other standards, though in some ‘high-performing’ countries the Bank allows the use of local project management systems. All Bank loans are subject to social and environmental safeguards, though the efficacy of these has long been a matter of debate, with NGOs and academics pointing to limitations. The Bank also has policies around anti-corruption in project management and procurement. And, under pressure from the US, the Bank established an Inspection Panel in 1993 to allow people negatively affected by its projects a means of redress. As Park (2017) outlines, institutional isomorphism saw most of the other RDBs follow suit over the next decade.
In terms of procurement, the Articles of Agreement were ahead of their time in specifying that loans must be untied; tied aid requires recipients to spend aid in the donor country. Bilateral Western donors have made commitments since at least the late 1980s to progress the untying of their aid particularly for the least developed countries and low-income countries but have not yet fully achieved this. Still, formal untying of procurement from donor states can be misleading, as Bracking (2009) notes there is an expectation that the largest shareholders will be the largest beneficiaries of contracts from MDBs and other development finance institutions (DFIs). In the 1990s, the US, UK, Germany, Japan, and France were the largest beneficiaries of procurement in the World Bank (51.5% of total), AsDB (98.6%), and IADB (86.3%), though they only accounted for 20% of AfDB procurement (Bracking 2009, 119). By the mid-2000s, China and India had risen dramatically up the World Bank list, dominating in civil works contracts, and they were followed by Brazil as the next largest beneficiary. In consultancy contracts, Indonesia and the Russian Federation have joined the US, the UK, and France in the top five. Of course, the beneficiaries of winning contractors are not always clear in today’s globalised marketplace; contracts won by the Indonesian office of a global consultancy firm likely flow through tax havens to top income earners in the Global North.
The World Bank provides the framework for how project procurement should be undertaken and requires all Bank-financed work to be done by contractors and not the state. In terms of contracting out, in a small number of cases the Bank allows direct selection, essentially where there is only one suitable firm to provide the work or where there is a strong reason for a preferred firm. Most of the work, however, is put through open, competitive procurement. For small contracts, this may be limited to contractors in the borrower state, but the preferred mechanism is international competition. This requirement dates to loan agreements approved in 1951 (Mason and Asher 1973, 26). Competition for these contracts is complex and requires high-level technical and legal skills, access to technology and finance, and there is often a preference for larger companies that have the scale to complete complex projects (Bracking 2009). Many Western governments have long had special programmes to help their firms bid for projects and they utilise their in-country networks with government officials to benefit their companies; in other words, they adopt crony practices and North firms are not adverse to paying bribes to win projects. In practice, the lowest cost bid is not necessarily the best quality and once a company has won a contract through a low-cost bid, they can often negotiate further funding to complete it. International procurement also means that Global South countries are largely buying goods and services from places that are more developed than they are, either from the Global North or from a handful of large emerging economies like China, Brazil, and India. Thus, their debt repayments embody the labour costs – either directly in technical assistance or indirectly through the price of goods – of people paid more, often much more, than most workers in the poorest countries. In other words, MDB loans promote what Marxists label unequal exchange (Amin 2014).
Loan rates and charges vary in the World Bank dependent on the recipient country’s gross national income (GNI) per capita. Low-income countries (set as GNI below $1,025 in 2020) are eligible for IDA loans, which are highly concessional. Lower-middle income countries (GNI between $1,026 and $3,995 in 2010) can receive a blend of IBRD and IDA loans. Upper-middle income countries (GNI between $3,996 and $12,375) are eligible for IBRD loans only, which are on harder terms. As of April 2020, regular IDA loans had a maturity of 38 years, a six-year grace period where no repayments are made, and an interest rate of 3.125%; terms for small economies are slightly more generous, while for somewhat wealthier states eligible for a blend of IBRD and IDA loans, they are harder. The commitment charge for loans was zero. IBRD rates are a lot more variable depending on the term of the loan (8–20 years), the currency borrowed in, whether the loan was fixed or variable, and GNI per capita of the country. Interest rates are set in relation to the London Inter-Bank Offered Rate (LIBOR), which is an interest rate average determined from estimates given by major London banks. In April 2020, they ranged between LIBOR +0.65% and +2.05% and the loans had a front-end fee and commitment fee each of 0.25%.
Earlier in the chapter, we outlined the key factors shaping assessments of MDB creditworthiness, which determine how cheaply they can borrow and, in turn, on-lend for. Examining the IBRD, IADB, and CAF, Humphrey (2014) demonstrated that the IBRD can borrow most cheaply, followed by the IADB, which has a large US shareholding, while the CAF faces higher costs because its shareholders are regional states. But the different institutions have also taken different positions on net income, decisions about how much is used to build up reserves and equity (in lieu of increases in paid-in capital), and allocation of net income to alternate financing streams, in particular to technical assistance, trust funds, and concessional arms. CAF has focussed on minimising costs to borrowers, so its loans are not as expensive as would be expected given their cost of borrowing, while the IADB is closer to the World Bank in terms of interest rates (Humphrey 2014). The IBRD could lend more cheaply if it were not for these decisions.

5.1.5 Comparing the IBRD and other MDBs

Each of the above sections – voting rights, capital subscriptions, governance, and loans – has made some comparisons to other MDBs, demonstrating that many key features are shared among them. Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli (2018, 112) regard the MDBs as ‘a family of peer cross-referenced institutions adhering to common norms and standards, with large MDBs often acting as role models.’ Table 5.2, however, provides a more systematic comparison of the IBRD to the regional, sub-regional, and specialised development banks.

  • The OFID is the only MDB with headquarters in a non-member state.

  • The IFC has 100% paid-in capital and the IDA around 75%.
  • Three other MDBs have subscribed capital over $100 billion – the EIB, IADB, and AsDB. The AIIB is just below $100 billion.

  • No political interference.
  • Finances projects that the private sector will not.
