8 The specialised development banks

The specialised development banks are the fourth and final multilateral development bank (MDB) grouping we explore in this book. Compared to the relative typological cohesion of the World Bank Group, regional development banks (RDBs), and sub-regional development banks (SRDBs), the specialised development banks are far more disparate in mandate and operation; they are specialised as niche development financiers. Adding to the typological confusion, a few of the specialised banks could arguably be classified as regional or sub-regional actors, with their operations occurring in discrete geographical areas. Still, the mandate of the specialised banks sets them apart, often being focussed on narrower objectives compared to the other MDBs. Emerging in response to changes in the global political economy, including post-Second World War reconstruction, the 1973 oil price shock, and the 21st-century rise of emerging powers, the specialised banks first arrived in 1956 when the Council of Europe Development Bank (CEB) – the first MDB after the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD) – was founded. They continue to appear through to the 2015 launch of the China-led Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB), though most were established in the decade between 1968 and 1977.
There are nine specialised development banks, as outlined in Table 8.1. Two, the CEB and International Fund for Agricultural Development (IFAD), work as stand-alone actors, while the remaining seven can be viewed as three strategic blocs: an Arab-led grouping, a Nordic one, and the BRICS: Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa.

The Arab-led group of MDBs include the Arab Fund for Economic and Social Development (AFESD), Arab Bank for Economic Development in Africa (BADEA), Islamic Development Bank (IsDB), and OPEC Fund for International Development (OFID). They are closely associated and emerged within several years of each other between 1968 and 1976, in large part catalysed by rapid capital accumulation in oil exporting Arab countries. It is not an Arab-only grouping, comprising Islamic and oil-exporting elements, but Arab countries are at their heart. The Nordic group evolved from the Nordic Investment Bank (NIB) established in 1975 and was later joined by affiliated Nordic multilateral institutions. It is positioned within Nordic development cooperation arrangements and is led by Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, and Sweden with support from the Baltic states: Latvia, Lithuania, and Estonia. It promotes development in the Nordic-Baltic region and globally. The third group comprises the BRICS-led New Development Bank (NDB) and China-led AIIB, established in 2014 and 2015. They are expressions of the burgeoning influence of emerging economies in the 21st century and have sparked an exhaustive debate about whether they reinforce or challenge the Global North-led status quo. More to the point, as Humphrey (2019, 165) contends, the ‘recent creation of the’ NDB and AIIB shows that ‘MDBs appear to have continued relevance in global development finance.’
The specialised development banks are niche development financiers, with each of their mandates revealing much about their role. The CEB helps European countries manage the social impact of refugees and displaced persons as well as ecological disasters and IFAD mobilises concessional resources for agricultural development. IsDB supports economic and social progress in accordance with Shari’ah financial principles, the Nordic Development Fund (NDF) – an affiliate of the Nordic grouping – has since 2009 specialised in the provision of grants for climate change interventions in low-income countries, and the NDB and AIIB both support sustainable infrastructure financing. There are grey areas where some of the specialised banks could be labelled RDBs or SRDBs, for example, the NIB began from Nordic regional arrangements. Nonetheless, given their niche mandates, the nine MDBs discussed in this chapter are best positioned typologically as specialised development banks.
Apart from the CEB and IFAD, the other specialised development banks represent Nordic, Arab, and emerging economy influence, as noted above. This point is worth further elaboration, particularly since the Nordic development model is distinct from that of the US and Western Europe, the Arab-led MDBs often support Arab solidarity, and the emerging economies offer a geopolitical alternative to Global North-led finance. The following section explores these alternative sources of development finance, before the remainder of the chapter provides an analytical overview of the specialised MDBs, beginning chronologically with the CEB and concluding with the AIIB.

8.1 Alternative sources of development finance

8.1.1 Nordic development finance

The Nordic countries – Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, and Sweden – have a strong reputation for their social democratic and regulatory systems, consistently high human development rankings, and eco- and climate-friendly outlook (Clement 2004). Compounded by their standing as ‘honest brokers’ and ‘faithful implementers’ of international norms and rules, they have built considerable trustworthiness in their bilateral and multilateral interactions with less developed countries (Bengtsson, Elgstrom, and Tallberg 2004, 312). Broadly defined by ‘human internationalism’ (Engh and Pharo 2009), the foreign aid and development assistance programmes of the Nordic countries have long promoted poverty alleviation as both a moral obligation and a benefit to aid donors as much as aid recipients (Stokke 1989). Poverty alleviation, social welfare, environmental protection, good governance, and human rights typically characterise Nordic aid, with each Nordic country delivering their own national aid programmes as well as collaborating multilaterally through the Nordic Finance Group.
Inter-Nordic cooperation has seen largely consistent progress since the end of the Second World War, encompassing finance and trade, collective security, and environment and development (Czarny 2017). There have been ebbs and flows, however, with tensions created during the Cold War and by European Union (EU) accession. Despite these, multilateral engagement within the Nordic region remains strong, with Forsberg (2013) arguing that it stems not only from institutions, like the Nordic Council (founded in 1952) and Nordic Council of Ministers (established in 1971), but also a shared ‘Nordic identity’. In the context of multilateral development assistance, the Nordic Finance Group illustrates inter-Nordic cooperation.
Ørvik (1974, 88) made the prophetic claim that the ‘seventies may well become a watershed in Nordic regional cooperation.’ From a multilateral development finance (MDF) standpoint, this was very accurate. Following the NIB’s establishment in 1975, there came the Nordic Project Fund (Nopef) in 1982, NDF in 1988, and Nordic Environment Finance Corporation (NEFCO) in 1990; collectively, they comprise Nordic Finance Group. The Nordic countries are generally more progressive than OECD and EU donors in their foreign aid, well exceeding average official development assistance (ODA) contributions and donating more than other European countries to multilateral organisations. They are also distinct from US and Western European aid models, with a reputation as ‘soft donors’ that rely on recipient terms rather than dictating conditions (Danielson and Wohlgemuth 2003, Elgström and Delputte 2016). Further, the Nordic countries ‘publicly assumed the role of bridge-builders between developing and industrialised countries’ during African decolonisation (Engh and Pharo 2009, 115). The Swedish International Development Cooperation Agency’s (SIDA) engagement with African SRDBs and the appointment of two Nordic citizens to run the East African Development Bank (EADB) are examples of this trend. In addition, foreign aid served as a connecting node between African and Nordic countries and many ‘diplomatic missions in African countries were only established after the [Nordic] aid agencies were located there’ (Selbervik and Nygaard 2006, 22). Nordic aid has had its controversies, notably a joint Nordic development aid project established in 1968 was tangentially involved in President Julius Nyerere’s ujamaa peasant village programme that used force and coercion to become one of Africa’s largest resettlement efforts (see Paaskesen 2010). But, overall, the legacy of Nordic development cooperation is positive, standing as a viable (if smaller) alternative to other Western aid programmes.

8.1.2 Arab development finance

Regional integration and cooperation between Arab countries began in 1945 with the founding of the Arab League, a time when the countries of the Middle East were ranked among the lowest levels of socioeconomic development globally (Yousef 2004). It was spurred by Arab nationalism, anti-colonial sentiment, and backed by oil wealth and institutionalised through successive initiatives in the 1950s. The intent was to strengthen the structural position of the region (Corm 2006, Worrall 2017), and development finance formed one pillar.
Since the 1970s, Arab ODA has exceeded the average among OECD countries. Although peaking in the 1970s and 1980s, Arab ODA averaged 13% of global ODA and 75% of non-OECD ODA between 1973 and 2008, with most aid being untied and without conditions (Kellerman 2019, Rouis et al. 2010, Shushan and Marcoux 2011, Tok, Calleja, and El-Ghaish 2014, ). Like the Nordic countries, Arab development finance offers an alternative investment stream, particularly to countries that are not allies of OECD donors, especially the US.
Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and the UAE-lead Arab ODA and the Arab MDBs. There is a complex network of bilateral and multilateral Arab development organisations, which is unified under the Coordination Secretariat for Arab National and Regional Development Institutions. The Coordination Secretariat was established under the auspices of the AFESD in 1975 to coordinate Arab aid and avoid resource duplication – although, in practice, its power is limited to data collection and dissemination. It comprises three national development agencies – the Kuwait Fund for Arab Economic Development (1961), Abu Dhabi Fund for Development (1971), and Saudi Fund for Development (1974) – and four MDBs – the AFESD, BADEA, IsDB, and OFID – and also the IMF-like Arab Monetary Fund (AMF) and other bodies (Fritz and Mühlich 2019, Momani and Ennis 2012, Rouis et al. 2010, Shushan and Marcoux 2011, Zimmermann and Smith 2011). While the IsDB and OFID are not Arab-only MDBs, Arab countries are among their largest shareholders (Neumayer 2003).
The emergence of so many Arab development organisations in the 1960s and 1970s was prompted by the 1967 Arab-Israeli War and 1973 oil price shock, as well as broader attempts to further Arab foreign policy, promote regional stability and solidarity, and establish an alternative to Global North-led finance (El Mallakh and Kadhim 1976, Neumayer 2003). Tudoroiu (2012, 25–6) argues that the Organisation of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) can be understood as a mirror of the BRICS in that both groupings are heterogeneous and ‘the creation of OPEC was due to Third World nationalism directed against Western economic neo-colonialism,’ just as the formalisation of the BRICS was to carve out space for emerging economies. Further, both OPEC and the BRICS exercise financial and geopolitical influence. The Arab-Israeli War led to an expansion of Arab foreign aid as oil-exporters not directly participating in the conflict – Kuwait, Libya, and Saudi Arabia – offered financial support to countries such as Jordan, Egypt, and Syria that suffered heavy economic and human losses (Corm 2006, Porter 1986, Williams 1976).
The 1973 oil shock prompted a major expansion of Arab MDF. The oil-rich Arab countries used some of their vast petrodollar reserves as aid to (i) offset potential negative political fallout from oil-importing countries suffering financial decline from oil price spikes and (ii) to dissuade the Global North and South that oil reserves were being used as an economic ‘weapon’ (Corm 2006, El Mallakh and Kadhim 1976, Porter 1986, Shushan and Marcoux 2011). Equally, though, key Western powers encouraged many of these ventures as outlined in Chapter 4. As Corm (2006, 294) argues, Arab aid was not only political but at the time ‘Arab oil-exporting countries did not have the capacity to absorb the new financial flows that were accruing to them,’ a situation similar to that of China in the NDB and AIIB. The resultant petrodollars were not just used in aid but invested in Northern banks, which became a key driver of the 1982 debt crisis.
Arab countries received 62% of Arab ODA between 1970 and 2008, followed by Asian and African countries with large Muslim populations (Momani and Ennis 2012). A ‘striking feature of Arab aid is its high volatility… Arab aid has had huge swings over the years, and changes in oil prices seem to be the major driving factor’ (Villanger 2007, 228). Cumulative Arab ODA increased from $7.7 billion between 1970 and 1974 to $32.7 billion from 1980 to 1984, and then declined to $6.8 billion between 1995 and 1999 as oil prices fell. Next, it rapidly increased to $15.7 billion as oil prices spiked between 2000 and 2004 (Corm 2006). The four Arab MDBs are financed from capital subscriptions and loan repayments and thus are fairly sheltered from oil price fluctuations (Rouis et al. 2010).

8.1.3 BRICS development finance

The BRIC acronym was coined by Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Jim O’Neill in 2001, describing countries ‘with the most potential for growth in the first half of the twenty-first century, based on demography, the size of potential markets, recent growth rates, and the embrace of globalisation’ (Robinson 2015, 4). At first used as an investment strategy, the 2008 Global Financial Crisis (GFC) catalysed the formalisation of the grouping as the crisis undermined the Global North’s paradigmatic credibility (Cammack 2018, Duggan 2015, Stuenkel 2015, Tudoroiu 2012). With the 2010 admission of South Africa, the BRICS built new institutional arrangements, including the NDB and Contingent Reserve Arrangement (CRA) – an IMF-style $100 billion fund that member countries can draw from in financial emergencies – and they are all members of the China-led AIIB, while India, Brazil, and South Africa comprise the IBSA Dialogue Forum. We agree with the argument that the BRICS do not intend to displace the capitalist status quo (see Harmer and Buse 2014, Nayyar 2016, Robinson 2015, Thakur 2014, Tudoroiu 2012). Instead, they ‘pose a within-system challenge… they challenge its most liberal content, while becoming dependent on, and engaging with, its existing institutional structures’ (Stephen 2014, 914). Nevertheless, the BRICS demonstrate an ‘aspiration to be “rule makers” instead of “rule takers”’ (Duggan 2015, 17).
The rise and sustainability of the BRICS have been exhaustively reviewed over the past two decades. Some economic analyses claim that the BRICS are finished because they have not been able to maintain predicted growth rates (see Sharma 2012), even though they recovered quickly after the GFC (Chen and De Lombaerde 2014). Such analyses overlook the fact that the BRICS are more than simply an investment strategy; they are a formalised grouping and they are actively changing global governance. Yet, their institutionalisation runs into difficulties given their disparate cultures, politics, and demographics. South Africa, as an example, has often been regarded an odd choice for the grouping, given its relative economic size. However, its inclusion reflects a logic of having strategic influence in sub-Saharan Africa, and South Africa has a leading role in the Southern African Development Community and African Union as well as being the only African country in the G20 (Petropoulos 2015, Tella 2017). Further, Russia has been constrained by low oil prices and Western sanctions (Stephen 2017) and Brazil under President Jair Bolsarno has created tensions by aligning closely with US President Donald Trump, hence some of their original unity has dissipated with political changes. The BRICS additionally display ‘various symptoms of internal crisis and socioeconomic oppression… including severe inequality, poverty, unemployment, diseases, violence… inadequate education and prohibitions on labour organising… [and] worsening political and civil rights violations’ (Bond 2013, 267).
The development agenda of the BRICS illustrates both their role as alternative sources of finance and their strategic pursuit of their own foreign policy interests, as well as some challenge to Global North donors (Chin 2014, Gu et al. 2016, Naim 2007, Tok, Calleja, and El-Ghaish 2014, 595, Woods 2008). In founding of the NDB and AIIB, it has been usefully suggested that the BRICS are not restructuring the world economy but positioning themselves more influentially within it. They do not want to supplant the existing system because they benefit from its institutions (Qobo and Soko 2015, Zavyalova 2016).
China, as the centrepiece of the BRICS, is particularly relevant to MDF. The NDB and AIIB are ‘entwined with the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) – a vast infrastructure-building, trade, and investment strategy that Beijing launched in 2013’ (Kalathil 2018, 52). Notably, however, the new MDBs are but one facet of Chinese development assistance, with finance moving in larger volumes through the China Development Bank and Export-Import Bank. This finance is generally well received by many developing countries given it lacks the conditionality of Global North-led MDF (Chin and Gallagher 2019, 246). Significantly, Chinese development assistance almost exclusively focusses on infrastructure investment.

8.2 Council of Europe Development Bank

Established in 1956, the CEB is Europe’s first MDB and the world’s second after the IBRD. While its European geographical focus suggests it could be labelled an RDB, its social mandate situates it better as a specialised bank: the
primary purpose of the Bank is to help in solving the social problems with which European countries are or may be faced as a result of the presence of refugees, displaced persons or migrants consequent upon movements of refugees or other forced movements of populations and as a result of the presence of victims of natural or ecological disasters.
(CEB 2019)
The impetus for CEB was the movement of large numbers of refugees and displaced persons into Western Europe after the Second World War. Nonetheless, it was structured as an MDB with an initial $6.7 million capital base obtained from member subscriptions. Of that amount, 25% was immediately paid up and the remaining 75% fully paid by 1960. This high level of paid-in capital enabled CEB to borrow from capital markets (Bussière and Willaert 2006, 24). In 2017, CEB’s subscribed capital base was €5.5 billion, ranking it broadly in the middle of the specialised development banks, although far smaller than the IsDB, NDB, and AIIB.
The Council of Europe was established in 1949 and today operates as Europe’s leading human rights organisation. The CEB was founded several years by the then eight Council members, with CEB ‘attached to the Council of Europe and administered under its supreme authority’ (CEB 2019), although the MDB was financially and legally independent. The Council emerged from political cooperation after the Second World War, with the 1948 Brussels Pact countries – Britain, France, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg – coming together with Italy, Ireland, Denmark, Norway, and Sweden in May 1949 to establish it. By 1950, the Council expanded to include Greece, Turkey, Iceland, and West Germany. Resettlement of displaced persons and refugees was prioritised by Council members, as were initiatives to promote human and social rights (Bussière and Willaert 2006).
Pierre Schneiter was appointed as the Special Council of Europe Representative for National Refugees and Over-Population in December 1953 to investigate the feasibility of establishing an organisation to manage European resettlement issues. Schneiter concluded that a socially oriented bank led by the Council would be most appropriate, financing resettlement actions and building employment opportunities (Bussière and Willaert 2006). After tensions were resolved between Council members over financial questions, the CEB was signed into effect on 16 April 1956 by West Germany, France, Belgium, Greece, Iceland, Italy, Luxembourg, and Turkey. Notably, the UK, although a Council of Europe founding member, did not join. When first established, CEB was called the Council of Europe Resettlement Fund for National Refugees and Over-Population in Europe, with the name change in 1999 to better reflect both its growth into an MDB and its revised strategic focus on development concerns beyond resettlement.
The first decade of operations addressed post-Second World War resettlement, but CEB became less relevant during the 1970s and 1980s as the inter-European movement of people stabilised. Still, it had a tenfold increase in annual approvals and diversified its loan portfolio to sectors not traditionally associated with resettlement such as housing. The end of the Cold War renewed the MDB’s relevance; a focus on post-Soviet transition became a masthead in the 1990s and 2000s, with CEB’s 2005–9 Development Plan prioritising its 20 new members mostly situated in Central and Eastern Europe that joined after 1991 (Bussière and Willaert 2006). Successive Council of Europe Summits revised the CEB’s mandate: 1993 Vienna Summit – promoting regional collaboration; 1997 Strasbourg Summit – strengthening European social cohesion; and 2005 Warsaw Summit – building a free, democratic, and more inclusive Europe. The Bank’s current priorities include sustainable and inclusive growth, job creation, public infrastructure, gender equality, the integration of refugees, displaced persons, and migrants, and climate change mitigation and adaptation. The CEB today increasingly resembles a traditional MDB.
The CEB has 42 member states, with admission eligibility contingent on membership in the Council of Europe, which has 47 members. Prior to Brexit, Austria and the UK were the only EU members not in it. All member countries are eligible to receive CEB loans, a contrast to the IBRD where lending is reserved for countries below a GNI threshold. Like the European Investment Bank (EIB), CEB is exclusively owned by regional European members; there are no non-regional shareholders. Germany, France, and Italy are the largest shareholders – each holding over 16% voting share – followed by Spain and Turkey. Over half of the remaining member countries each control less than 1% shareholding.
As per Table 8.2, five Governors have led the CEB, with appointee nationality alternating between two of the three largest shareholders: France and Germany. The first Governor, René Plas, called the Resettlement Fund an ‘organisation for human solidarity on a banking basis’ (cited in CEB 2016a, 1), demonstrating both Keynesian ideals and the legacy of the banking model. Rolf Wenzel is the incumbent Governor and he previously served in Germany’s Federal Ministry for Finance, as Senior Advisor to Germany’s IMF Executive Director, and as an Asian Development Bank (AsDB) Director, again demonstrating MDB networks. Wenzel (cited in International Banker 2017) highlighted the benefits of the banking model when he said that the
CEB is a self-financing institution, so there are no demands on its members for capital beyond their subscribed capital contributions. The bank is financially sound, has a solid loan portfolio, and has no default or arrears on its loans. It also maintains a prudent financial strategy and an efficient risk-management framework.
Wenzel’s predecessor Raphaël Alomar similarly noted in 2004 that CEB ‘intends to enhance its capacity as a development bank’ by strengthening its expertise and intensifying ‘pre-existing close partnerships with other financial as well as non-financial international institutions’ (cited in CEB 2004, 11).

Like the IBRD, CEB’s main lending instruments are project and programme loans. Funds are raised on international capital markets and, in 2017, CEB issued its first social inclusion bond. In summary, CEB is a relatively small MDB compared to the RDBs, but it is comparable to many of the larger SRDBs. Despite its social mandate, its organisational structure and operational guidelines reveal it to be an investment-oriented MDB, prioritising economic growth through small and medium enterprises (SMEs), job creation, physical infrastructure, and housing. It also collaborates extensively with financial intermediaries – an approach the large MDBs are now increasingly taking – and relies on joint-venture financing to deliver its projects. Thus, despite its specialised mandate, it sits comfortably within the standard MDB framework, reflecting many common organisational traits.

8.3 Arab Fund for Economic and Social Development

The Agreement Establishing AFESD was adopted by the Economic and Social Council of the Arab League on 16 May 1968, making it the first Arab-led MDB, although the agreement only became effective on 18 December 1971 (Rouis et al. 2010). It commenced operations in 1974. As per its original Articles of Agreement, its purposes were to participate in the ‘financing of economic and social development projects in the Arab states and countries’ by financing ‘economic projects of an investment character’ and delivering technical assistance to public and private enterprises. At the time of its establishment, it differed from the national Arab development funds and most MDBs, with the exception of CEB, in that ‘social projects’ were explicitly mandated (Porter 1986, Tok, Calleja, and El-Ghaish 2014). The original Articles were amended in 1997 following a study sanctioned by the Board of Governors to investigate how it could better support the Arab private sector (AFESD 1997). The revised Articles expand its mandate to finance private sector projects, establish and participate in equity capital, and establish and manage special funds. This aligned its lending operations to reflect contemporary mainstream MDBs.
Unlike IsDB and OFID but like BADEA, the AFESD is an exclusively Arab- led development bank, with shareholder eligibility contingent on Arab League membership. It has an entirely regional and borrowing membership, currently consisting of the 22 Arab League member states. Consistent with trends in Arab bilateral and multilateral development assistance, Kuwait and Saudi Arabia are the largest shareholders, with Kuwait being responsible for submitting the original proposal for AFESD to the Arab League in the late 1960s (Shushan and Marcoux 2011).
A Director General leads the AFESD and chairs the Board of Directors. There have been three Directors General. Incumbent Director General Abdlatif Al-Hamad has led AFESD since 1985, a long-term appointment rare among the MDBs, though IsDB is similar. Interviews conducted with Al-Hamad by Villanger (2007, 242) revealed the significance of AFESD: ‘recipient countries facing severe conditions for receiving finance from the WB/IMF have turned to Arab donors to get the same finance without having to implement any conditions.’
The AFESD provides financing to public sector projects on concessional terms and to private sector projects promoting private sector development, as well as through a special account to fund small- and medium-sized projects, which was established in 2009 (Shushan and Marcoux 2011, 1970). In 2016, it softened its lending terms and reduced interest rates for low-income and other member countries. In addition, it provides a suite of technical and advisory services to support project feasibility.
Between 1974 and 2017, AFESD approved 663 loans totalling $32 billion, with infrastructure projects receiving close to 71% of all approvals (Engen and Prizzon 2018). As Corm (2006, 308) argues, AFESD’s ‘basic activities are to provide soft lending to Arab League countries for infrastructure projects,’ with an emphasis on regional infrastructure projects such as those ‘promoting the inter-Arab grid to connect national electricity systems.’ Rouis et al. (2010, 26) expands this point, commenting that a
landmark of AFESD’s contribution to Arab regional integration is the financing of the Arab Electricity Grids Interconnection Projects. Over the past ten years, total investment from all sources in these projects has reached US$ 1 billion… AFESD has contributed over two thirds of the financing of these projects on concessional terms.
Egypt, Morocco, Tunisia, Oman, and Mauritania have been the largest recipients of AFESD disbursements. Cumulative loan approvals nearly doubled between 2007 and 2017, reflecting its capital base reaching $10 billion. Further, like many other MDBs, it has promoted co-financing to deliver its development projects. Between 1974 and 2016, over 11% of AFESD co-financing was with the World Bank, IFAD, and AfDB and over 56% with Arab national and regional development institutions.
Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Qatar have not received loans from AFESD but have received grants, demonstrating that even the wealthiest oil-exporting shareholders can benefit from it. Every other member country – except for Comoros, with which it has had very limited engagement – has received both loans and grants. As of 2017, Palestine had received only five loans but 173 grants totalling over $92 million, reflecting Arab concern for the territory. Between 2001 and 2010, it allocated 10% of its yearly net income to the Palestinian Authority and, from 1974 to 2007, 19% of AFESD grants were allocated to the West Bank and Gaza (Rouis et al. 2010).
In summary, while it is a relatively small specialised development bank, reflecting the subscribed capital and annual approvals of an SRDB, the AFESD has nonetheless performed an important role as a niche financier of Arab integration, mirroring the CEB’s social mandate.

8.4 Arab Bank for Economic Development in Africa

The BADEA was the second MDB established by the Arab League. It was formed on 18 February 1974 when its establishing agreement was signed, and it began operations in March 1975. It was the first inter-regional bank founded to build ‘Arab and African solidarity’ (Neumayer 2003, 136), emerging in part because the 1973 oil price shock severely impacted many African economies (Johnson 1983). Politically, BADEA aimed to curtail Israel’s influence in Africa, with Uganda being the first country to break ties with Israel in 1972. BABEA thus represented both the expansion of Arab capital in line with the region’s growing petrodollar reserves and a political tool to build Arab-African cooperation to minimise Israel’s influence following the 1973 Arab-Israeli War (Johnson 1983, Porter 1986, Shushan and Marcoux 2011).
BADEA’s (2009) objective ‘is to foster economic, financial and technical cooperation between Africa countries and Arab World countries,’ drawing Arab public and private investment into African natural resources and extractives (Johnson 1983, Porter 1986, Shushan and Marcoux 2011). The beneficiaries of BADEA operations may include African governments, public and private companies operating in African countries in which African governments or citizen have a majority capital shareholding, and mixed Arab-African companies pursuing development outcomes (BADEA 2009). BADEA encourages Arab-African trade by financing Arab exports into Africa and, in the mid-2000s, it began offering concessional loans (Shushan and Marcoux 2011). The mandate on Arab-African cooperation makes it a specialised development bank.
The BADEA’s shareholders are 18 Arab League member countries. The five largest shareholders (in order) are Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Libya, Iraq, and the UAE. Collectively, these countries hold over 77% shareholding, with Saudi Arabia controlling close one quarter. All shareholders are Arab countries, whereas African non-members are the recipients of BADEA’s financial and technical assistance; the borrowers do not have any voting power – it is very much a donor led MDB.
A Director General leads the BADEA, who is appointed from the non-permanent members of the Board of Directors. The Board has eleven members, with the nine largest shareholders holding permanent seats and the smaller member countries jointly selecting two non-permanent representatives. Since 1974, five Director Generals have led BADEA, each from a different country (see Table 8.3). In line with South-South cooperation ideals, Dr Chedly Ayari, the first Director General, commented in 1975 that
Our intention is… to put Afro-Arab cooperation on the map as a dimension of international economic cooperation which all countries, especially the rich industrial nations, must reckon with. Arab aid and cooperation efforts should be in a context of a new world economic order, based on equality and justice.
(cited in Johnson 1983, 625)
Such rhetoric has been repeated in the NDB and AIIB.

Of that, $1.1 billion was earmarked for public sector projects, $450 million for private sector projects, and $50 million for technical assistance operations. Three pillars underpinned the seventh Plan: support for African development through (i) public and private sector project financing, (ii) technical assistance to build human and institutional capacity, and (iii) Arab exports to African states; the pillars reiterate BADEA’s Arab-African cooperation mandate as a specialised development bank.

8.5 Islamic Development Bank

An international Islamic financial institution, IsDB’s Articles of Agreement were ratified in 1974 pursuant to the Declaration of Intent issued by the 1973 Conference of Finance Ministers of Muslim Countries held in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia (Ray and Kamal 2019). The Board of Governors held their inaugural meeting in July 1975 and the MDB formally began operations in October. It is unique even among Arab-led specialised banks because it aims to foster ‘economic development and social progress [in] member countries and Muslim communities individually as well as jointly in accordance with the principles of the Shari’ah’ (IsDB 2019 emphasis added). Thus, it ‘strictly bans the practice of interest on financial transactions’ (Tok, Calleja, and El-Ghaish 2014, 597). Like the RDBs and SRDBs, it promotes national and regional development, participates in equity capital, undertakes public and private project investment, manages special funds, promotes trade, and conducts research (IsDB 2019). Still, its focus on lending to people from one religion and conformance to its religious principles mean it is best understood as a specialised development bank.
IsDB emerged as a response to the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC) discussing the role of a multilateral organisation delivering Shari’ah-compliant financial resources to member countries. The idea was initially debated during the 1970 Second Islamic Conference of Foreign Ministers and was endorsed by King Faisal Bin Abdulaziz of Saudi Arabia (Hernandez and Vadlamannati 2017, Kuhn 1982). It ‘was specifically created to bridge the gap between rich and poor Muslim countries’ (Wilson 1989, 444), promoting Islam and building Arab solidarity (Villanger 2007). The focus on Shari’ah principles means IsDB has never instituted policy-based lending, nor engaged significantly – compared to other MDBs – in financial sector lending given the Shari’ah prohibition on excessive speculation (Kellerman 2019, Rouis et al. 2010).
Conformance to Shari’ah principles has not prevented IsDB from becoming an influential MDB. It has developed a suite of innovative lending instruments. During its first decade, it introduced short-term trade credits delivered under a service charge, machinery leasing, and private sector equity investment arrangements (Porter 1986). Its current modalities include: interest-free loans with a service fee to recoup administrative costs of no more than 2.5% per annum; profit-sharing schemes; leasing of capital goods; and, receipt of rent from borrowers (Corm 2006, Lessambo 2015, Rouis et al. 2010, Shushan and Marcoux 2011). It is worth noting that IsDB has been able to pursue these approaches in large part because of the petrodollar surpluses of its leading shareholders, notably Saudi Arabia. Yet, in the current low interest rate environment, its products are not as competitive as they were.
The IsDB has 57 member countries, including Arab and non-Arab shareholders, with membership eligibility contingent on being an OIC member. Arab countries are a minority numerically, with non-Arab members from Africa, Asia, and Latin America, but they are the largest capital subscribers (Shushan and Marcoux 2011). The largest ten shareholders are: Saudi Arabia (23.5%), Libya (9.43%), Iran (8.25%), Nigeria (7.66%), the UAE (7.51%), Qatar (7.18%), Egypt (7.07%), Kuwait (6.92%), Turkey (6.25%), and Algeria (2.54%). These shareholders hold over 85% of capital subscriptions. Saudi Arabia has exercised considerable influence through its large shareholding as well as ISDB’s headquarters being in Jeddah and the only two Presidents both being Saudi nationals. As Hernandez and Vadlamannati (2017, 913–4) comment, ‘fulfilling and maintaining its role as a leader in Islamic societies is a foreign policy priority for Saudi Arabia and it perceives itself as the principal responsible for the development, cooperation and support of the region.’ This foreign policy priority echoes that of the US in the World Bank.
The small number of presidents is unusual for an MDB. The first President, Dr Ahmad Mohamed Ali Al Madani, led IsDB from 1975 to 2016; he was re-elected several times. Thus, much of IsDB’s history was written by Dr Ali’s leadership. From Saudi Arabia, Dr Ali previously served as Director of the Scientific and Islamic Institute in Yemen (1958–9) and Acting Rector of King Abdulaziz University (1967–72). Dr Bandar Hajjar succeeded Dr Ali in October 2016. Among other roles, Dr Hajjar once served as Saudi Arabia’s Hajj Minister. His IsDB nomination caused discontent due to his alleged mismanagement of the annual Hajj Pilgrimage and the 2015 ‘Hajj Stampede’ (Taylor 2015). At the time of his appointment, Dr Hajjar said that IsDB ‘needs to shift away from providing individual, small-scale intervention to value chain solutions’ (cited in Zuvela 2017). This shift became part of an ongoing transformation of IsDB, decentralising its organisational structure and altering how it engages with borrowers. Interestingly, as Ray and Kamal (2019, 204 and 206) comment at length, day-to-day decisions are overseen by a
larger than usual number of engineers and other technical members. The bank more or less relies on the judgment of these teams, and thus avoids the need for the Board’s involvement for every project approval. Indeed the… IsDB does not have a resident Board. The Directorial Board only gets involved in the approval process for projects deemed sensitive.
Over its history, the IsDB evolved into the IsDB Group, comparable to the expansion of the IBRD into the World Bank Group. Its conformance to Shari’ah principles is unique but the rapid growth of trade finance via ITFC demonstrates that traditional banking demands of turnover and income (perhaps even profits) are not absent.

8.6 Nordic Investment Bank

Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, and Sweden agreed to establish the NIB in 1975 to promote economic growth and cooperation between the five Nordic countries. It began operations in 1977. As per its Establishing Agreement, NIB prioritises investment projects, with financing available based on sound banking principles and socioeconomic considerations. Like the EIB, it regards itself more an investment bank than a development bank (Normann 2019), though its sectoral focuses resemble the other MDBs (Engen and Prizzon 2018). Operations were initially restricted to the Nordic region, but the NIB approved its first investment loan outside of its membership area in 1983 and in 1992 participated in the Baltic Investment Programme designed to help build the private sectors of Latvia, Lithuania, and Estonia following independence from Soviet rule (Hellén and Eriksson 2011). NIB’s broad mandate today is a prosperous and sustainable Nordic-Baltic region, with project finance directed to improving Nordic and Baltic competitiveness and environmental sustainability, and is part of an institutionalised ‘Nordic voice,’ underpinned by the ‘common values of a democratic tradition, pluralism, environmentalism, and a common sense of solidarity’ (Rūse 2014, 235). In 2016, Finland’s then Prime Minister Juha Sipilä described the NIB as ‘not only a child of Nordic co-operation; today, it is also the most tangible institution of Nordic-Baltic co-operation’ (cited in NIB 2016).
Attempts to establish a Nordic investment bank had been made in the 1950s, alongside efforts to create a customs union. New attempts were made during the 1960s. It was not until the third attempt made in late 1974 and early 1975 that success followed, formalising Nordic integration (Normann 2019, Wiklund and Sundelius 1979). The NIB, as it would become, came to be favourably regarded by the Nordic governments as being able to effectively coordinate major investment projects and stave off pressures from high oil prices and deteriorating current account balances (Dennis 2015, Normann 2019). This is not to suggest that support was uniform; Iceland demonstrated unequivocal support, Finland and Sweden broadly supported it, but there was significant resistance from Denmark and Norway and from large Nordic business organisations. Tensions focussed on weighted membership representation – given economic and population size disparities between the Nordic countries – and loan eligibility conditions, i.e., the factors considered when approving projects (Dennis 2015). In the end, several factors came together to explain why this proposal succeeded, including the political costs of failing a third time to establish a Nordic bank, a compressed timetable for negotiations, and the proposal not being attached to a complex customs union (Dennis 2015).
The five Nordic countries are the NIB’s founding members, while the three Baltic states – Latvia, Lithuania, and Estonia – joined in January 2005. The Baltic states expressed interest in joining after receiving loans in the early 1990s (Hellén and Eriksson 2011). In contrast to other MDBs, member shareholdings are based on economy size and not share purchases. Sweden is thus the largest shareholder with 34.6% capital share, followed by Norway (21.5%) and Denmark (21.1%). Iceland and Estonia are the smallest shareholders, holding 0.9% each. Its authorised capital is €6.1 billion and paid-in capital is €418.6 million, which is significant given that the NIB can grant loans and guarantees up to 250% of capital reserves (Lessambo 2015).
As per Table 8.5, five Presidents have led the Bank, one from each of the Nordic states. The incumbent President is Henrik Normann, who took office in April 2012. The Office of President is supported by the Executive Committee, Asset and Liability Committee, Credit Committee, Finance Committee, and Business and Technology Committee – a governance structure resembling more an investment bank than an MDB. As of December 2018, the NIB employed 199 people in permanent positions, comparable in size to most SRDBs, with the average employment length being 12.2 years.

To build its non-member lending portfolio, NIB has established cooperation agreements with the BSTDB, CABEI, and CAF and with countries in Africa, Asia, Eurasia, Latin America, and the Middle East. Notably, in comparison to the ‘flexible’ and ‘rapid’ project approval process of the NDB and AIIB, the NIB opts for more rigorous and lengthy processes: ‘NIB considers its thorough credit and mandate reviewing processes as adding a quality stamp to our customers’ project’ (Normann 2019).
Over the past two decades, annual loan approvals doubled from €2.1 billion in 1997 to €4.3 billion in 2018. It offers long-term loans and guarantees on competitive market terms to the public and private sectors and normal total project costs are €20 million and above, with NIB financing generally not exceeding 50% of total project costs (Normann 2019). Illustrating again that it regards itself more as an investment bank than a development bank, Normann (2019) says that the ‘NIB does not offer development finance as such, but provides long-term loans and guarantees… to projects contributing to our mandate, based on sound banking principles.’ In 2018, NIB’s sectoral approvals were industries and services (31%), infrastructure, transportation, and telecommunications (27%), energy and environment (22%), and financial institutions and SMEs (20%); such sectoral approvals denote its investment project portfolio.
The NIB has an explicit environmental mandate, translated into climate project financing and green bond issuances. Between 2011 and 2018, the Nordic MDB issued €3.5 billion in environmental bonds, and the Nordic share of cumulative green bond issuances between 2007 and 2018 was an estimated 7%, meaning the Nordic countries are at the forefront of this area of climate action. Further, all loans are assessed on their environmental benefits – ranging from pollution prevention to climate change mitigation and adaptation – and, in 2018, NIB updated its Environmental Bond Framework ‘to more widely finance investments that benefit the environment’ (Normann 2019).
Like the World Bank Group and IsDB Group, the NIB is part of the Nordic Finance Group made up of four international financial institutions (IFIs) headquartered in Finland: the NIB, NDF, NEFCO, and Nopef. While relatively small, the Nordic Finance Group reveals a complex network of complementary financing arrangements oriented to the approval and disbursal of climate finance. Nopef was the first part of the group formed in 1982. Today, it is managed by NEFCO and offers financing to Nordic private sector SMEs to prepare (notably green) investments outside the EU area; it approved some 3,000 projects totalling €104 million between 1982 and 2018. Compared to other MDBs, the Nordic Finance Group IFIs were early adopters of climate and environmental action, with rhetoric supported by their loan portfolios and mainstreaming initiatives.
The NDF was established in 1988 and began operations in 1989 originally as a concessional lending window for social and economic development in developing countries globally. After acting as a minor MDB for its first fifteen years, the Nordic Development Cooperation Ministers in 2005 recommended disbanding it after the member countries, notably Denmark, failed to agree on a fifth capital replenishment and suspended financing. In 2009, following a review supported by Finland and the Nordic Council endorsing that the NDF continue its activities, the member countries approved a new mandate, to shift from soft loans for social and economic development to grants for climate change interventions in low- income countries (Amland 2018, Normann 2019). Its head office is in the NIB’s Helsinki headquarters, with the Nordic fund acquiring services at self-cost price from NIB – office premises, administration, ICT services, and accounting – while remaining legally independent. The NIB has an appointed observer on the NDF’s Board of Directors. Thus, it seems to operate a bit more separately than the IDA does from the IBRD.
The Nordic fund’s capital stock has increased steadily since commencing operations: 1991 €4 million; 1994 €60 million; 2000 €360 million; and 2008 €691 million. As of 2015, its subscribed capital was €1 billion, with capital drawn from the development cooperation budgets of the five Nordic member countries: Sweden (37%), Denmark (23%), Norway (21%), Finland (18%), and Iceland (1%). Yet, as Amland (2018) highlights,
If the Nordic donors do not inject more capital, the fund’s resources will slowly be depleted… Several sources confirm that there is disagreement about the way forward among Nordic donors. Denmark remains opposed to new replenishments, Norway and Sweden have a wait-and-see approach while Finland and Iceland favour an expansion of NDF. Since Nordic donors cannot agree upon a joint replenishment of NDF, voluntary donor contributions have been raised as an alternative way of providing more resources to NDF.
Wihtol (2013) argues that the niche fund should be strengthened due to its strong track record as nimble, respected, and efficient. It has a client-friendly reputation that focusses on co-financing, promotion of ‘progressive’ Nordic values such as equality, and on the most vulnerable. Its innovative project portfolio has been emulated in Latin America.
Since adopting its new mandate in 2009, the NDF has been led by three Managing Directors: Helge Semb, Pasi Hellman, and Karin Isaksson, whose history demonstrates links to MDBs and development players. Iterating its relevance as a niche MDB, Hellman said that the Nordic fund
has its own narrow pigeonhole in the field of climate finance… One of our greatest strengths is the fact that we are a small and agile fund. We can even focus on small and micro-enterprise projects for which the World Bank, for instance, is too big.
(cited in Toivanen ND)
Between 1989 and 2008, the Nordic fund approved 188 concessional credits valued at €940 million, with sectoral priorities being infrastructure, education, agriculture, and environment. Since 2009, annual climate intervention grants have been few and small in cost: an average of 12 projects totalling €40 million annually. In 2018, it approved nine projects totalling €47.2 million (48% to Africa, 22% to Latin America, 20% to Asia, and 10% to multiple regions) (NDF 2018). It regularly co-finances projects with other MDBs and the Nordic bilateral development organisations. The NDF also finances and manages the Nordic Climate Facility (NCF), which targets private and public partnerships between Nordic and NDF-partner organisations with the aim of building innovative climate change projects. Twenty-one countries in Africa, Asia, and Latin America are eligible for NCF financing.
In 1989, concurrent to the NDF commencing operations, NIB formed NEFCO as a special unit. NEFCO and NIB became independent organisations in 1993, with NEFCO mandated to facilitate investments in green growth and climate change action. It ‘focuses on small and medium-sized projects with demonstrable value. The IFI also offers bankability through risk capital, grants, and environmental verification’ (Normann 2019). NEFCO in 2018 was the first Nordic institution to receive a Green Climate Fund (GCF) accreditation. In that same year, it approved 124 new projects for €64.6 million.
In summary, the NIB is like other specialised development banks in that it could be classified as a regional, sub-regional, or specialised bank. Its distinctly Nordic political and economic values as well as its weight on environmental and climate action, however, makes it a specialised bank, as does its role at the centre of the Nordic Finance Group. Similarly, the NDF is ‘an important addition to the Nordic toolbox’ (Wihtol 2013); it is small and its future depends upon Nordic capital replenishments but it is innovative and demonstrates that debt is not always the answer.

8.7 OPEC Fund for International Development

OFID was the fourth and final Arab-led MDB to emerge between 1968 and 1976. While not an Arab-only MDB, Saudi Arabia is the largest shareholder and Arab countries hold close to 65% of capital subscriptions (Tok, Calleja, and El-Ghaish 2014). Established in January 1976, it was proposed at the March 1975 Conference of the Sovereign and Heads of State of OPEC to channel aid to developing countries. It was an instrument to strengthen financial cooperation and solidarity between Global South oil-exporters and importers (Shushan and Marcoux 2011). As Ibrahim Shihata (1979, 31), OFID’s first Director General, commented, the
adjustment of the price of oil and the financial surplus it generated, coupled with the growing scarcity of this strategic commodity, have all given the oil producing countries of the South a new bargaining position, placing them, at least temporarily, in the vanguard of developing countries in their quest for a new world economic order.
Put simply, OFID arose from the 1973 oil price shock as both an expression of South-South solidarity and the growing geopolitical influence of oil-exporting emerging economies. While the global importance of OPEC foreign aid has declined in recent decades, ‘OPEC aid was a major player in international development,’ contributing ‘on average 13.5% of all aid between 1974 and 1994,’ and its aid was ‘heavily driven by geopolitics’ (Kragelund 2008, 565).
As per its Establishing Agreement, OFID provides concessional financing for balance of payments support and the implementation of development projects in non-OPEC developing countries as well as loans to international development agencies with developing countries as beneficiaries. OPEC countries are not eligible to receive OFID finance. OFID was originally called the OPEC Special Fund and had a different charter but its mandate was similar, especially the strengthening of financial cooperation between OPEC members and other developing countries by providing concessional finance (Shihata 1976). The major distinction between the Special Fund and OFID concerns their organisational autonomy and formalised status. The Special Fund was ‘a collective aid facility of OPEC member countries, in the form of an international account jointly owned by these countries and jointly administered by a “Governing Committee” of which they were all members’ (Shihata 1980, 879). In other words, the Fund was a temporary ‘clearinghouse for aid that oil-producing states were well-placed to give as a result of sharply increased revenues from oil exports’ (Shushan and Marcoux 2011, 1970). OFID, however, is an MDB. The Special Fund commenced operations in August 1976 with an initial endowment of $800 million. Saudi Arabia and Iran contributed 25% each of the initial capital contribution and Venezuela, the third largest contributor, contributed 14% (Zamora 1980). Following a revision of its charter in May 1980, the Special Fund became OFID, transitioning from a short-term facility ‘into a multilateral agency with its own legal personality’ (Porter 1986, 53) in another demonstration of the staying power of international organisations.
There are now twelve OFID member countries. Saudi Arabia is the largest shareholder with over 33% of shares, mirroring its position in the AFESD, BADEA, and IsDB. Venezuela is the second largest shareholder with 15% and Kuwait, Nigeria, and Iran round out the five major shareholders, with these five countries controlling over 76% of shares (Tok, Calleja, and El-Ghaish 2014, Zimmermann and Smith 2011). The other member countries are Algeria, Ecuador, Gabon, Indonesia, Iraq, Libya, and the UAE. Ecuador reactivated its membership in June 2014 after a 22-year absence and Qatar terminated its membership in July 2019. By December 2016, member pledged capital contributions totalled $4.4 billion of which $3.5 billion were direct contributions.
As Table 8.6 details, the OFID has been led by four Director Generals since 1976, who have been from Egypt, Nigeria, and Saudi Arabia.

8.8 International Fund for Agricultural Development

The IFAD is a specialised agency of the United Nations founded in 1977. Its mandate is to ‘mobilise additional resources to be made available on concessional terms for agricultural development in developing Member States’ (IFAD 2019). Of the MDBs outlined in this chapter, IFAD is arguably the most specialised, with its projects and programmes aimed at expanding rural food production and improving the nutrition of the poor (King 1985). As Tasie (2013, 187) notes, unlike other IFIs,
which have a broad range of objectives, the Fund has a very specific mandate to combat hunger and rural poverty in developing countries… [It] is generally accepted that the Fund has a distinct and catalytic role in improving the livelihood of both subsistence and market-oriented small-holder farmers and producers.
IFAD began operations in 1978 with $1.05 billion and led 60 country surveys in 1979 to build its initial project pipeline.
The origins of IFAD date back to the early 1970s and the sub-Sahelian food crisis (Wihtol 2014). Global food insecurity and a sudden decline in agricultural investment created extreme food shortages in developing countries, aggravated by the 1973 oil shock, which drastically curtailed agricultural production. In response, the UN General Assembly convened the November 1974 World Food Conference that, among other achievements, endorsed Resolution XIII to establish IFAD, addressing a gap in MDF other MDBs were not adequately closing (Kamau and Colaiacomo 2012, King 1985, Talbot 1980). While there ‘was little open opposition to the fund proposal,’ with the US and Soviet Union publicly supporting it, the US opposed it behind the scenes, perceived IFAD as challenging the World Bank’s role in agriculture. It changed its position after ‘it became reasonably clear that OPEC would give substantial financial support to the fund’ (Talbot 1980, 263–4). Indeed, Szasz (1999) reports there was a challenge between OECD DAC donors and OPEC states in 1974 to each put up around $600m for agricultural development in developing states and it was the desire of Arab states for a new institution where they would have more influence that was decisive in IFAD’s establishment. Kamau and Colaiacomo (2012, 473) equally contend that, while there was a financing gap, IFAD ‘was more of a political consideration than a functional one,’ with OPEC ‘preferring the establishment of an independent organization which they could control by exercising their swing vote.’ Like the Arab-led MDBs, OPEC’s support for IFAD was also in part to restore its reputation in oil-importing developing countries following the 1973 oil shock. The OFID directed $861 million from OPEC to the IFAD’s initial capital and first replenishment round.
There are 177 IFAD shareholders. Membership is open to all UN member countries and shareholders are divided into List A (primarily OECD members), List B (primarily OPEC states), and List C (developing countries). List C is sub-divided into C1 (Africa), C2 (Europe, Asia, and the Pacific), and C3 (Latin America and the Caribbean). List A members hold 49% of shares, List B 11%, and List C 39%. Each IFAD shareholder is represented on the Governing Council, with representation made up of Original Votes and Replenishment Votes. Below the Governing Council, the Executive Board has 18 representatives elected from member countries.
IFAD’s ten largest shareholders are the US (6.99%), Japan (4.18%), Germany (4.24%), the UK (3.12%), Italy (3.74%), the Netherlands (3.88%), Sweden (4.07%), France (3.1%), Canada (3.47%), and Saudi Arabia (3.14%). Collectively, they control almost 40% of shareholdings and they are some of the biggest providers of agricultural subsidies, which distort global food markets. Further, like the World Bank, those countries which benefit most from IFAD interventions are the smallest shareholders, with the majority holding less than 0.25% each. Like the IDA and other concessional financiers, but rare among stand-alone MDBs, IFAD relies on capital replenishments drawn from member states, which gives large donor countries strong influence over IFAD’s operational priorities and budgets.
The IFAD is led by a President, who serves as Chairperson of the Executive Board. Since 1977, six Presidents have led the MDB and as they have come from a diverse range of countries: Saudi Arabia, Algeria, Kuwait, Sweden, Nigeria, and Togo. The incumbent President is Gilbert Houngbo, who took office in April 2017.
Between 1978 and 2016, IFAD approved over one thousand loans and debt-relief grants totalling $17.1 billion and over 2,700 grants totalling $1.1 billion (IFAD 2016). It mobilised $10.4 billion in co-financing, with its largest multilateral co-financing partners being OFID (21.2%), AfDB (17.7%), IBRD (8.1%), and AFESD (7.4%) (IFAD 2017). Given that the Arab-led MDBs were central to establishing IFAD, it is relevant that two of its largest co-financiers are OFID and AFESD. IFAD’s main work areas include climate and environment, crops, fisheries and aquaculture, gender, indigenous people, land, livestock and rangeland, market access, nutrition, farmer organisations, rural finance, water, and youth. These priority areas iterate the IFAD’s specialised focus on rural poverty alleviation and smallholder agricultural development.
Like many MDBs, IFAD has an independent evaluations office reviewing project performance. In 2002, the office published its first Annual Report on Results and Impact of IFAD Operations. This revealed that between 2002 and 2016, IFAD’s project performance in agricultural and rural development was comparable to the World Bank but significantly better than the AfDB (70% moderately satisfactory or better compared to 48%) and AsDB (86% compared to 62%) (IFAD IEO 2018).
In summary, IFAD is a small organisation with an annual lending capacity a quarter the size of the AfDB., Yet, it plays a significant, specialised role in the agricultural and rural sectors, with a project portfolio that is far more pro-poor than the large MDBs.

8.9 New Development Bank

The Agreement on the New Development Bank was signed in July 2014 by Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa at the BRICS Fortaleza Summit. The Agreement entered into force in July 2015 and the NDB commenced operations the same month. An NDB-like organisation was first proposed by Indian Prime Minister Manmohan Singh after the 2008 GFC, as an alternative to the Global North-led MDBs. Nicholas Stern and Joseph Stiglitz – both former World Bank chief economists – played a role in its development, presenting on it when it was circulated in 2011 among BRICS countries. Stern claimed at a 2013 conference in London that their ideas were as much about ensuring BRICS governments were tied-up in partnerships with big business in order to guarantee their contractual reliability as it was to increase focus on sustainability and climate finance (see Bond 2020). The March 2012 Delhi Summit discussed the feasibility of forming a BRICS-led MDB, a discussion that led to the formal agreement to establish the NDB at the March 2013 Durban Summit. The Durban Summit Declaration (BRICS 2013) argued that the
prevailing global governance architecture is regulated by institutions which were conceived in circumstances when the international landscape in all its aspects was characterised by very different challenges and opportunities… We aim at progressively developing BRICS into a full-fledged mechanism of current and long-term coordination on a wide range of key issues on the world economy and politics.
China was initially sceptical of the utility of the proposed MDB, but agreed to it after ‘India’s proposal received support from all the other members’ (Wang 2019, 224).
NDB’s (2015) mission is to ‘mobilise resources for infrastructure and sustainable development projects in BRICS and other emerging economies and developing countries, complementing the existing efforts of multilateral and regional financial institutions for global growth and development.’ It earns its title as a specialised bank due to its focus on infrastructure and sustainable development as well as being currently limited to financing projects in BRICS countries; there appears to be little interest from other states to receive NDB project financing (Wang 2019).
The formal rationale for the NDB is like the AIIB, that emerging economies and developing countries need infrastructure investment, MDBs can finance long-term projects that private investors tend to avoid, and are able to offer technical and advisory support (Lingxiao, Yao, and Zexian 2015, Wang 2017). Infrastructure projects have always been the bread-and-butter of the MDBs, as discussed in Chapter 9, though they are often difficult terrain too. Qobo and Soko (2015, 279) link the neglect in infrastructure investments in emerging markets and developing countries to, narrowly, signs of market failure and, more broadly, historical neglect and power imbalances in the global economic system.
Beyond the formal rationale, however, the BRICS-led MDB serves other purposes. One important catalyst, as Stuenkel (2015, 108) outlines, is that the BRICS ‘have large foreign exchange reserves and the question is whether these reserves can be beneficially pooled so that more of the savings can be invested rather than hoarded.’ Thus, the NDB, particularly for China, is a means of circulating excess capital and transforming that capital into political currency. Further, Chin (2014, 370) outlines at length the respective national interests motivating the BRICS to form NDB:
  • Brazil: a source of finance for infrastructure development needs and a tool to raise the profile of Brazil’s National Development Bank.
  • Russia: a means to encourage intra-BRICS trade and investment and a geostrategic tool to connect with the Global South.
  • India: a source of finance for infrastructure, given its ineligibility to receive aid from certain donors, including the IDA.
  • China: a means of opening new avenues for its construction companies and stimulating job creation, as well as a vehicle for its global leadership, particularly in the context of its stewardship of the AIIB.
  • South Africa: a source of development finance for Africa and a tool for South African leadership in its region.
The NDB is thus both an expression of BRICS formalisation and a political instrument, particularly in the context of their limited voice in other MDBs (Reisen 2015, 302, Wang 2017, 4).
The five BRICS countries are currently the only members of the MDB. They hold an equal number of shares and voting rights and no one member has veto power, a significant departure from the World Bank’s structure. Each NDB shareholder is currently allocated 100,000 shares, with a further 500,000 shares unallocated. Authorised capital is $100 billion but subscribed capital is a modest $50 billion, with each of the BRICS contributing $10 billion, only 20% of which is paid in. Non-BRICS countries are eligible to join the NDB, although to date none have. The AIIB had 37 members in its first year of operations, but the NDB is yet to expand. If new states join, the Establishing Agreement prevents the voting power of the founding BRICS to drop below 55% of total voting power (NDB 2015), meaning they would always hold a veto provided they acted in unison, which has not always been the case (Serrano Oswald 2019). Indeed, political differences between members have increased. The prominence of the BRICS is further enshrined in the Establishing Agreement by the condition that the NDB President must be appointed on a rotational basis from one of the founding members.
KV Kamath was the NDB’s first President and he contended at a UN South-South Roundtable in 2015 that
the NDB believes that we are amongst the first development lending institutions to be set up at the initiative of the South. We would also like to believe that the setting up of the NDB, in a way, is a coming of age of the countries of the South.
(Kamath 2015)
Although a misleading statement, given the number of MDBs owned and operated by Global South countries, it reveals that NDB is not only a financial institution but a political actor focussed on the global balance of power between the North and South. Further, examining NDB’s management team highlights the embeddedness of the financial status quo. Kamath served in the AsDB’s Private Sector Department and came from a career in the Indian private banking sector and Leslie Maasdorp, NDB Vice President and Chief Financial Officer, ‘was the main privatiser of South Africa’s state assets and also worked in the local leadership of Bank of America and Barclays’; the appointment of such individuals suggests a ‘re-legitimisation of world financial order’ as opposed to a rejection of it (Bond 2013, 615). Indeed, President Kamath, although championing the NDB as a Global South MDB, said that ‘Our objective is not to challenge the existing system as it is but to improve and complement the system in our own way’ (cited in Tejas 2015). This result is not surprising given the structural implication of setting up the NDB as a traditional development bank.
President Kamath was replaced in July 2020 by an appointee from right-wing populist Brazilian President Jair Bolsonaro: Marcos Troyjo. Bolsonaro, though nationalist and conservative, started espousing liberal economic policies during his 2018 presidential campaign, which is where Troyjo fits in. He was a Deputy Economy Minister and Special Secretary for Foreign Trade and International Affairs under Bolsonaro and represented the Brazilian Government on the boards of multilateral development institutions. Prior to that he had been a diplomat and an academic economist at Columbia University. Troyjo (2017) is a critic of deglobalisation and of neostructuralism in Latin America (discussed in Chapter 4) and a fan of free trade. Indeed, he said to have played a key role in the 2019 deal between the South American MERCOSUR trading bloc and the EU, which had been under negotiation for 20 years. It seems likely he will align the NDB in neoliberal directions, with a greater focus on infrastructure and less on the environment.
The NDB approved its first projects between April and June 2016, renewable and clean energy projects to Brazil, India, China, and South Africa, which was at least in part about virtue signalling with its first loans. However, a close examination reveals NDB investments involved questionable co-financing and execution through local intermediaries. Bond (2020) outlines how the loans to South African parastatals, including the power utility Eskom and to financial intermediaries, are very problematically caught up in South Africa’s unsustainable and crony-based regime of accumulation. Further, most of these loans were issued in US dollars not South African rand, as were the bulk of NDB loans in its first five years, increasing foreign currency exposure for borrowers. Corruption and procurement fraud are also major concerns in most of the BRICS and the NDB does not appear to have adequately addressed this, but the same is true of most MDBs.
The NDB has signed partnership agreements with several MDBs, including the AfDB, AsDB, AIIB, EBRD, EIB, and World Bank, as well as national development banks: Brazil’s BNDES, the China Development Bank, and Development Bank of Southern Africa. Between 2016 and May 2020, the NDB approved 55 projects worth $16.6 billion. In 2017, its lending portfolio grew to include projects to the transportation, water management, urban development, environmental efficiency, and social infrastructure sectors (NDB 2017). In addition, it established a Project Preparation Fund in January 2017, mirroring the older MDBs in demonstrating the need for adequate advisory and technical assistance to draft sound investments, which suggest too that an adequate project pipeline and project quality will be ongoing challenges. The NDB’s strong capitalisation and prudential rules resulted in S&P’s awarding it an AA+ rating in 2018, which it has maintained since, again demonstrating it fits the standard MDB model.
In summary, alongside the China-led AIIB, the BRICS-led NDB is significant because it is the first specialised bank established in over two decades and the first new MDB in close to a decade. The creation of more MDBs either demonstrates the continued relevance of the model or the lack of imagination across the global community in development thinking. Either way, the NDB is not a rejection of the MDF status quo. As Reisen (2015, 297) argues, the NDB and AIIB, with their focus on infrastructure, ‘introduce choice for potential borrowers in terms of funding cost and modalities,’ but do not break with established norms.

8.10 Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank

The idea for the AIIB came about in 2009 when the China Centre for International Economic Exchange considered the feasibility of a China-led MDB. Chinese President Xi Jinping publicly proposed the AIIB with President of the State Council Li Keqiang in October 2013 (Shelepov 2016, Wang 2019). With support gained from Southeast Asian countries through multilateral consultations, a memorandum of understanding on establishing the AIIB was signed in October 2014 between China and twenty Asian countries (Wang 2019). Following several negotiation rounds, its Articles of Agreement were signed by fifty countries on 29 June 2015 and entered into force on 25 December (Shelepov 2016). The AIIB officially commenced operations on 16 January 2016, approving its first projects on 24 June.
As per AIIB’s Articles of Agreement, its purposes are to ‘foster sustainable economic development, create wealth and improve infrastructure connectivity in Asia by investing in infrastructure and other productive sectors’ and ‘promote regional cooperation and partnership’ in Asia and Oceania (AIIB 2015). Its functions include promoting public and private investment, financing development projects and programmes, and encouraging private co-financing. It is very similar to the NDB, with both being specialised banks investing in sustainable economic development and infrastructure (Humphrey 2015, Wang 2019, Wihtol 2014). The AIIB serves ‘China’s need to export its production overcapacity in order to arrest its domestic economic slowdown’ and is a response to China’s limited influence over major MDBs (Chan 2017, 569), after a decade or so of expanding its influence in a number of them.
The AIIB has been interpreted as a financial arm of China’s BRI (Callaghan and Hubbard 2016, Chan 2017, Hameiri and Jones 2018), which is interlinked as part of ‘a grand economic and diplomatic strategy’ to ‘promote Chinese influence’ and minimise ‘the effects of Washington’s Asia-Pacific policy’ (Cai 2018, 841). President Xi proposed the Silk Road economic belt in September 2013 while touring Kazakhstan and the 21st-century maritime Silk Road in October 2013 while in Indonesia, matching the October 2013 proposal for the AIIB (Callaghan and Hubbard 2016, Chan 2017). Pairing an MDB with a strategic initiative is not new; many SRDBs and specialised banks were established as part of formal regional arrangements. While clearly a projection of China’s geostrategic influence, the AIIB – just like NDB – is not a rejection of the MDF status quo, indeed it is very similar to the AsDB (Engel and Bazbauers 2018, Hameiri and Jones 2018). In fact, it has been said that one reason why AIIB conforms to established norms and standards – from governance structures to lending terms – is to legitimise by association the BRI (Callaghan and Hubbard 2016). Certainly, this has paid off for AIIB credit ratings; it received a AAA rating from S&Ps and equivalents from Moody’s and Fitch in 2017 and has maintained that since.
As of mid-2020, there were 45 regional member countries and 37 non-regionals in the AIIB, as well as five prospective regional members and 15 prospective non-regionals. The 45 regional members hold 76.35% of capital subscriptions and the 37 non-regionals hold 23.65%. China is the largest shareholder with 30.78%, followed by regional members India (8.65%) and Russia (6.76%). Germany (4.64%), France (3.49%), and the UK (3.16%) are the largest three non-regional members. This means that three of the BRICS countries hold the largest total capital subscriptions, with the other two – Brazil and South Africa – currently listed as prospective founding members. Thus, while AIIB is positioning itself as a broad multilateral body with a large membership, NDB’s shareholding is very small, suggesting that it is more focussed on reinforcing and localising the BRICS grouping whereas AIIB is more focussed on China-centric regional leadership (Cooper 2017, Grabel 2019, Wang 2019). Further, as Humphrey (2015, iv) argues, while
bringing in a mix of industrialised and developing countries will give the AIIB strong standing in the international arena as well as capital markets, it also means that it will face the same pressures as many existing major MDBs to impose stringent requirements on its operations.
Thus, the AIIB could be more accountable and norm-conformist than the NDB. Further, key decisions require a 75% supermajority, meaning that China could be vetoed by groupings of member countries (Hameiri and Jones 2018, Serrano Oswald 2019). However, the AIIB Board is not resident and has delegated a large degree of authority to management to approve loans (to the value of $300 million), which limits oversight and gives staff and management remarkable authority over the MDB.
Headquartered in Beijing, the influence of China over the AIIB is clear, particularly with the appointment of Jin Liqun as the MDB’s first and incumbent President. Jin was appointed by consensus at the sixth Meeting of Chief Negotiators held on 24 August 2015. A former Vice Minister of Finance in China, Jin served as a Vice President of AsDB (Gabusi 2017), further demonstrating that the AIIB is likely to conform to established MDB standards and is enmeshed in the MDB system. In a telling commentary by an anonymous high-level Chinese official, Serrano Oswald (2019, 208) records that
AIIB is not doing something new or different. It is trying to fill the gap that the existing MDBs have failed to do so. The demand in infrastructure is huge but the existing MDBs are not able to fund. AIIB is taking the best parts from the MDBs and trying to make improvements on those shortcomings… The Operational Policy on Financing was drafted by experts from ADB, EIB, EBRD, WB.
The US actively campaigned against the AIIB’s establishment, far more forcefully than it had in attempting to prevent the formation of many other MDBs, which is not surprising as the only other banks established by a hegemonic competitor – the USSR with the International Bank for Economic Cooperation (IBEC) and International Investment Bank (IIB) – were small and not organisations that the US could influence. The US claimed that the China-led MDB would ‘fail to meet environmental standards, procurement requirements, and other safeguards adopted by the World Bank’ (Reisen 2015, 302). It also claimed that it was unnecessary given the work of the AsDB. It even went so far as to pressure its allies not to join (Chin and Gallagher 2019, 246), which ended up embarrassing the Obama administration as allies including Australia, Canada, France, Germany, and the UK became members. Former US Treasury Secretary Lawrence Summers referred in April 2015 to the considerable number of countries joining the AIIB as ‘the moment the United States lost its role as the underwriter of the global economic system’ (cited in Wang 2019, 222). It appears that opposing the AIIB was less about norms and standards and more about the fact that China was leading it.
By the end of 2018, the AIIB had approved 35 projects. Fourteen projects were stand-alone financed by the AIIB and 21 were co-financed. Only $7.5 billion was invested between 2016 and 2018 though the aim was $10 to $15 billion per annum; the slow start is not surprising as the history of MDBs demonstrates that project pipelines take time to develop. Indeed, it established a Project Preparation Special Fund in June 2016 to support eligible members, particularly low-income countries, to prepare infrastructure projects through feasibility studies and advisory services. By the end of 2017, three countries had contributed $108 million to the Special Fund: China, South Korea, and the UK. The AIIB’s thematic priorities are sustainable infrastructure, cross-border connectivity, and private capital mobilisation. During its first two years, $716 million in private capital was mobilised, reflecting the financialisation agenda and indicating the capitalist banking norms of the AIIB.
In summary, like so many other MDBs, the AIIB is not just a development bank but a political actor and an arm of a broader regional arrangement – in this case, the semi-formal BRI (Wang 2017, 4). As an MDB, however, the AIIB is not a counter-hegemonic challenge to established capitalist banking or MDF norms. In form, function, and purpose, it is quite indistinguishable from other specialised and regional banks and would be unlikely to receive the media and academic attention it currently does if it were not led by China (Chin 2016, Serrano Oswald 2018). The NDB and AIIB thus ‘herald a new era’ in terms of the geopolitical influence of the Global South ‘but also provide a valuable continuity with both the post-World War II era and the more recent past’ by reflecting long-standing MDB and MDF norms (Griffith-Jones in Humphrey et al. 2015, 8).
