5.2 Conclusion

This chapter has outlined the key structural features of the World Bank, revealing it to be the standard template for an MDB. It explored voting systems, capital subscriptions, governance structures, and loan types, terms, and conditions. Overall, the clear picture is that the World Bank is a bank-like development institution, disciplined by financial markets and focussed on a development as economic growth mentality. Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli (2018, 26) argue that the MDBs are not banks because they do not take deposits and that they are not licensed or supervised, operating only according to their establishing agreements and rules agreed by their Boards of Governors and Directors. Further, as international organisations, they have important special features, including ‘preferred creditor treatment, legal ex-territoriality, immunity and so on’ (Delikanli, Dimitrov, and Agolli 2018, 22). And, yet, while the MDBs may not meet such formal definitions of banks, they are strongly governed by banking and financial norms, credit rating assessments, and global capital markets, as both of the IBRD’s official histories have noted (see Kapur, Lewis, and Webb 1997, Mason and Asher 1973) and Humphrey’s (2014, 2016, 2019) excellent recent analysis has demonstrated.

6 The regional development banks

The regional expansion of the multilateral development bank (MDB) system entrenched multilateral development finance (MDF) in the emerging global governance architecture, leading the regional development banks (RDBs) to become ‘the titans of the emerging post-war world of development’ (Culpeper 1997, 164). As Table 6.1 shows, there are five RDBs: European Investment Bank (EIB), Inter-American Development Bank (IADB), African Development Bank (AfDB), Asian Development Bank (AsDB), and European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD). They largely emerged between 1958 and 1966, with the EBRD as an outlier established in 1991. The RDBs closely resemble the IBRD in structure but differ in purpose and function, particularly their mandates on regional development, collaboration, and integration. Common among the RDBs is the pursuit of regional economic development, with support either for the growth of common markets (as in the case of the EIB) or for individual and collective economic and social development (as in the IADB, AfDB, and AsDB).
Over their histories, the RDBs have evolved greatly, to the point of straining their establishing agreements. Their memberships and capital bases have also grown significantly, and their lending operations have diversified to both address a broader range of development issues and become more interventionist through prescriptive legislative, policy, and institutional reforms. Despite two operating in the Global North and the other three in the Global South, the RDBs are similar in that their creation coincided with, and reflected, the mainstreaming of development economics and the increasing formalisation of development theory and practice. Further, they all promote regional integration (English and Mule 1996, Kappagoda 1995, Tussie 1995).
After briefly comparing the governance structures of and main challenges faced by the RDBs, this chapter provides an analytical overview of the five RDBs, presented in order of their establishment.

6.1 Governance compared

The RDBs have similar organisational and managerial structures, mirroring the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development’s (IBRD) corporate makeup almost identically. Although different titles are used, the IBRD and RDBs are comprised broadly of Boards of Governors and Directors and senior management teams led by Presidents and Vice Presidents, under which are professional staff, loan officers, economists, and other employees necessary to run large public organisations. Whereas the IBRD employs more than 10,000 people operating in over 120 offices worldwide, the EIB has 2,900 staff (up from 66 in 1958), IADB and AfDB employ 2,000 staff each (the African RDB began in 1966 with ten staff members), and AsDB and EBRD each employ over 3,000 staff members. For the EBRD, this includes non-staff contracted employees. Like the IBRD, the RDBs employ external contractors and consultants to supervise projects. Although the RDBs have fewer offices worldwide than the World Bank, they do have country offices, resident offices, and regional hubs throughout their working areas. The EIB has 23 country offices in the European Union (EU) and 27 offices in Africa, Latin America, and Asia, as well as Australia and the US. Similarly, the IADB, AfDB, and AsDB have country offices throughout their regions with the AfDB also having regional hubs in Africa, while the EBRD has resident offices in all countries of operation. Their regional offices mean they are generally better linked into local events than the World Bank (Anonymous 2019a, 2019b).
As with the IBRD, the Board of Governors is the supreme governing authority of the RDBs, with control over capital increases, appointing and dismissing members, and approving annual reports. Each RDB member is represented on the Board by a Governor, with voting power determined by weighted capital share subscriptions. As outlined in Chapter 5, this structure enables large vote holders to exercise considerable influence over the RDBs, comparable to US influence over the IBRD.
Much formal authority in the RDBs is delegated by the Board of Governors to the Board of Directors, again mirroring the IBRD and, like it, the Directors are responsible for the strategic direction of the RDBs and approving lending operations. Some have large executive boards, for example, the AfDB has 20 elected Executive Directors and the EIB has 29 Directors, one appointed by each member country and one by the European Commission; the World Bank has 26 Directors. Other RDBs have smaller executive boards. The IADB has 14 Executive Directors – one for the US and ten regional and three non-regional Directors. The AsDB has 12 Directors – eight representing regional member countries and four non-regionals. Distinguishing regional and non-regional shareholdings and Directors was an innovation introduced by the IADB.
Day-to-day operations in the RDBs are led by Presidents and Vice Presidents, just like the IBRD. Reflecting their membership, the EIB and EBRD Presidents are European and likely their selection reflects the EU’s rotation of leadership positions among member states. The IADB and AfDB Presidents have been Latin American and African respectively, reflecting their regional missions despite having members from outside the region. However, in 2020, the US against all convention proposed a US citizen as IADB President (Vigdor 2020). The outlier is the AsDB, whose President has always been Japanese, just as IBRD’s Presidents have always been US citizens. Notably, the IADB’s Executive Vice President – its second-in-command – has always been from the US, with an exceptional appointee being Nancy Birdsall during the 1990s. This highlights one of the key challenges for the RDBs: balancing shareholder influence.

6.2 Key challenges

Four of the most significant challenges faced by the RDBs are (i) establishing and retaining financial credibility in a context reliant on private international money markets, (ii) maintaining their regional character, (iii) ensuring legitimacy in a changing global environment, and (iv) balancing shareholder influence.

6.2.1 Financial realities

The RDBs fundamentally exist to provide financial assistance to borrowers that otherwise could not receive enough financing from private sources. This necessarily means that the RDBs, as with other MDBs, are constrained by the exigencies of money markets. As Humphrey (2016, 93) outlines at length, the ‘financial realities’ of global and regional money markets shape how the RDBs operate as much as shareholder influence, professional norms, and institutional structures. The MDBs, Humphrey continues, must ‘be run in a way that ensure access to capital markets,’ for potential investors routinely are less concerned with development outcomes than with ensuring adequate rates of return. As outlined in Chapter 5, credit rating agencies (CRAs) have a tremendous influence on MDBs too. Changes to credit ratings after the 2008 Global Financial Crisis (GFC) hit smaller MDBs particularly strongly and undoubtedly the AsDB and IADB’s decisions to merge the balance sheets of their concessional arms into their ordinary capital resources (OCR) in 2015 and 2016 respectively was one avenue for overcoming the impact of these changes. For the AsDB, this move tripled their equity and hence lending capacity. However, concessional arms have long faced political attack, and difficulty with replenishments was the norm since the 1990s (Ben-Artzi 2016). These sometimes came from donor states but at other times from non-concessional borrowers they argued that concessional arms too often see them providing development assistance to low-income states. This is because concessional funding comes from transferring profits from non-concessional resources.
Ben-Artzi’s (2016, 236) study of four of the five RDBs in this chapter – not including the EIB – concluded that overall they ‘function more like banks’ than development agencies, not really meeting the goals set out in their Articles of Agreement. Much of their lending is ‘strategic,’ preferencing borrowers that are unlikely to default and states with mid to high per capita incomes in their respective regions (Ben-Artzi 2016, 240). Still, the RDBs remain an appealing – and, at times, the only – avenue for borrowers because they ‘offer loans at very attractive financial terms compared to what borrowing countries could generally get themselves from other sources’ (Humphrey 2014, 612). Further, they generally offer borrowing member states somewhat more voice than the World Bank does, though that varies.

6.2.2 Regional character

The RDBs are mandated to promote regional development and they have decades of close engagement with regional borrowing countries. They promote regionalisation through common market integration (EIB), individual and collective economic and social development (IADB, AfDB, and AsDB), and economic transition to facilitate regional assimilation (EBRD). The ‘region’ is at the core of the RDBs, flowing normatively over into each MDB being instilled with a strong ‘regional character.’ The EIB and EBRD promote a European identity, the IADB sponsors a common Latin American and Caribbean community, the AfDB advocates pan-Africanism, and the AsDB markets itself as an organisation working for the people of the Asia-Pacific. The challenge for the RDBs is how to balance this regional character amidst changing memberships, mission creep as they engage with and are influenced by non-regional stakeholders, and the fluidity of their regions as social constructs.
As an illustration, the AfDB was compelled to admit non-regional member countries in the 1980s, a turn it had resisted in the 1960s and 1970s as its founders desired it to be apart from the colonial legacy that shaped the African continent over several centuries (English and Mule 1996). Yet, as a decolonising region with limited capital reserves, the ‘financial realities’ of global and regional money markets had their impact on the MDB (Humphrey 2016, 93). Its ability to provide capital to support regional economic and social growth was restricted, and its chance of acquiring a AAA credit rating were significantly boosted by having Western member states (Humphrey 2014). During its first ten years, the AfDB approved projects totalling $451 million, whereas the AsDB – established two years after the African MDB with several wealthy Global North countries – approved $3.36 billion over its first decade. After non-regional members joined, its authorised capital increased from $6.3 billion in 1983 to $22.3 billion in 1987 (Babb 2009), but this came at the cost of constraining its autonomy. In the 1990s, as the AfDB’s balance sheet deteriorated due to accumulating bad debts as borrowers entered arrears, non-regional members bailed the AfDB out on the condition that its Articles of Agreement were revised to grant non-regional stakeholders greater voice over the organisation. The US became its second largest shareholder (Coburn, Restivo, and Shandra 2015). The decision to expand the AfDB’s membership made the RDB better able to serve its region but at the cost of constraining its autonomous African identity and approach to development.
A different challenge to regional identity has been created for the AsDB by the establishment of the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB) in 2015. Japan and China demonstrate different understandings of ‘Asia,’ which Cook (2015) explained as reflecting each country’s geographical position; China is a land power with an inland capital and ‘an East-West continental understanding of Asia.’ The AIIB members have a strong representation from Central and South Asia and the Middle East, and of note there are eight Middle Eastern countries in the AIIB that are not in the AsDB, though several Middle Eastern states expressed early interest in the AsDB. Russia is a regional member in the AIIB but not a member of the AsDB, while Turkey is a regional member in the AIIB and a non-regional AsDB member. Japan, as ‘an archipelagic power on the North Pacific periphery of the Eurasian landmass, has a North-South maritime understanding of Asia’ (Cook 2015). The AsDB includes 14 Pacific Island states that were not AIIB prospective founding members, though a couple have since joined. Here we see a battle for leadership in Asia being played out in how MDBs are structured and how ‘region’ is constructed by regional powers.

6.2.3 Legitimacy

To maintain legitimacy, the RDBs have revised their approach to development assistance, introduced new agencies and affiliates, and updated their establishing agreements to respond to the changing global environment. Legitimacy is a significant factor for all MDBs, for donors must regard concessional arms as viable for replenishment, money markets must see them as creditworthy, and borrowers as preferable lenders to other funding sources and to accept their lending schedules and reform proposals. Many of the poorest and smallest countries are severely limited in pursuing alternative financing options other than MDBs, but the RDBs, as banks, need a broad borrower base and large borrowers to provide adequate profits, which fund their staff and other costs. Recent changes responding to demand and legitimacy challenges include the EIB integrating the European Investment Fund (EIF) and European Fund for Strategic Investments (EFSI) in 2000 and 2014 respectively to better engage with private investment flows and the AsDB and IADB merging their concessional loan windows with their OCR to increase equity. These expansions were likely a response to threats perceived by the creation of the AIIB and NDB but also reflect the push from the Global North to shift from grant to loan financing, the so-called ‘beyond aid’ or retroliberal agenda.

6.2.4 Shareholder influence

Shareholder influence is a perennial issue for the MDBs. They are public organisations owned by their members, but RDBs have unequal shareholder structures with the distribution of voting rights based on purchased capital shares. With inequality the norm, how do the RDBs balance the interests of both small and large shareholders? Commonly, leading shareholders exercise considerable influence over strategic directions and the appointment of presidents and for the IBRD this still means the US. While the US does not play a formal role in the EIB, it is the largest shareholder in the IADB and EBRD, the equal largest shareholder in the AsDB alongside Japan, and the second largest in the AfDB, behind Nigeria, though the AfDB does not effectively have a dominant shareholder. Further, in both the EIB and EBRD, the dominant shareholders are a group of core EU states – Germany, France, and Italy (and formerly the UK, which left the EIB due to Brexit).
The significant influence of the US is felt formally via political appointees in staffing – the IADB’s Executive Vice President has always been an American – and informally by refusing loans to countries in opposition to the US, for example, the IADB was pressured during Antonio Ortiz Mena’s presidency (1971–88) to refuse loans to Sandinista-led Nicaragua and the AsDB did not lend to Vietnam for many years following the end of the Vietnam War (Babb 2009, 29). The US served as ‘the writer, producer, and main actor of the script’ (Shaw 1991, 537) in establishing the Bretton Woods baseline, a condition maintained in most of the RDBs.
Of the seven presidents leading the EIB, only one – Philippe Maystadt of Belgium – has not been from one of the four largest shareholders. Similarly, the AsDB’s President has always been Japanese. The exception is the AfDB, with each of its nine presidents coming from nine different countries: Sudan, Tunisia, Ghana, Malawi, Zambia, Senegal, Morocco, Rwanda, and Nigeria. Block voting by the AfDB’s regional member countries – that today control over 59% voting power – has played a central role in electing its presidents. For example, Kwame Donko Fordwor of Ghana (May 1976–July 1979) was elected President with 50.66% of the vote after several months of political manoeuvrings by various African nations (Mingst 1990, 20). Further, shareholder influence emerges in project approvals and lending practices. Of 5,087 projects by the EIB between 1958 and 2016, Germany France, and Italy – the largest shareholders – were among the top four recipients, totalling 33% of all loans approved to its 28 members. Shareholder dominance impacts operations and borrower ownership; the EBRD and AsDB have little borrower influence, while the IADB has more, and the AfDB the most (Ben-Artzi 2016).

6.3 European Investment Bank

The EIB was established on 1 January 1958 by the Treaty of Rome as an organ of the European Economic Community (EEC), the latter also being founded by the same treaty. As per Article 2 of the Treaty, the EEC and EIB were created to establish ‘a common market and… to promote… a harmonious development of economic activities, a continuous and balanced expansion, an increase in stability, an accelerated raising of the standard of living and closer relations between the [member states]’ (cited in Griffith-Jones and Tyson 2013, 2). Gutner (2002, 68 and 219n80) outlined one unique feature of the EIB that distinguishes it from the other RDBs – in addition to having the lowest profile of the RDBs, ‘It prefers not to think of itself as a “development” institution,’ with Gutner repeatedly reminded in interviews with EIB staff ‘that “development” is not in the Bank’s name.’ This point of distinction was recently repeated by Gavas (2019), Co-Director of Development Cooperation in Europe, who compared the EIB with the EBRD: ‘In a nutshell, the EIB is the EU’s bank but not a development bank, while the EBRD is a development bank but not the EU’s bank.’ While avoiding development nomenclature, the EIB’s mandate includes financing projects for developing less developed regions, undertakings that cannot be entirely financed by other sources, and activities of common interest to its members. Further, it has the structure of an MDB and participates in several MDB cooperation forums and sectoral efforts like the 2016 MDB platform to coordinate on economic migration and forced displacement. In other words, it does not just have all the hallmarks of an MDB, it is one.
The EIB is the EU’s bank – its aims are to further the objectives of the EU – and it has been the world’s largest multilateral lender and borrower. While the World Bank Group committed $60 billion in 2015, the EIB Group approved €92 billion. Capital increases agreed in 2018 for the World Bank Group, however, will see its capital base expand beyond the EIB. The European RDB’s historical mandate and current lending practices detailed in Article 309 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the EU reveal influences from the IBRD and a focus on loans and guarantees from its own resources and capital markets. The EIB set precedents for later RDBs with its focus on regional development and its purposes specify that it is not-for-profit. Later RDBs followed the EIB’s commitment to cooperate with other investment programmes and funds.
Since the EIB’s establishment, it has had two main goals, first, reducing income differentials in the EEC (later EU) and, second, allocating financial resources to facilitate a functioning integrated market (Griffith-Jones and Tyson 2013). It initially only lent within the European region and its first loan was approved jointly with the IBRD in April 1959, demonstrating that the inter-organisational relationship between MDBs was established from their outset. The EIB authorised project financing outside of the EEC in 1962, slowly expanding activities to Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Still, 90% of financing is allocated to EU member countries, with the remaining 10% in 2017 divided between Asia and Latin America (3%), the Mediterranean (3%), Africa, the Caribbean, and the Pacific (2%), Eastern Europe, Southern Caucus, and Russia (1%), and members of the European Free Trade Association: Switzerland, Iceland, Norway, and Lichtenstein (1%). Non-EU developing countries do sometimes request financial assistance from the EIB, supposedly due to its ‘trusted partner’ reputation and focus on project-based lending (Anonymous 2019b) – in the crowded MDF field, the EIB’s clearly defined mandate to support the EU have resulted in limited interest. The 2019 Wise Persons Report’s investigations into the EIB found it ‘to have little or no experience working with low income countries and countries with fragilities,’ exposing weaknesses in activities outside the EU (Wieser 2019, 23).
On 5 June 2000, the EIB became the EIB Group when it became the majority shareholder of the EIF. The EIF commenced operations in 1994 as a public-private partnership to finance trans-European network infrastructure and promote small and medium enterprises (SMEs). Since 2000, the EIF has acted as EIB’s specialist arm for risk capital for SMEs, indicative of a growth in MDB focus on private sector financing. On 26 November 2014, the EIB Group grew to include the EFSI, which mobilises private capital in projects of strategic importance for the EU and was designed to help the EU’s investment gap. EFSI’s activities are supported by a €16 billion guarantee from the EU and a €5 billion from EIB capital. EIF and EFSI operations broadly parallel those of the International Finance Corporation (IFC) and Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA) in the World Bank Group.
There are 27 member countries in the EIB, with membership limited to EU members. Germany, France, and Italy each own 16.1% shareholding. Combined with Spain’s 9.7%, the top four shareholders control 58% of the RDB. In contrast, the smallest five shareholders – Luxembourg, Cyprus, Latvia, Estonia, and Malta – each control less than 0.11%. Capital shares are determined by a member’s economic weighting in the EU in terms of relative size of GDP at the time of accession. The weighting is capped, however, so that France, Germany, and Italy retain the same share level. Due to Brexit, the UK’s former 16.1% shareholding was to be sold to other member countries and it will be interesting to see how that process is managed and how relative shareholdings are altered. Brexit has restricted lending targets by 15% to mitigate risks (Anonymous 2019b) and there is speculation that mid-tier shareholders may absorb shares of the UK. Current President Werner Hoyer has noted the damage Brexit could have on the EIB’s ability to lend, creating uncertainty for an otherwise stable MDB (Giles and Pickard 2018). However, Brexit may not have a drastic impact on capital, since capital invested is small compared to callable capital and other European states should be able to take up the capital relatively easily.
Day-to-day activities are overseen by a Management Committee led by the EIB President and eight Vice Presidents. Seven Presidents have led the EIB since 1958, all of whom except one have been citizens of the four largest shareholders – see Table 6.2, which also provides an indication of key priorities and issues under each President. Incumbent President Hoyer, who assumed office in January 2012, has commented on the need for the European Commission and EIB to clearly demarcate responsibilities; ‘As long as… we do not try to become policymakers and the European Commission does not try to play bank, we are prepared for more impact’ (cited in Salinger 2018). This comment demonstrates a significant difference between the EIB and the IADB, AfDB, and AsDB: the EIB is an organ within a larger regional organisation, whereas the Global South RDBs are standalone entities. Notably, many of the sub-regional development banks (SRDBs) have similar origins to the EIB, starting with the Central American Bank for Economic Integration (CABEI) in 1960 (see Chapter 7). Given its association with the EU, Robinson (2009) argues that the EIB is not just a regional bank but an actor within multi-level governance networks enabling the EU to operate as a regional organisation.

The EIB’s investments impact EU policymaking and are said to have a ‘“catalytic effect”, leveraging greater [EU] expenditure than would otherwise occur’ (Robinson 2009, 652). This is because, in addition to financing loans, guarantees, microfinance, and equity investments, the EIB unlocks financing from other sources, including the EU budget. Further, its contribution to project costs is limited to half of the amount established during appraisal, differentiating it from the IBRD which can fund full project costs. EIB loans last between four and 20 years, with loan rates varying from project to project (Faure, Prizzon, and Rogerson 2015). Due to its confidential relationship with partners, mirroring the EBRD’s ‘merchant bank’ model, the EIB does not publish details of financing terms and conditions. This lack of transparency is concerning given that the EIB invests taxpayer funds, which is a growing challenge as MDBs engage more extensively with the private sector and is another way MDBs are contributing to financialisation.
Over €1.1 trillion has been invested by the EIB in over 11,800 projects in more than 60 countries since commencing lending operations in 1959. Between 2000 and 2017, its annual approvals more than doubled, from €41.4 billion in 2000 to €83.8 billion in 2017. Its lending peaked in 2009 – €103.9 billion – during the 2008 GFC indicating some commitments to Keynesian counter-cyclical financing, which the World Bank has only infrequently achieved despite the commitments to this in its Articles of Agreement. Of new loans in 2016, €33.7 billion were for SMEs, €19.7 billion for infrastructure, €16.9 for climate change, and €13.5 billion for innovation and skills. Historically, transportation and credit were the sectors that received the most funding, totalling just over 50% of cumulative lending. Recent EIB projects include the Intesa San Paolo Midcap Loan IV, a €300 million line of credit project to finance eligible investments promoted by mid-cap companies in Italy – such financial intermediary loans further increase concerns around loan transparency. There was also the Aeroporto Di Roma Fiumicino Sud, a €500 million transportation project increasing capacity and operational efficiency of Fiumicino Airport in Italy. These indicative examples demonstrate that the EIB is a conservative MDB favouring investment projects, with an agenda of ‘smart’ and ‘inclusive’ growth focussed on ‘building a high-value, high-employment economy with innovation in multiple fields leading to new commercial activity and employment’ (Griffith-Jones and Tyson 2013, 5).
Gutner (2002, 68) noted close to two decades ago that the EIB does not have a ‘policy voice’ like the other RDBs, for its members
have little incentive to create an institution that tells them what to do. As a result, the EIB has no separate policy research arm or country lending strategies, and it does not directly promote a particular set of its own policy ideas or innovative types of loan conditionality.
For several decades, the EIB often did ‘not even design or develop the projects it lends money to. Project promoters present project ideas to the Bank, and the Bank will evaluate projects of interest through an appraisal procedure’ (Gutner 2002, 123). In contrast, the World Bank was deeply engaged in project design from the outset, but this has led to questions about whether borrowers have ownership of their projects. The EIB changed during the 2000s and 2010s with the establishment of several advisory and trust fund facilities supporting private sector investment, public-private partnerships, and pre-investment studies. The EIB Group also coordinates the EIB Institute, an in-house teaching and learning institute that funds research, partners with academia, runs seminars and conferences, and functions as a social and cultural hub. It is comparable to the IBRD’s Economic Development Institute (EDI) and World Bank Institute (WBI), demonstrating again organisational convergence at play in the MDB system, and the way that MDBs aim to educate or socialise people around its world view.
In summary, the EIB is a very large bank annually approving cumulative loan amounts far exceeding the other RDBs combined. Its large capital base lends support to Humphrey’s (2014) argument that raising the capital base is easier for MDBs that have shareholders as borrowers, than for those where the major stakeholders are non-borrowers. Members as borrowers also seem to have ensured limited conditionality and more borrower ownership of projects. While mirroring the MDB template of the IBRD, it has become as much a financial investor in European SMEs, infrastructure, and climate projects as a development bank, though it is not the only MDB that operates along this model and the World Bank’s Maximising Finance for Development (MFD) agenda seems to take inspiration from the EIB. Still, it funds technical assistance to get regional cooperation projects going and for projects in Europe’s less developed regions, so there it operates more like a standard development bank, mobilising finance for public goods and where the private sector under-invests (Anonymous 2019b). The EIB’s organisational priorities are clear: financing loans that balance and stabilise the EU’s internal market and are of common interest to EU members. Griffith-Jones and Tyson (2013, 17) list the ‘critical enablers’ of the EIB as a successful MDB as: its AAA rating paired with a non-profit rationale enabling long-dated, low-cost, and large-scale financing, coupled with its intertwined position within the EU enabling support for the high-level strategy for European integration. It is the model of what other regional integration-based MDBs wanted to become, though in most cases the regional integration projects have collapsed.

6.4 Inter-American Development Bank

The background history of the IADB is the product of complicated efforts to foster Latin American regionalism dating back to the late 1800s (Barria and Roper 2004), as discussed in Chapter 2. There was a further push to create a body for the region from 1939 which again proved unsuccessful, in part because the proposed body was far broader than just a development bank and because the US withdrew its support. The Organisation of American States (OAS), founded in April 1948, organised the 1957 Economic Conference of OAS in Buenos Aires, Argentina, which (again) proposed that the Inter-American Economic and Social Council form a specialised committee to investigate the feasibility of an inter-American financial organisation. The US supported the proposal in 1958, in part because of Vice President Richard Nixon’s hostile reception on a tour of Latin America and of the increasing success of Cuban revolutionaries (Barria and Roper 2004, 623, Tussie 1995, 18–19). Interestingly, one other trigger for the IADB’s formation was as a ‘counterweight’ to the IBRD’s monopolistic position as a ‘market player rather than a market maker or facilitator’ (Phillips 2009, 17). Even investors on Wall Street were concerned by the unrivalled presence of the World Bank. Further, countries in the region ‘felt that the World Bank was not paying enough attention to Latin America, was uninterested in regional integration projects, and was too concerned with past defaults’ (Krasner 1981, 305). To illustrate, the IBRD approved $170.9 million to Latin America and the Caribbean in 1957 and 1958, while Asia and the Middle East received $516.9 million. IBRD loans of the time to Latin America also focussed on electric power and transportation, not domestic industry and social projects as were wanted by countries from the region (Humphrey 2016, 98).
Many Latin American and Caribbean countries during the 1950s felt their region lacked suitable investment to meet development financing needs. The IADB emerged from talks favouring the state-led development model of Raúl Prebisch and the UN Economic Commission for Latin America (UN-ECLA). The IADB was seen as a crucial link in resource transfers to overcome market failures, weak private sectors, and insufficient capital investment (Tussie 1995). That the US accepted these arguments confirms Rapley’s (2002, 1) position that, ‘[i]n the early post-war period, development thought like conventional economic wisdom, was really neither left nor right. There existed a broad consensus that economies need more state intervention than they have been given in the past.’ The IADB was finally established in 1959, when the OAS approved its Articles of Agreement, which is quite like the IBRD’s.
The IADB was mandated to foster economic and social development in the developing member countries of the region by promoting both individual country growth and collective regional integration. The concept of a ‘Latin American and Caribbean region’ is relatively less contested and more geographically determined compared to the far more contentious and fluidly constructed ‘Asian’ or ‘Asia-Pacific’ region (Bull and Bøås 2003, 250). The US suggested including a concessional lending arm in the establishing agreement – the Fund for Special Operations (FSO) – as a parallel to the International Development Association (IDA), which was only established in 1960. Further, the establishing agreement ‘permitted more flexibility than the World Bank to make loans directly to private industry without requiring a government guarantee, an important demand of Latin American countries intent on developing their domestic industrial base’ (Humphrey 2016, 99), a territory the World Bank was, in fact, not willing to traverse. Still, in its early years, it actually lent mostly to sovereign states for housing, education, and sanitation (Ben-Artzi 2016). Technical assistance has an explicit place in the IADB’s functions, which was not the case in the earlier MDBs.
There are now 48 member countries in the IADB: 26 regional developing member countries, 20 non-regional member countries, and the US and Canada. As per its Articles, each member country holds 135 votes plus one for each share of ordinary capital stock held. The US is the largest shareholder with 30% voting power (enabling it to veto FSO decisions, though not ordinary capital loans), while regional developing countries collectively hold over 50% voting power. However, the US exercises considerable influence due to the RDB’s headquarters being in Washington, DC, its Executive Vice President always being an American citizen (Babb 2009, 27–28), and it employs a ‘large percentage of American citizens’ (Ben-Artzi 2016, 92). If the US does not support a proposal, it is unlikely to get to the voting stage (Ben-Artzi 2016).
The Board has 14 members and they meet weekly and are fairly active (Ben-Artzi 2016). This may be part of the way that it keeps it distinct regional characteristics. Regional states as collective majority shareholders perceive themselves as decisive in decisions, leaders in development finance and donor coordination, and the IADB President has evolved into a ‘regional statesperson’ (Nelson 2000, 407). Of the regional member states, Argentina and Brazil dominate with close to 11.4% of voting power each, followed by Mexico with 7.3%. Most remaining regional member countries own less than half a per cent each. Although not greatly influential during its early decades, the IADB – just like the AfDB and AsDB – has become a powerful regional actor (Bull and Bøås 2003).
Four Presidents have led the IADB (see Table 6.3). Elected for five-year terms and able to be re-elected, the President is supported by an Executive Vice President and three Vice Presidents: for Countries, for Sectors and Knowledge, and for Finance and Administration. The President has always been chosen from a regional member country, unlike the IBRD and AsDB’s appointment of American and Japanese leaders. In practice, the US, Argentina, and Brazil have informally picked the presidents (Lopes 2019). Reflecting the World Bank’s MFD agenda, incumbent President Luis Alberto Moreno argued in 2017 that the IADB must become ‘bigger’ in its annual financial approvals and more flexible to respond to its heterogeneous region. A new president is scheduled to be elected in September 2020 and in June the US broke protocol by nominating an American, Mauricio Claver-Carone, a Trump Administration appointee in the National Security Council and ‘anti-Castro warrior’ who seems have little expertise on the region outside Cuba (Waldron 2020). But the US has Brazilian backing so may yet prevail.

The World Bank Group, AfDB, and AsDB all have concessional lending facilities created after their establishment and operating as separate legal entities. The IADB’s soft loan window, the FSO, was built in and began operations when the Bank was founded in 1959, making it the first concessional lending body of the MDBs (Babb 2009, 27, Tussie 1995, 5). The purpose of the FSO is rather vague: ‘the making of loans on terms and conditions appropriate for dealing with special circumstances arising in specific countries or with respect to specific projects’ (IaDB 1996, Article IV, Section 1). The IDA is clearer in providing concessional terms to less developed member states but both organisations probably took the idea of replenishments from SUNFED (see Chapter 3). The Articles allow for financing FSO operations from the income derived from IADB operations, though it has only made minimal allocations over the years (Humphrey 2014). This is because borrowing states oppose the reallocation as a transfer from some borrowers to others. Transfers to the IDA are not in the IBRD Articles; they occur but have been opposed by many IBRD borrowers. This is a case of ideas flowing from an RDB to the World Bank rather than the reverse as generally highlighted in this chapter. In 2016, the IADB agreed to effectively close the FSO, transferring its resources to ordinary capital, though it did create a new blended loan instrument that five member countries have access to. This followed a similar AsDB decision in 2015 and, as noted earlier, is part of a donor push to limit grants, expand loans, and achieve harder financing terms for Global South countries.
Like the World Bank and EIB, there is an IADB Group, which comprises the IADB, Inter-American Investment Corporation (IIC), and Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF). The IIC commenced work in 1989, though the establishing agreement became effective in March 1986. It finances private sector investment projects without government guarantees, and so it is comparable to the World Bank’s IFC. The IIC approved 162 projects totalling $2.24 billion in 2016, with sectoral approvals divided into financial markets (41.3%), energy (31.2%), science and technology (7.5%), transport (6.9%), agriculture and rural development (3.9%), and other (9.2%). In ‘other,’ the IIC mostly funds projects supporting private sector financing partnerships, securitisation, trusts, and development banks. This growth in IFC-equivalents funding financial intermediaries is a contemporary trend in the MDBs. The MIF was launched in 1993 to provide technical assistance for private sector growth. It approved $86 million in 2016, with an active portfolio of over 500 projects supporting micro and small private business development.
The IADB was a marginal development financier up until the 1980s under President Antonio Ortiz Mena. The vast increase in petrodollars after the 1973 and 1979 oil price shocks saw large commercial loan flows into the region, made possible by the removal of restrictions on finance ministers to offer government guarantees on commercial loans (Scheman 1997, 86). However, the 1982 debt crisis ended private capital flows to the region, and public lending was supported by structural adjustment. Compelled by a changing political climate and the impact of US President Ronald Reagan’s neoliberal turn, the IADB moved away from social sector lending and became ‘closely identified with the World Bank’s economic liberalisation and integration agenda’ (Nelson 2000, 407). Robin Broad, as an international economist working on the World Bank and IADB at the US Treasury Department, ‘was struck by the certainty of political appointees at Treasury about the unquestioned correctness of [structural adjustment] policies’ (Broad and Cavanagh 2009, 7). This situation was heightened by the close proximity of the IBRD and IADB headquarters in Washington, DC (Culpeper 1997, 115), and the centrality of the World Bank and IADB to the Baker Plan for debt relief proposed in 1985 (Babb 2009, 128). Further, Tussie (1995, 96) notes that President Iglesias (1988–2005) ‘warned against “counterproductive dichotomies” and promoted a pragmatic convergence of ideas’ under the Washington Consensus. During the 1980s, the IADB’s annual lending increased to help with its role in debt and averaged $2.3 billion (the IBRD’s average per annum was $12.3 billion). A large capital increase in 1988, likely a reward for its support in implementing structural adjustment, further enhanced lending capacity. The 1988 increase was overseen by newly elected President Enrique Valentín Iglesias García and was $26 billion, followed by an additional $40 billion in 1994, meaning its capital base reached $101 billion (Scheman 1997, 87). Further, like the IBRD, it has been consistently building up its reserves, another key determinant of capacity to borrow on financial markets.
Thus, the IADB’s fortunes changed in the 1990s. Lending dramatically increased in 1991 and since 1994 it has been Latin America and the Caribbean’s main source of development financing, only recently rivalled by the Development Bank of Latin America (CAF) (see Chapter 7). From 1994 to 2008, the IADB approved over $108.6 billion in loans to the region, totalling 50% of all multilateral lending. Notably too, from 1998 to 2009, the IADB was able to loan at a slightly lower interest rate than the IBRD, despite not having the backing of as many Northern states (Humphrey 2014). This may have been because of growing competition from a range of private and public financiers; on the public side, Chinese and Brazilian development finance increased substantially in the region and the CAF became more of a competitor.
Like the IBRD, the IADB sought a capital increase after the 2008 GFC, but it obtained quite a bit less than it sought, with the US and Europeans reportedly opposed to higher contributions, while borrowers sought a greater increase. As with the World Bank, the reluctance to increase paid-in capital has resulted in pressure to increase earnings to build up reserves, as a substitute for capital. The transfer of FSO resources to OCR also increased its capitalisation. As of the end of 2018, IADB’s subscribed capital reached $176.8 billion, while its paid-in capital was $11.9 billion. With many wealthy non-borrowing countries reinforcing its AAA credit rating, the IADB is comparable to the IBRD ‘in relation to capitalisation ratios and the use of reserves to build equity as opposed to paid-in capital’ (Humphrey 2014, 634).
Between 1961 and 2017, IADB OCR and FSO approvals totalled $266.8 billion, with FSO loans making up just $20 billion. The four largest recipients of OCR loans were Brazil (21%), Mexico (16%), Argentina (16%), and Colombia (9%). Approvals per annum increased significantly during the GFC, with $5.3 billion approved in 2000 while $15.5 billion was approved in 2009 (IADB 2010). In 1999, they started using country groupings to organise loans. Originally, they had four, but it is now two, with ‘Group I’ comprising the higher per capita income states that get 65% of loans, while ‘Group II’ gets 35%. In 2017, approvals included 90 sovereign-guaranteed loans totalling $11.4 billion, divided into 73 investment projects ($7.9 billion) and 17 policy-based loans ($3.4 billion) (IADB 2017). The 90 sovereign-guaranteed loans focussed on infrastructure and the environment (51%), institutions for development (38%), the social sector (8%), and trade and regional integration (2%). Thus, its portfolio appears to have matched the general shift back to infrastructure that has been happening across the sector.
Beyond financial assistance, the IADB Group manages several advisory facilities for project preparation. These facilities include the Project Preparation Facility that provides up to $1.5 million in project preparation financing, an Infrastructure Fund that assists public, private, and mixed entities develop and prepare infrastructure projects, and the Regional Infrastructure Integration Fund that provides technical assistance for the preparation of strategic integration projects. Project preparation has long been a challenging issue for the World Bank (Bazbauers 2018), and the number of trust funds on this topic demonstrates that IADB faces the same challenge.
In summary, the IADB was the first Global South MDB, whereas the EIB emerged from the Global North. While the US is the leading shareholder controlling nearly 20% voting power more than the next largest shareholder, the RDB has legitimacy in the region and helped set groundwork for structures and institutional approaches adopted by the AfDB and AsDB.

6.5 African Development Bank

The AfDB is Africa’s leading development finance organisation. As per its Articles of Agreement, its mandate is to ‘contribute to the sustainable economic development and social progress of its regional members individually and jointly’ (AfDB 2016) and it follows the purposes and functions model established by the IBRD, although notably it does not mention guarantees only direct financing. Like the IADB, it promotes regional integration and individual country growth. It pursues its mandate through project and programme lending, resource mobilisation, technical assistance, and private investment promotion in Africa and has become a beacon of pan-Africanism (Babb 2009, 30).
The RDB’s origins can be traced to a report published in 1957 by the Council of Europe Study Group for the Development of Africa, which recommended the formation of a Bank for African Development. The Tubman-Nkrumah-Touré Conference of 1958, named for the then Presidents of Liberia, Ghana, and Guinea, held in Saniquelle, Liberia, announced a commitment to building bodies for regional integration and solidarity. These proclamations took place amid the decolonisation of most of the African continent during the mid- to late 1950s and 1960s; it was a point of significance to establish an ‘African bank’ independent from former colonial powers. Robert Kwaku Gardiner, the former Ghanaian Minister of Economic Planning, commented that the AfDB ‘is one of a number of continental institutions that African governments have set up to provide assistance to indigenous organs to enable member countries to free themselves from the grip of colonial dependence’ (cited in Fordwor 1981, ix).
After several conferences in 1960, including the All-African Peoples Conference and the Second Conference of Independent African States, the UN Economic Commission for Africa (UN-ECA) – at the request of African nations – coordinated studies for an African development bank. The in-principle decision to create such a bank was made in March 1962. Concurrently, the Committee of Nine – a thinktank made up of financial experts and economists from Cameroon, Ethiopia, Guinea, Liberia, Nigeria, Sudan, Tanganyika, and Zanzibar – engaged with African and non-African states to propose recommendations on its financial and administrative structure (Ajomo 1976). Thus, despite the early involvement of the Council of Europe, the AfDB was largely an African initiative. The Agreement Establishing the AfDB was signed on 14 August 1963 in Khartoum, Sudan, after two years of consultation and preparation; it became effective on 10 September 1964. The first Board of Governors meeting was held in November 1964 and it commenced operations on 1 July 1966.
There are 80 member countries in the AfDB: 54 regional and 26 non-regional shareholders. The regional shareholders include every recognised African mainland and island state, whereas the non-regionals are far more disparate, including the US and wealthy European, Asian, Middle Eastern, and Latin American countries. Regional members control roughly 59% of voting power, while non-regionals control 41%. As of December 2019, regional voting power was led by Nigeria (9.4%), Egypt (5.7%), South Africa (5.1%), and Algeria (4.3%). Non-regional voting power was led by the US (6.7%), Japan (5.6%), Germany (4.2%), and Canada (3.9%). China is also a member, but with only 1.2% voting power. Unlike in the IADB, the US is only the second largest shareholder, behind Nigeria. To become an AfDB member, non-regionals must first become members of the Bank’s concessional arm: the African Development Fund (AfDF). Compared to the World Bank and other RDBs, AfDB voting power is more diffuse, meaning that no one country can effectively dominate formal decision-making.
The AfDB has always advocated a regional ‘African identity,’ much more so than the IADB or AsDB. Up until the 1950s, except for Liberia, Ethiopia, and Egypt, the African continent was either under European colonial rule or, as in South Africa, living under apartheid. Its recent history of oppression led the fledgling organisation to initially restrict membership to independent African states, prohibiting non-regional entrants. At the outset, it was proposed
that the Board of Governors, the Board of Directors, the President, and the top management of the Bank would be wholly African. The memory of colonial domination was too recent, and for many too bitter, to countenance new ties to the North.
(English and Mule 1996, 20)
This approach made the AfDB unique. Its ‘African identity,’ however, was tempered during the 1960s and 1970s by a split between francophone African states and other African countries, with the smaller francophone states determined to maintain their diplomatic relationship with France, while mitigating the influence of larger members like Nigeria (English and Mule 1996, 20). While an exemplar of pan-Africanism, ideological and strategic differences between African leaders and countries translated into emerging tensions: ‘Beneath the alleged unity of the [Organisation of African Unity] were acute ideological differences between African countries’ (Biney 2019, 182).
The AfDB’s governance structure has ‘produced a particularly active role for the Board of Directors. With no one country claiming the right to nominate the President, his or her re-election depends to a large extent on satisfying as many’ Directors as possible (English and Mule 1996, 2). Given the diffusion of voting power between AfDB’s members, presidential elections have been contentious, with competing regional states alleging ‘favouritism’ and efforts to undermine consensus leadership (English and Mule 1996, 22–24). AfDB’s governance has thus tended to shift between consensus decision-making and political bargaining by coalitions, far more so than the other RDBs (Shaw 1991, 542). There is an informal understanding the Presidents will rotate between the three broad linguistic and geographical groupings of the continent: Arabic, Anglophone, and Francophone (Ben-Artzi 2016). There have been nine Presidents to date, all from regional member states and Table 6.4 outlines their priorities and achievements. The incumbent President is Akinwumi Ayodeji Adesina of Nigeria, who assumed duties in September 2015.

Paralleling the expansion of the World Bank, within its first decade the AfDB grew to become a Group through the launch of the AfDF and Nigeria Trust Fund (NTF). The AfDF is AfDB’s concessional lending arm. It was agreed to in 1972 and established by President Abdelwahad Labidi in November 1974. Between 1974 and 2014, it invested $45 billion using finances replenished every three years by donor countries. It arose because AfDB loans at commercial interest rates (though lower than most states could access on their own) requiring on-time repayment were not viable for a number of African nations and many of the poorest African members did not qualify for OCR loans (Coburn, Restivo, and Shandra 2015, 277). In its functions and operations, it was copying the trend set by the IDA and FSO. The NTF was built in 1976 by an agreement between the AfDB and Nigeria to provide concessional financing to eligible low-income members. It began operations with $80 million and that increased to $253 million by 2012, prioritising agricultural loans. The AfDF and NTF were useful to the AfDB, increasing financial approvals during its early decades when it was strongly constrained by its limited capital reserves and many of its member countries did not qualify for non-concessional financing.
By the late 1970s, in the wake of the oil price shocks, the constraints of AfDB’s limited capital base became too great and it began efforts to invite non-regional countries to become members (Culpeper 1997, 31, Mingst 1990, 13). Its initial authorised capital stock was only $370 million and annual approvals averaged $21 million in the early 1970s, compared to the IADB approving an annual average of $685 million and AsDB approving $272 million (English and Mule 1996, 10). As Babb (2009, 30) remarks, the ‘AfDB’s members came to the conclusion that it was better to sacrifice some measure of autonomy in order to build a bigger and more influential bank.’ To build the AfDB’s reputation and role as a leading regional lender, its Board of Governors approved in-principle the admission of non-regional members in 1977 and amended its Articles in 1982. Non-regional members were first admitted in 1987 and this saw AfDB’s authorised capital increase from $6.3 billion in 1983 to $22.3 billion in 1987. AfDB and AfDF loan approvals consequently increased, from $1.7 billion approved between 1979 and 1981 to $8.8 billion between 1991 and 1993 (Babb 2009, 31). As its credit rating rallied, the AfDB began to invest in and borrow from international capital markets. This pragmatic move by the Governors demonstrates the financial power of the Global North and its finance markets, and the limited capacity of a development banking model to challenge it. Indeed, a neo-Gramscian analysis says it shows the disciplinary financial power of the Global North.
Like the AsDB, in its early decades the African RDB mostly lent for agriculture and infrastructure. It reluctantly introduced structural adjustment loans in the 1980s after pressure imposed by newly admitted non-regional members, specifically the US (Pandolfelli and Shandra 2013, 340). The AfDB was concerned with being ‘IMF-ised’ and rather than attach policy conditionalities to loans, it preferred to inform potential borrowers that failure to implement reforms would make future loan approvals more difficult (Mingst 1990, 74). Nonetheless, lending increased significantly from 1985 and close to half of loan approvals were policy-based in 1987, up from negligible levels in preceding years and they resembled IMF and World Bank structural adjustment packages. A lack of in-house expertise in adjustment lending led the AfDB to follow the Bretton Woods siblings, along with pressure by the US, to coordinate more closely with the IBRD (Pandolfelli and Shandra 2013, 341–2).
In the 1990s, the AfDB fell into difficult times due to accumulating bad debts as its borrowers entered arrears and regional economic stagnation limited new loans (Coburn, Restivo, and Shandra 2015, 278). Lending declined from 1993 and the Bank’s recently acquired AAA credit rating was downgraded to AA+. Grant and subsidised loans from non-regional members prevented default on AfDB loans (Bulow and Rogoff 2005), on the condition that the AfDB commit more resources to structural adjustment lending, alter voting shares to increase non-regional voting power from 23% to 40%, and modify voting rules to require a 70% majority on decisions, granting non-regional states an effective veto (Babb 2009, 31). This forcefully demonstrates how Global South initiatives can be overwhelmed by Northern financial discipline. Further, it was long regarded as having problems with inadequate country offices, lack of technical expertise, in particular project preparation, and inefficiency and corruption (Ben-Artzi 2016).
Following an attempted coup d’état in Côte d’Ivoire in 2002 and a rapid deterioration in the country’s stability, the AfDB’s headquarters were relocated from Abidjan to Tunis, Tunisia, where it remained between 2003 and 2014, before returning to its original host country. Interestingly, this transition period coincided with a rapid improvement in the strength of the AfDB as an MDB. It resumed operations within just three months of moving ‘over a thousand personnel and several tons of vital documents’ (Akin-Olugbade 2003, 738) and managed to exceed the previous few years annual lending commitments, restore its AAA credit rating not held since 1995, and launch its first $1 billion global bond (Akin-Olugbade 2003, 738). Though, rather than the move, the Bank’s revived fortunes need to be understood in relation to the global commodities boom and its positive impact on African growth as well as growing Chinese investment in the continent and organisational reforms initiated by President Omar Kabbaj (1995–2005) and continued by Donald Kaberuka (2005–15).
The AfDB returned from an uncertain future to become in the 2000s and 2010s Africa’s leading financial institution. Under incumbent President Akinwumi Adesina, who assumed office in September 2015, the African RDB has pursued the High 5s agenda, outlining five operational priorities: Light Up and Power Africa, Feed Africa, Industrialise Africa, Integrate Africa, and Improve the Quality of Life for the People of Africa (AfDB 2018). Aligned with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), the Higher 5s agenda promotes green growth, infrastructural development, and regional integration, underpinned by the central message that African development requires greater private sector mobilisation, which the AfDB argues it is positioned to catalyse. Note the emergence of a strong emphasis on the private sector for MDB investments, while the infrastructure agenda can be linked to Chinese influence on the subsequent re-focussing of the World Bank and other MDBs.
Like the World Bank, AfDB Group assistance is divided into three categories of eligibility depending on income levels: AfDB-eligible only, AfDF-eligible only, and a blend of AfDB and AfDF resources. Morocco, Tunisia, Nigeria, South Africa, and Egypt have historically been the largest recipients of AfDB project approvals, receiving 55% of all loans and grants. Cumulative Group lending highlights that it is still a small RDB approving $121 billion between 1967 and 2016. In contrast, the IADB committed $267 billion from 1961 to 2017. The African RDB approved 5,279 projects between 1967 and 2016, which means the average loan size has also been quite small at just over $50 million. The IBRD’s average loan size was $100 million up until the mid-1990s and their size involved a ‘trade-off between the quantity and quality of lending’ (Ranis 1997, 86). For the AfDB, given the politics around its lending programme, the small size of loans is unlikely to indicate a greater focus on quality. It does reflect the Bank’s small capital base, the relatively small economies of many borrowers, and the history of default by some states.
Like the IBRD and IADB, the AfDB Group offers a range of technical and advisory services and has developed several trust funds, such as the South-South Cooperation Trust Fund, which is a technical assistance initiative the AfDB and Brazil signed in March 2011 to build partnerships for knowledge exchange. It launched the African Development Institute (AfDI) in 1973 to enhance lending operations. Through knowledge exchange, the AfDI builds policy dialogue and development management in Africa. In 2010, the AfDI was reoriented to become the AfDB’s capacity building point-of-contact, delivering face-to-face and e-learning courses and seminars, research, and capacity and strategic cooperation seminars. It is broadly equivalent to the World Bank’s WBI.
In summary, the AfDB was the first Global South-controlled MDB, but because of several factors – including regional member financial situations, global economic changes, and isomorphic pressures – it had to embrace more of the global MDB order and resultantly became more influenced by the Global North, directly – in the case of the US – and indirectly – in the case of broader MDF approaches. Yet, the AfDB has grown from a minor and uncertain MDB to become Africa’s leading multilateral financial organisation, setting standards on regional development both practically and intellectually.

6.6 Asian Development Bank

As the IADB and AfDB were founded, calls came from Asia-Pacific countries – including Japan, Thailand, Sri Lanka, and India – that an Asian RDB should be formed to transfer financial resources and support regional cooperation and integration (Nakao 2017). There was growing dismay from Asia-Pacific countries that the World Bank’s share of lending to the region was inadequate to meet its needs and that its procedures were cumbersome. Japan in particular was concerned about the IBRD’s political dominance by the US and Europe and that this curtailed the capacity of the region to effectively provide a voice in the institution (Kellerman 2019). Japan was interested in establishing an Asian RDB in 1962, as it viewed states in the Asia-Pacific as a vital source of raw material, food, and markets for exports and foreign investment (Kappagoda 1995, 14); an MDB was a more neutral channel for investment for a country that had only two decades before attempted to occupy much of the region through force. While the US originally opposed the AsDB’s formation, it came to support it – as usual Cold War geopolitics overcame its concern that an Asian RDB would undermine its influence in the region, much like the IADB (Kappagoda 1995, 14). Deepening US involvement in the Vietnam War led to the position that an economically strong Asia-Pacific would be better able to resist perceived Soviet and Chinese expansionism (Nakao 2017).
In March 1963, the UN Economic Commission for Asia and the Far East (UN-ECAFE) proposed a resolution to establish an Asia-Pacific development bank. The Agreement Establishing the AsDB, referred to as its Charter, sets its purpose as fostering ‘economic growth and co-operation in the region of Asia and the Far East… and to contribute to the acceleration of the process of economic development of the developing member countries in the region, collectively and individually’ (AsDB 1967). The AsDB mirrored earlier MDBs closely from its inception, with the first President Takeshi Watanabe attending meetings held by the IBRD and IADB ‘to gather information about how they were run’ before the Asian RDB began operations (McCawley 2012, 51). The AsDB opened its doors on 19 December 1966 with 19 regional and 12 non-regional members, a significant difference from the AfDB’s initial regional-only membership, and its initial focus – given the region’s agricultural orientation – was food production and rural development (McCawley 2012, 75), though it quickly turned to infrastructure too.
The ‘most obvious factor distinguishing’ the AsDB from other RDBs ‘is its strong Japanese influence’ (Babb 2009, 29). It’s history and governance structure are interwoven with Japan’s organisational and regional leadership, from the appointment of Japanese nationals to positions of importance to Japan’s initial financial contributions (Rathus 2008). From senior management to voting capacity, Japan is the AsDB’s most influential regional member. In fact, studies have argued that all AsDB projects ‘require Japanese support; otherwise, they do not even proceed to the Board of Directors for approval’ (Lim and Vreeland 2013, 44). However, the US has equal shareholdings and Ben-Artzi (2016) noted the strong tensions between the two, in particular over visions for economic development.
There are 67 member countries in the AsDB: 48 regional shareholders and 19 non-regional. Regional members control over 65% voting power and non-regionals close to 35%. As of 2019, Japan and the US are the equal largest shareholders, with 15.6% voting power each, which is an increase from 2015 when they both had around 12.7%. This power structure is unique with two hegemons and little borrower influence (Ben-Artzi 2016). China only joined the AsDB in 1986 but by 2016 was its third largest borrower behind India and Indonesia. China also has the third largest voting share, which jumped from 5.5% in 2016 to 6.4% in 2019, with India a close fourth at 6.3%. Both China and India are active in AsDB governance (O’Keeffe, Pryke, and Wurf 2017), which is a change from the traditional deficit in borrower influence. With China’s establishment of the AIIB and the One Belt, One Road initiative (Jakupec and Kelly 2015, Kajimoto 2017), the Japan-led AsDB may struggle to maintain its key role in the region.
Like the IBRD and other RDBs, daily operations are managed by the AsDB President and six Vice Presidents, overseeing a permanent staff of over 3,000 personnel. There have been ten Presidents, all of whom have been Japanese, paralleling US control over the World Bank. Table 6.5 highlights their tenures, priorities, and challenges. Prior to the appointment of Masatsugu Asakawa in January 2020, President Takehiko Nakao led the AsDB from 2013 to 2020 and emphasised the importance of climate change, infrastructural growth, and private investment to the future of the Asian RDB (Anonymous 2019a).

The AsDB is supported by the AsDF. It was founded in 1974 and is AsDB’s concessional lending arm and the largest of the AsDB’s Special Funds. During the AsDB’s first decade, numerous Special Funds delivered concessional funding; President Watanabe described their multitude as a ‘nightmare,’ creating unnecessary overlap and confusion (McCawley 2012, 10). The AsDF reduced the overlap, becoming the focal delivery point for concessional lending. During the upheavals of the 1973 and 1979 oil price shocks, many low-income countries accessed AsDF loans instead of AsDB OCR. AsDF operations were at first denominated in US dollars but after 1982 were linked to Special Drawing Rights (SDRs) to improve currency stability. Between 1973 and 2016, the AsDF was replenished 11 times to a value of $31.7 billion, but, as with the IDA, each replenishment led to disputes between leading donors over its activities (Erquiaga 2016, 10).
Grant lending was introduced in 2005 and the AsDF became a grant-only affiliate in 2017 because, in May 2015, the Governors approved an initiative to combine AsDB OCR with AsDF concessional resources, merging the balance sheet of both. OCR equity tripled from $18 billion to $53 billion; the consolidation was proposed to increase annual financing capacity to $20 billion by 2020 with co-financing meant to double that (O’Keeffe, Pryke, and Wurf 2017). In other words, this was a way for the Bank to increase its capital base without having to negotiate the politics of a capital increase with key shareholders. It was also likely a response to the establishment of the AIIB and the World Bank’s MFD agenda. AsDF-eligible countries continue to receive concessional assistance, even though operations are approved through AsDB OCR.
The AsDB began as a conservative project lender with a strong focus on agriculture and its lending grew quite slowly until the 1990s. It later diversified into infrastructure so much that it was sometimes referred to as the ‘Asian Dams and Bridges’ bank. By the late 1970s, it had introduced a diverse range of lending products including policy-based lending in 1978. The 1979 oil shock expedited these changes, as policymakers in developing Asian countries became intensely concerned about food and energy security, and AsDF concessional approvals grew. Besides project loans, the AsDB began to prove programme loans (quick-disbursing loans designed to increase productive capacity in high-priority areas), multi-project loans (financial products that draw together projects considered too small for normal operations), and sector loans (projects financing capital requirements in specific sectors) (McCawley 2012, 126). It started lending to the private sector in 1983 but this lending remained small for decades (Ben-Artzi 2016). The IBRD, IADB, AfDB, and AsDB followed a similar path in introducing lending instruments, demonstrating isomorphic alignment.
During its early years, the AsDB’s comparative advantages over the larger World Bank were its strong networks in the region, greater flexibility, and a less bureaucratic approach to lending but, as Bank staff acknowledged in interviews, this has reduced over time. These networks meant they had a focus on sub-regional integration initiatives like the Greater Mekong Subregion, which are part of its strategy to 2030. The AsDB was a little less hostile to the state than the World Bank (see Chapter 4), though some analysts have questioned whether they in fact offered a unique model (Komori 2016).
In the early 1990s, the AsDB undertook an internal review like the World Bank’s Wapenhans review, prompted by concerns over project quality. As with the World Bank, the review found that there was an ‘approval culture’ (cited in Komori 2016, 30). The report and pressure from the US led to it become a broader MDB, funding social sector projects. The 1997 Asian Financial Crisis also impacted the AsDB greatly, with much of the optimism surrounding the ‘East Asian Miracle’ evaporating (Bello 1999). Significantly, the Crisis saw the AsDB move beyond project and programme lending to reflect more an IMF-style role as an emergency liquidity provider, although the AsDB addressed more social sector support while the IMF’s principal focus was balance-of-payments stability (McCawley 2012, 219). Following the Asian Crisis, and in part because of critiques of its approach to the crisis, the AsDB became the first RDB to have a Board-approved governance policy ensuring its portfolio benefits the poor. In November 1999, it adopted poverty alleviation as its main mission, reassessing Asia’s development path following exposed vulnerability. Notably, this change preceded the World Bank, which introduced its policy on 31 December 1999. Still, as Sims (2015) argues, its poverty alleviation objectives tend to be constrained by its neoliberal agenda.
AsDB’s poverty-alleviation mission saw it support the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) unveiled in 2000, alongside the IADB and AfDB. In 2001, its lending dramatically increased and continued to increase across the rest of the decade (Ben-Artzi 2016). The poverty focus shaped its response to the 2003 outbreak of severe acute respiratory syndrome when it focussed on developing health systems and to increased natural disasters in the region such as the December 2004 Asian tsunami. The Bank also dramatically increased its lending to the private sector, going from 1% of lending in 2001 to 16% in 2015 (O’Keeffe, Pryke, and Wurf 2017, 63), with plans to get to one third of lending by 2024 with $2.50 in co-financing for every dollar invested (AsDB 2018). With the AsDB supporting the innovative financing agenda, following the trend of the World Bank’s MFD, relations with the US have improved (O’Keeffe, Pryke, and Wurf 2017).
Historically, most of the AsDB Group’s lending came from its OCR, while AsDF projects made up a third a total lending. Between 2008 and 2017, OCR annual approvals averaged $11.35 billion, while AsDF approvals averaged $2.93 billion. Illustrating its growth trajectory, the AsDB approved $3.4 billion between 1967 and 1976 and $140.3 billion between 2007 and 2016. Of $17.46 billion total approvals in 2016, the sectoral division of activities was energy (26%), transport (22%), public sector management (13%), finance (11%), urban infrastructure and services (9%), industry and trade (6%), agriculture, natural resources, and rural development (6%), education (5%), health (1%), and communications (0.3%) (AsDB 2016). It no longer lends much for agriculture but a lot for energy and transport and a significant amount of policy-based lending for public sector reform. The largest recipients of AsDB Group finance in 2016 were India (17%), China (12%), and Azerbaijan and Indonesia (10%). India and China have also been large borrowers in the World Bank, which is not surprising since their development challenges and number of people living in poverty are still large. Despite that, the US has been pushing for China to graduate to developed country status (and thus stop borrowing) from the AsDB and World Bank.
Like the World Bank and most RDBs, the AsDB maintains a technical assistance portfolio, although relative funding declined significantly for it in the mid-1990s (O’Keeffe, Pryke, and Wurf 2017). As with the IADB and AfDB, advisory services are mandated in its Charter. Its technical assistance has been consolidated into ‘transaction technical assistance’ and ‘knowledge and support technical assistance.’ The former covers services directly benefitting projects financed by the AsDB, while the latter includes other advisory services, such as general institutional capacity building and policy advice. It also coordinates several technical assistance grants and funds. Finally, like the IBRD and AfDB, it manages a training institute: the AsDB Institute (AsDBI). Established in 1996, it conducts research and delivers face-to-face and online training to mid- and senior-level country officials. Its work in the knowledge terrain is not regarded internally or externally as being of the quality found at the World Bank (O’Keeffe, Pryke, and Wurf 2017). But, still, it has a fairly strong commitment to the production of developmental ‘common sense’ and in 2018 it produced 7,102 knowledge outputs (Sims 2020).
In summary, the AsDB has similar organisational structures and operational mandates to the IBRD, IADB, and AfDB. It has always been led by a Japanese President, just as IBRD’s President has always been an American. Shareholder power in the AsDB is divided between Japan as the leading regional member and the US as the leading non-regional member, like Nigeria and the US in the AfDB, but Chinese influence has grown. Its region has gone from one of the poorest in the world when the MDB was formed to one of the fastest growing; inequality and pockets of persistent poverty remain key challenges. One question is whether it has kept up with the times – up to the 1990s, it was regarded as close to its members, very flexible, and the most state-friendly MDB, but this reputation waned after the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis. It now faces competition from the China-led AIIB (see Chapter 8), which challenges its regional construction of ‘Asia’ and its development of more complex loan procedures reflecting institutional isomorphism. Still the symbiosis between MDBs reduces competition, with the AsDB having signed memoranda of understanding with both the AIIB and NDB and already co-financing projects with the AIIB.

6.7 European Bank for Reconstruction and Development

The EBRD was proposed to facilitate the post-Soviet transition of Central and Eastern Europe following the end of the Cold War. As per the Agreement Establishing the EBRD, signed in Paris, France, on 29 May 1990, its mandate is to ‘foster the transition towards open market-oriented economies and to promote private and entrepreneurial initiative in the Central and Eastern European countries committed to and applying the principles of multiparty democracy, pluralism and market economics’ (EBRD 2013). The EBRD is unique among the RDBs in that, first, it was created decades after the other regional banks and, second, its mandate focusses explicitly on private sector development and democratic reform, while most other MDBs specifically exclude any interference in the political affairs of member countries (Korfker 2013, 86). Indeed, its purposes and functions have nothing of the Keynesian tone of the IBRD and other RDBs. Rather, the language around ‘structural and sectoral economic reforms, including demonopolisation, decentralisation, and privatisation’ (EBRD 2013) speaks to the Washington Consensus agenda. Nevertheless, its establishment at the height of this era signifies that the ‘public sector still had an investment role in transitional economies’ (Phillips 2009, 9).
Unlike most other RDBs, it does not have a concessional lending arm and its Articles limit the amount it can lend to states (Ben-Artzi 2016). As Babb (2009, 31) said, ‘because it lends heavily to private investors, in many respects it looks more like the World Bank’s IFC, or even a private bank, than it does a development bank.’ But many SRDBs, especially the trade and development banks, look similar and the larger MDBs have been expanding their non-sovereign operations. The EBRD’s Establishing Agreement entered into force on 28 March 1991 and it began operations on 15 April. Between 1991 and 2017, it approved over 5,300 projects totalling €290 billion.
French President François Mitterrand proposed the establishment of a post-Soviet transition bank in 1989, which was endorsed by the European Council in December 1989 as it was clear that the Soviet bloc was in the throes of collapse. After a meeting between OECD member countries and representatives from Malta, Cyprus, Central and Eastern European countries, the EEC, and EIB in January 1990, negotiations for the EBRD concluded in May 1990, ‘barely four months after the initial meeting – perhaps the speediest creation of a new multilateral institution in history’ (Culpeper 1997, 29). The rapid turnaround from proposal to establishment is explained by the speed of the Soviet bloc’s collapse and the capacity of the states backing the proposal.
There were initial disputes over whether a new European RDB was necessary, with the US claiming that the World Bank and IFC already encompassed the EBRD’s mandate. Further, some Western European states claimed that the EIB could be expanded to focus on post-Soviet transition, rather than establish an entirely new MDB (Gutner 2002, 59–60). Calls to expand the EIB were immediately shut down by the EIB itself, which ‘was distinctly uninterested in accepting responsibility to lead a Western aid initiative’ in Central and Eastern Europe (Gutner 2002, 60). In the end, it was the uniqueness of the EBRD’s mandate – the interplay between economic liberalisation and the promotion of democratic polities – that facilitated the creation of the new RDB. However, its operational overlap with commercial banks has led to a new set of critics who continue to question its need for existence. Further, the EBRD promoted ‘big bang’ privatisation and liberalisation in Central and Eastern Europe, which was largely a failure. Output declined rapidly while the number of people living in poverty increased. Privatisation often resulted in the enrichment of a new authoritarian oligarchy. Thus, it is not surprising that the EBRD was often mired in scandal (see Table 6.6) and came under critical scrutiny (Ben-Artzi 2016).
The EBRD has 66 shareholder countries from five continents plus the EU and EIB. The US joined and took the largest capital subscription of 10% while France, Germany, Italy, the UK, and Japan each have 8.52%, totalling close to 53% of votes. The EIB and EU own 3% each. No other RDB has a regional organisation or RDB as a shareholder, however many of the SRDBs do, which often serve as important sources of capital and security.
In the EBRD’s case, the EU and EIB are important capital sources; the EU has contributed 36% of all grant financing since the EBRD’s establishment and it aims to align the EBRD’s operational priorities with EU policy – a distinctive disciplinary approach to development. The EBRD’s Board is very large, which Strand (2003) attributes to its political mandate resulting in many state’s wanting their voices heard. It has more donors than borrowers on its Board; borrowers have almost no influence and the major shareholders have even more power than their shareholdings suggest (Strand 2003). The 2019 Wise Persons Report concluded that the EBRD is ‘generally responsive to EU policies,’ though member states do not always coordinate because of their different interests and this could increase after Brexit (Wieser 2019, 22).
The EBRD President serves as chief of staff and legal representative, just like the other RDBs. Six Presidents have led it, all European nationals. Table 6.6 provides highlights of each presidency and demonstrates that the EBRD has had ongoing challenges with scandals, poor lending practices, and profligacy. While other RDBs have had frequent challenges with project quality, the number of scandals and level of profligacy seems more in keeping with poorly run state banks in some developing countries or a few of the SRDBs than it does an RDB. The most recent President was Sir Suma Chakrabarti, who was born in India but is a British national; he stepped down in July 2020 and, due to COVID-19, there has been a delay in appointing his successor. Upon joining the EBRD, President Chakrabarti emphasised two priorities: first, the economic situation in Eastern Europe and, second, supporting the Southern and Eastern Mediterranean after the Arab Spring (EBRD 2012). EBRD staffing is more dominated by bankers than the other RDBs, with few development experts, and thus ‘bankers and bureaucrats carry out the EBRD’s political goals’ (Ben-Artzi 2016, 93).

EBRD finance is divided into three categories: loans, equity investments, and guarantees to promote trade. Its loans have a maturity period of one to 15 years, no grace period, although project-specific grace periods may be incorporated, and are offered at either fixed or floated interest rates (Faure, Prizzon, and Rogerson 2015). Thus, its standard terms are much harder than the other RDBs, especially given the absence of a concessional lending arm. Loan repayments are normally in equal, semi-annual instalments. Annual investments averaged $9.1 billion between 2010 and 2017. Loan disbursements steadily grew since its establishment, with a jump in loans from around 2006; Russia has been its major borrower (Ben-Artzi 2016). Since commencing lending operations, its main sectoral focusses have been financial institutions (€44 billion cumulative 1991 to 2007), industry, commerce, and agribusiness (€33.5 billion), infrastructure (€22.3 billion), and energy (€19.6 billion). Its interest rates are similar or only slightly lower than commercial banks, though maturities are generally longer (Ben-Artzi 2016). These foci are reflective of its commercial bank persona and are similar to the IFC, with the 2019 Wise Persons Report stating that the EBRD’s ‘expertise is strong in relation to the development of the private sector, even if it does not have comprehensive sector coverage and is weak in, especially, social sectors such as health care and education’ (Wieser 2019, 22). Lending to financial intermediaries has been coming under increasing scrutiny in recent years due to a lack of transparency and environmental impact. Its private sector projects generally range from €4 million to €218 million, but average investments are a relatively small at €22 million. In contrast to the IADB, AfDB, and AsDB, the EBRD is far more aligned to project-based lending and equity investments, revealing again it is more like a commercial bank than a traditional RDB.
EBRD lending is supported by technical assistance, divided into policy reform dialogue services and advisory services. Policy reform dialogue services focus on coordinating the strategies, policies, and related initiatives that support the EBRD’s transition mandate and advisory services help SMEs access business advice just like the technical assistance portfolio of the IFC. The EBRD also sponsors the Central European Initiative (CEI), a regional forum founded in 1989 comprised of 18 member countries mainly from Central and Eastern Europe that promotes collaboration and consultation.
In summary, reflecting its persona as a commercial bank with a development agenda, the EBRD is less transparent than the other RDBs. It also has several unique features vis-à-vis other MDBs. First, it does not provide concessional finance and focusses on private investment and, second, it promotes a political agenda by encouraging democratic reform. Despite being established much later than the other RDBs, its founding is reminiscent of the other regional banks, in the sense that the AfDB responded to the integration and development of post-colonial Africa. The major difference is that the EBRD emerged at the height of the Washington Consensus, which it presents as ‘common sense,’ in contrast to the Keynesian/New Deal tone of the earlier RDBs. Thus, the EBRD aims ‘to offer structural coherence in post-communist transition’ (Shields 2015, 180).
