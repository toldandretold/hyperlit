9 Infrastructure

Infrastructure is a broad sector. The World Bank (1994) says the sector incorporates power, telecommunications, water supply, sanitation and sewerage, solid waste, roads, railways, ports, airports, and major dams and canals. It is undoubtedly vital to development. Roads give people access not just to markets but to education and health care, water and sanitation are central to extending longevity, and energy and telecommunications are human rights. Infrastructure was the original focus of the multilateral development banks (MDBs) and retroliberalism has seen it return to the centre of the development agenda, aided by Chinese interests, international investors, and extravagant estimates of the value of infrastructure needs proposed by the MDBs. The Brookings Institute claimed that from 2016 to 2030 $90 trillion will be needed for infrastructure and the World Bank has adopted this estimate (Ahmad and Alawode 2017). In Asia, over the same period, the Asian Development Bank (AsDB) said that developing countries will need $26 trillion (Ahmad and Alawode 2017).
Infrastructure investment is written into the DNA of the MDBs. Only three of the 30 MDBs operating today do not make it a priority – the Council of Europe Development Bank (CEB), International Bank for Economic Cooperation (IBEC), and International Fund for Agricultural Development (IFAD). Infrastructure was a key focus during the 1950s and 1960s, although allocations declined in the crisis years of the 1970s as the focus on basic needs increased and declined further in the 1980s due to the hostility of the Washington Consensus to state investment. Attention returned from the late 1990s and today it is at the centre of development practice. Engen and Prizzon’s (2018, 41) study of 22 MDBs found that for most of them ‘infrastructure is the largest sector’ and that 12 of them ‘allocate more than 50% of their disbursements to infrastructure development,’ with transportation and energy being the two largest sectors.
Neoliberalism’s focus on limiting the size of governments has not disappeared and, as a consequence, the MDBs now define infrastructure as a problem arising from a lack of private finance (Ray 2015). Hence, the MDBs shifted from privatisation to public-private partnerships (PPPs) to de-risking infrastructure as an asset class, which is argued to multiply billions in investment into trillions (Gabor 2018). This has resulted in the MDBs doubling down in their efforts to develop innovative new financial instruments that expand private sector engagement in infrastructure to every corner of government operation. Still, private capital is hesitant to engage with the poorest states and in a range of sectors. Further, as infrastructure, it involves a high level of risk. There are environmental, social, political, and economic risks and a long history of poor planning and construction, particularly for large-scale, complex projects, which are back in fashion.
This chapter begins by explaining why infrastructure lending is a core business of the MDBs and equally why it is a risk-laden sector. The second section of the chapter outlines the history of MDB engagement with infrastructure and the third section explores the eruption of complex financial instruments in the context of rapidly expanding global debt. All in all, it is hard not to worry that the current boom in infrastructure investment may end with a bang.

9.1 Infrastructure: a core but high-risk business

Infrastructure lending was well suited to both the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development’s (IBRD) structure and its early staffers concept of the Bank. The IBRD, like most MDBs, predominantly lends to nation states and its founding documents stipulated that loans must be mostly for specific projects and must have been evaluated and recommended by a ‘competent committee.’ Further, most MDBs have clauses in their founding agreements that they can only loan funds where they cannot be procured on market terms; in other words, they are meant to generate additionality for borrowers (Ryan-Collins and Spratt 2013). In addition, as Chapter 5 outlined, the MDBs are reliant on finance markets and credit rating agencies and so they need projects that bankers find acceptable. Infrastructure meets this brief perfectly; it involves specific projects that produce, on paper at least, the rates of return that finance markets want to see. Projects need to be substantial enough to warrant a good deal of technical expertise and study and still produce a scale of loan that is useful for a multilateral bank with a lending imperative; historically, the average World Bank loan size was around $100 million (Ranis 1997, 86). Moreover, the scale, risk, and timeframes of infrastructure investments mean that, all too often, private capital is not willing to lend, which is exactly why the MDBs were created. And, lastly, from the perspective of bankers, economists, and the IBRD’s neoclassical growth model, infrastructure is the key driver of private sector growth.
Still, the scale of infrastructure projects means that they are often high-risk investments. As Flyvbjerg, Garbuio, and Lovallo (2009, 171–2) note,
across the globe, large infrastructure projects almost invariably arrive late, over-budget and, and fail to perform up to expectations. Cost overruns and benefits shortfalls of 50 percent are common; cost overruns above 100 percent are not uncommon.
Given that the MDBs are not meant to fund commercially viable projects that can be financed by private capital, they are likely to be funding the riskiest infrastructure projects, though it is not always the case that they do. Delving deeper into the source of risk highlights that large infrastructure projects have long lead times, a high degree of complexity and sometimes a lack of clarity, technology is often uncertain, and geology, climate, and other issues create unforeseen and unbudgeted obstacles (Flyvbjerg 2009). In developing countries following the Second World War, there was an acute shortage of skilled personnel, especially engineers, and, as a consequence, complex infrastructure projects created an over-dependence on foreign skills and dependent development (Mold 2012, Sogge, Biekart, and Saxby 1996).
Infrastructure demand forecasts also tend to be dramatically wrong (Flyvbjerg 2009). Flyvbjerg, Garbuio, and Lovallo (2009, 172) argue that problematic forecasts do not just arise from delusions or genuine mistakes and bad luck but that ‘deception’ and the ‘strategic manipulation of information’ also play a role. This is because there are vested interests involved: politicians and policymakers who want to be seen leading projects of benefit to their communities, contractors and developers who stand to gain multimillion dollar contracts, not to mention the agendas of landowners, lawyers, financiers, and a host of others. For contractors, once they have won a contract, they can often renegotiate it to reflect ‘unanticipated’ costs and governments too often are left with all the risk and the private sector with the gains (Alston 2018).
All of this can influence the planning process and not everyone has the same level of information about the likely costs and benefits of proposals. In New Institutional Economics terms, there is asymmetric information. The long-term time horizon can serve to dampen risk concerns, such as for the politicians who leave office before a project is completed. The multiple parties involved mean it is often difficult to hold any one person or group to account for failures or cost overruns (Flyvbjerg, Garbuio, and Lovallo 2009). When investments are funded by MDBs, that adds yet another set of interests to be accounted for.
Projects in the Global South tend to have higher levels of corruption than those in the North. This meant that the infrastructure boom of the 1950s to 1970s produced many so-called ‘white elephants’ – status projects like highways to nowhere, dams that quickly silted up, poor quality roads that became impassable, and so on. The World Bank and other MDBs only finally admitted that corruption was an issue in the late 1990s but even then, they only focussed on corruption by government officials. They paid insufficient attention to reducing corruption in large-scale infrastructure projects and the (often Northern) ‘corporations who supply the bribes’ (Hostetler 2011, 235). The standard MDB refrain to such critiques is that was in the past, things have changed now, but recent examples are easy enough to find, for example, the International Finance Corporation (IFC) was implicated in a corruption scandal impacting a 2019 Colombian highway project (Goodman 2019).
MDB infrastructure projects have often negatively affected communities, including: ‘loss of livelihoods; spread of communicable diseases; expansion of the production and trafficking of illicit goods; community conflicts; loss of life through dam collapses and construction accidents; and forced displacement and resettlement’ (Sims 2020, 153). This happens despite the safeguard policies that most large MDBs have established because they are difficult to enforce. Further, gender policies tend not to be considered in the planning and implementation of World Bank infrastructure projects (Ferguson and Harman 2015). When infrastructure projects are privatised, the poor are unlikely to benefit because the private providers are interested in reducing costs and charging high user fees, which the poor can rarely afford, and thus they sometimes end up excluded from services (Alston 2018). In the case of the expensive and grossly under-funded area of sanitation, the World Bank – rather than help subsidise latrine construction costs – chose to use active shaming of the poor to trigger personal latrine construction. This approach is widely supported because its anti-subsidy model fits with neoliberalism’s hostility to the state, but the programmes infringe human rights and mimic derogatory colonial programmes (Engel and Susilo 2014), while deliberate shaming harms the psychosocial well-being of the poor (Bateman and Engel 2018).
Large projects may offer sizeable economic benefits, but their environmental costs can be equally substantial. The Polonoroeste highway project outlined in Chapter 10 and the resultant mass deforestation of the Amazon is one of the better known examples (Rich 1994). Sims (2020, 153) offers this summary of the environmental impacts of recently funded MDB infrastructure investments: ‘clearance of protected forest lands; increased pollutive outputs of expanding industries; species loss through habitat fragmentation, poaching, and vehicle-wildlife collisions; deterioration of riverine ecosystems including fisheries loss through hydropower development.’ These concerns do not just relate to the World Bank but to the AsDB, Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB), and other MDBs.
Maintenance of infrastructure has also been a huge challenge often exacerbated by infrastructure inappropriate for the economic or environmental conditions of a region (Mold 2012). The World Bank acknowledged the lack of attention to this challenge in documents published in the 1980s (Mold 2012). In the Pacific Islands, this situation is still so endemic that the Pacific Regional Infrastructure Facility (2013) contends that donors operate a build-neglect-rebuild paradigm, which is a reference to the PPP strategy of build-operate-transfer.

9.2 The evolution of MDB lending in infrastructure

Infrastructure did not just suit the structure of the MDBs, it was a core part of their initial Keynesian, welfare, modernisation model. Modernisation and Growth Theory both situated expanding capital formation as key to accelerating economic growth. Paul Rosenstein-Rodan’s (1961) argument that capital produces increasing returns to scale thanks to the benefits of scale that accrue from development and that a ‘big push’ in infrastructure investment is needed to achieve this was particularly influential (Mold 2012). This was the World Bank’s goal between the 1950s and 1970s. In the 1950s, infrastructure lending was 63% of total IBRD lending commitments and rose to a peak of 81% in the 1960s (World Bank 1994). The Bank at this time established the model that infrastructure and other projects must be tendered out to the market. Only on rare occasions does the Bank allow the use of a force account; that is, allowing governments to do the construction work themselves. This model was meant to ensure the most efficient use of Bank funds. Bank lending for infrastructure remained at a high 63% in the 1970s, even as it became interested in basic needs. Official flows were overrun by private finance in the 1970s, particularly re-invested petrodollars. These were the infrastructure boom decades, but they ended in a bust with project failures and poor maintenance on the one hand and general crisis in many developing countries on the other hand, caused by rising oil costs, decreasing prices for primary commodities, and general economic malaise.
Infrastructure investment declined rapidly with the rise of the Washington Consensus; structural adjustment lending came to total 43% of World Bank lending in the 1980s (World Bank 1994). This is not surprising, given that a key part of the neoliberal critique of statist development was that it focussed too much on the formation of physical capital, at the expense of ‘human capital’ (Toye 1993). Equally, neoliberalism in the 1980s was focussed on privatising state assets rather than constructing new ones, starting with those that operated in market sectors and then extending into natural monopolies or public utilities such as telecommunications, electricity, gas, water, and even sewerage, though this rarely proved profitable enough for privatisation.
By the late 1980s, some of the social and environmental costs of World Bank-funded infrastructure projects were becoming public, with civil society movements highlighting failures. This was another reason that infrastructure investments declined further in the early 1990s to just under 34% of lending commitments. The Bank responded with the development of a range of safeguard policies and – as outlined in Chapter 4 – other MDBs followed suit. Not all MDBs abandoned infrastructure, however. It remained a key focus for the Islamic Development Bank (IsDB); the Islamic MDB regards the sector as a key strength. IsDB Group (2016, 19) claims to prioritise more than ‘most peer MDBs’ and remarks that almost 80% of financial allocations ‘are in infrastructure sectors – energy, water, transport, and telecommunication.’ Infrastructure is a safe space for an MDB reliant on fossil fuel rents and working with conservative religious regimes (Engel and Bazbauers 2020). In Latin America, the Development Bank of Latin America (CAF) started to turn away from short-term credit for trade towards infrastructure from the early 1990s; from this time, it doubled its infrastructure commitments roughly every five years (CAF 2000, 2005). The CAF has consistently supported regional development with a focus on transport and energy projects.
The AsDB never abandoned infrastructure either. It is one of the most ‘traditional’ MDBs in that it lends as much as 80% of its funds for infrastructure (Park 2015, 456). Indeed, it has been known in the region as ‘Asian Dams and Bridges.’ It only created an accountability mechanism in 1995 largely thanks to US pressure and it managed to escape detailed scrutiny of the negative environmental and social impacts of most of its projects until 2000 when civil society groups made a major push for safeguard policies. In the late 1990s, the AsDB acted as a broker for regional initiatives in Southeast Asia with a strong focus on infrastructure, including the Greater Mekong Sub-Region (GMS) initiative. As Sims (2020, 149) argues, this is one of the ways that the AsDB ‘has sought to establish normative views on development’ and it represents ‘transnational (primarily infrastructure) connectivity as constitutive of development.’ Still, these projects resulted in negative attention for the Asian MDB thanks to their environmental and livelihood costs (Sims 2015).
By the late 1980s and into the 1990s, the market was simply not supplying major infrastructure projects, as neoliberals had said it would. This is because, as Mold (2012, 246) comments at length, in
many developing countries, the size of the market was too small to justify large-scale private participation in sectors such as the energy sector, and technological advances to provide a ‘reasonable’ minimum efficient scale of production were not forthcoming either. As a result, private investment in crucial sectors such as was rarely forthcoming on the anticipated scale.
With the 1980s debt crisis, infrastructure investment was often one of the first areas sacrificed by the state. Thus, investment fell below what was needed even to sustain existing infrastructure in some parts of the world, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa.
But, in the late 1990s, the tide started to turn for infrastructure. The AsDB’s vocal support helped, though China’s strong practical interest in and backing for the sector was even more important. In Latin America, neo-structuralism refocussed interest on infrastructure. There were quite a few regional infrastructure initiatives around the world and a number of these were centred on the MDBs, though some were initiatives of regional powers with leadership aspirations (Palestini 2016). Examples include the African Union’s 2001 New Partnership for African Development and its associated Infrastructure Project Facility managed by the African Development Bank (AfDB) as well as the 2000 Initiative for the Integration of Regional Infrastructure of South America, proposed by Brazil but coordinated by the Inter-American Development Bank (IADB) and CAF with FONPLATA Development Bank and the Central American Bank for Economic Integration (CABEI) later involved (Palestini 2016). In addition, the AsDB and ASEAN established the ASEAN Infrastructure Fund (AIF) in 2012 to address infrastructure needs by mobilising regional savings and, of course, the AIIB was launched in 2015 to improve infrastructure connectivity in Asia.
Chinese development finance was a major driver of the infrastructure push in the 2000s. This was almost certainly a way of helping domestic Chinese firms deploy their surplus capacity. The largest Chinese public development banks are the China Development Bank and China Export-Import Bank (CHEXIM). By 2015, these two banks lent more in Latin America than the combined funds of the World Bank, IADB, and CAF (Palestini 2016) and they were major supporters of infrastructure in Africa too. The China Development Bank is the larger of the two and it mainly provides non-concessional loans for infrastructure and energy projects, while CHEXIM loans are concessional. CHEXIM and the IADB established a joint investment platform in 2012 focussing ‘on infrastructure, mining, and energy projects’ (Palestini 2016, 26). The Chinese interest in infrastructure also reflects their competitiveness in the sector, which has translated into winning contracts to implement MDB loans. In the early 1990s, firms from the US, Germany, Japan, France, and the UK won 50.5% of World Bank derivative procurement work and 96.8% of the AsDB and – with Italy – 96.5% of the IADB, although only 25% of AfDB Group contracts (Bracking 2009, 117). By 2002, however, China and India topped the list of suppliers for civil works contracts and China was also the number one supplier in goods contracts (Bracking 2009, 123).
In the 2000s, infrastructure became a cornerstone of the emerging retroliberal development agenda. The first step was viewing public investment in infrastructure as increasing the productivity of private investment, meaning the two were deemed complementary (Mold 2012). Next, plans for how infrastructure might be financialised by becoming an asset class made it a key part of the World Bank’s Maximising Finance for Development (MFD) agenda. The rapid and multifaceted innovation in infrastructure contracting and financialisation is outlined in the next section of this chapter.
Interestingly, however, World Bank infrastructure lending commitments have not skyrocketed, though they have gone up. In the second half of the 2000s, infrastructure increased to 38% of lending commitments and in the second half of the 2010s they reached just over 40% (World Bank 2010, 2019). The CAF continued to expand its focus on infrastructure, with its cumulative loan portfolio as of March 2015 ranking transport in first place with 36% of approvals, followed by the energy sector in second place with 28% (CAF 2015). In the IsDB, the infrastructure sector accounted for 59% of OCR flows in 2018, a slight decline from 2010 to 2014 when it was 60.6% (Aid Flows 2019). Notably, the IsDB’s 2018 Annual Report emphasised the importance of infrastructure contracts using neoliberal frameworks promoted by the World Bank like PPPs (IsDB 2018). Similarly, the Black Sea Trade and Development Bank (BSTDB), while largely focussing on private sector and trade lending, is shifting towards lending to governments for infrastructure as part of the 2019–22 Strategy (BSTDB 2019).
In Asia, the AsDB highlighted the region’s infrastructure needs with its 2009 flagship report: Infrastructure for a Seamless Asia. The report estimates a large infrastructure gap and provides the standard case that closing this would improve efficiency and market access (O’Keeffe, Pryke, and Wurf 2017). It also notes that infrastructure financing ‘is complicated because national governments are reluctant to finance infrastructure investment through foreign borrowing, while most of Asia’s domestic savings are not at the disposal of national governments’ (O’Keeffe, Pryke, and Wurf 2017, 36). Perhaps unwittingly, then, they provided part of the justification for China to create a new MDB focussed on infrastructure. Further, infrastructure is a priority of the AsDB’s Strategy 2030 and it has taken a leaf out of the MFD playbook with a strong focus on leveraging bank resources to expand financing (AsDB 2018). On PPPs, the Asian MDB has even developed an operational framework to expand their use (Abon and Chiplunkar 2013). The AsDB has also devoted considerable amounts of technical assistance to infrastructure projects.
The AIIB, with its infrastructure-focussed mandate, has targeted a lending programme of $10–$15 billion annually, however it only reached $3.3 billion in loan commitments in 2018 and $4.5 billion in 2019. Most MDBs have taken some time to build their portfolio and, as outlined in Chapter 3, developing a project pipeline is difficult. Further, the AIIB focusses on the complex realm of infrastructure, making the situation more difficult. To achieve its current level of commitments, it has pursued a fair amount of co-financing and funding for financial intermediaries has increased significantly – more on this below.
Infrastructure is likely to remain a priority for the MDBs for the foreseeable future. It is a key focus of the MDBs and Chinese development finance, and the COVID-19 pandemic has led to the fast-tracking of infrastructure investments. There is legitimacy to the concern that the proliferation of bilateral and multilateral development finance programmes is causing inefficiencies and resource overlap. But the rapid build-up of infrastructure financing and the increasing financialisation of the sector are the reasons we ask, as Mold (2012) did, whether the infrastructure boom is again going to end in tears. To answer this question, a look at the contractual and financial innovations changing the sector is vital.

9.3 Financial innovation in infrastructure: the journey from competitive tendering to de-risking infrastructure as an asset class

The World Bank’s Articles of Agreement have a range of clauses about the effective use of funds, but they did not originally specify international competitive bidding (ICB) for procurement. This was introduced in 1951 and, according to Mason and Asher (1973, 187), was ‘one of the Bank’s proudest moments.’ ICB is not without its challenges, however. There are questions around the weighting for domestic procurement, the specifications for goods and services, the engagement of consultants in appraisal work that are also interested in providing inputs at the project stage, and more. In terms of consultants, today they frequently circumvent the Bank’s prohibition by creating different consortiums for the appraisal and implementation stages of projects. Indeed, this is one reason why some of the supposed benefits of ICB have been overstated. Further, in major projects, there are generally only a handful of firms able to undertake the work, thus the bidding is often not as competitive as is made out. As noted above, this has resulted in a handful of Global North countries being the major beneficiaries of World Bank and other MDB work, with China and India now joining that group. In Africa, outside of the telecommunications sector, almost no African firms win major infrastructure projects (Mold 2012).
In the 1980s, the attention of the MDBs turned to corporatisation and privatisation of existing infrastructure projects. This often involved splitting firms or utilities into their component parts and privatising the most profitable parts, while corporatising the remainder with the view that one day it too would be privatised. In the case of electricity, for example, this means splitting services into three parts: generation, distribution, and supply. First, independent power providers are introduced into generation as a lead into privatising this component. Next, supply is privatised. Then, lastly, distribution is privatised as it is hard to make the poles and wires profitable on their own (Bayliss and Hall 2002, Yi-chong 2005). Still, it takes a lot of regulation and monitoring to create ‘markets’ in electricity as it is still largely a natural monopoly and the privatised sector has seen many scandals and market failures (Beder 2003). One illustration of the way that private capital is still being inserted into electricity markets is a 2018 project financed by the AIIB and IsDB supporting an independent gas power producer in Bhola, Bangladesh. The project was unsolicited and approved by the government without any open bidding, with the MDBs nevertheless providing finance; it demonstrates how the ideology of privatisation and neoliberalism take precedence over basic competitive practices. The project has also been subject to very critical reports by the NGO Forum on the AsDB for its impacts on the local environment and livelihoods.
The AIIB-IsDB project is what is called a build-own-operate model, but the growth of PPPs has led to the use of multiple modalities in MDB financing. As Abon and Chiplunkar (2013, 12) outline, the PPP modalities
range from build-operate-transfer, build-own-operate-transfer, concession, private independent power producers, joint ventures, service contracts, build–own–operate, design–build–finance–operate, financing facility, and purely private sector investments such as direct loans to and equity investments in private organizations.
For infrastructure projects, it is common to see a special purpose vehicle (SPV) company created for the project that private investors and the government have equity shares in, with governments providing a capital subsidy through a range of possible mechanisms. Governments often guarantee tax breaks and revenues to the SPV, effectively de-risking it for private and institutional investors. Alston (2018) notes that this kind of privatisation of infrastructure has dramatically change the relationship between people and their governments and questions whether these arrangements can protect human rights in the face of the profit motive and corporate secrecy.
The World Bank’s MFD agenda targets infrastructure and aims to turn it into an asset class. As Gabor (2018) argues, both the Bank and the G20 are focussed on de-risking projects, particularly infrastructure for private capital, and Gabor’s parallel to David Harvey’s accumulation by dispossession in the term accumulation by re-risking is apt. What we could add to Gabor’s analysis, is that the traditional MDB approach of borrowing on bond markets to fund infrastructure that states would guarantee to repay, is also a way of de-risking private finance invested in MDBs. Still, extending de-risking to the asset and privatising its ownership extends the reach and power of private capital and interests over public ones in profound ways. The irony of the World Bank’s approach to harnessing private finance in innovative ways is that the Bank was precisely meant to be an alternative to the dangers and violence of private capital. Instead, the Bank is expanding that influence.
States caught in the straitjacket of independent central banks and small government budgets are almost forced into privatisation and PPPs, assisted by the fact that accountancy rules mean PPPs can be excluded from public debt. Yet, PPPs are very often more expensive than traditional public investment though they are presented to citizens as more competitive (Alston 2018, Bayliss and Van Waeyenberge 2018) and their social outcomes are not satisfactory despite the multitude of safeguard policies that MDBs require (Alston 2018). The renewal of the austerity agenda following the 2008 Global Financial Crisis (GCF) saw a further growth in privatisation as a way of paying down state debt, which was created by bailing out private banks and businesses. PPPs are also being driven by the masses of surplus capital floating around the world looking for safe and profitable investments; investors are demanding states create these vehicles.
The AIIB’s loans offer insights into the financialisation of infrastructure. Loans to financial intermediaries went from 15.4% of AIIB loan commitments in 2017 to 24.6% in 2018 and 28.8% in 2019. Of the 28 loan commitments in 2019, there were at least five PPPs and a further 15 loans to ‘innovative’ financial instruments including: financial intermediaries, private equity firms, climate bond portfolios, managed portfolios and the IFC’s Emerging Asia Fund. One of the equity investments is in CITIC Capital’s Pan Eurasian Fund. CITIC Capital calls itself an ‘alternative investment management and advisory company’ and its largest owner is CITIC Group, which is China’s largest state-owned conglomerate and investment company. In other words, the AIIB is borrowing on bond markets to fund a Chinese state-backed private equity group, which will invest in projects and firms in Eurasia. This is a complex financial structure that requires profits to be paid to investors including the AIIB and salaries and bonuses to be paid to the fund managers as well as to the managers of the actual end projects. There will be very little transparency in the investments in Eurasian states.
AIIB loans to financial intermediaries include a subsidiary of the giant Indian conglomerate Tata – the Tata Cleantech Sustainable Infrastructure On-Lending Facility – and Turkiye Sinai Kalkinma Bankasi – Turkey’s first privately owned development and investment bank, which is majority owned by Turkiye Is Bankasi Group, the country’s largest private bank. In other words, these subsidiaries are both SPVs that reduce liability and accountability for their owners.
The $500 million AIIB investment to establish the Asia ESG (Environment, Social, and Governance) Enhanced Credit Managed Portfolio in 2019 looks like a way for the new MDB to rapidly increase its portfolio, though the Managed Portfolio will take some time to invest in projects. The ESG ratings are meant to signal to investors that the asset is not ‘dirty’ and hence this lowers its risk, though the contents of these ratings are deeply problematic. The administrative costs of establishing projects will be borne by the new entity and not the AIIB, making the AIIB look ‘leaner’ than it is. The fundamental issues with the intermediaries are their cost, lack of transparency around investments, and whether the projects eventually funded support basic human rights and poverty reduction.
A further irony here is that China itself used domestic and direct finance to support its rapid development in the 1980s and beyond with the state (through force account) or state-owned companies undertaking most infrastructure development. In its development finance, however, China is pushing other countries in the Global South to rely on international lending for finance and multinational corporations for construction. These examples show that China is pushing a retroliberal agenda that is ‘kicking away the ladder for developing countries’ (Yağcı 2016, 43).

9.4 Conclusion

Infrastructure is a core work area for the MDBs. It is also important for development and human well-being. There is, however, no simple relationship between the two. The type, quality, accessibility, and environmental impacts of infrastructure all need to be context-appropriate for large-scale, costly investments to be a net benefit. The MDBs discovered this with their early decades of investment producing poor outcomes and creating debt burdens in many countries. Notably, the MDBs now have access to better social cost benefit analysis tools than were available in the 1960s and 1970s (Mold 2012), so project selection should be better but these are not perfect and large infrastructure investments are still regularly subject to large cost overruns and overestimations of their benefits (Flyvbjerg 2009, Flyvbjerg, Garbuio, and Lovallo 2009). But infrastructure is back in fashion and it is a key part of the current retroliberal development agenda. The MDBs are being pressured to lend because of Chinese development finance and large quantities of surplus private capital seeking investments and they are increasingly using complex financial structures to do so.
This chapter has demonstrated how the MDBs have always promoted private sector participation in infrastructure regardless of its efficacy and they are now pushing complex financial mechanisms to increase sectoral investment. This is even though the MDBs were created to combat problematic private sector financial instruments and World Bank research demonstrating that every financial crisis of the post-Second World War period was preceded by ‘innovative’ financial tools (Kose et al. 2020). Further, this research shows that the current debt build-up is the fastest and most rapid in the post-war period. All of this suggests that the world is heading for another debt crisis and that many infrastructure investments from the MDBs will end up as debts, dragging down the prospects of states for decades.
Still, even with the World Bank and MDBs de-risking projects, there are limits to investments. The World Bank (2020) Private Participation in Infrastructure Dashboard tracks projects in 139 low- and middle-income countries and reveals that between 1990 and 2020 there have been close to 8,200 projects investing nearly $2 trillion. However, only 3.17% of this went to low-income countries. Indeed, five countries accounted for over 60% of investments – China, Brazil, India, Vietnam, and Russia – and China alone accounted for over 27% of investments in 2019. Electricity is the largest sector for investment, as it is one of the safest and most profitable sectors. Roads are a distant second, while sectors such as water, sanitation, and waste generate very little investment overall and even less in the poorest states. This suggests that direct MDB financing still has a role. The MDBs, however, are now more interested in promoting and working with financial sector actors than in the hard work of developing project pipelines in difficult though vital sectors like sanitation.

10 Climate finance

Climate finance ‘refers to local, national, or transnational financing – drawn from public, private, and alternative sources of financing – that seeks to support mitigation and adaptation actions that will address climate change’ (UNFCCC 2020). Through their climate investments and advisory services, the multilateral development banks (MDBs) work as climate intermediaries. Exercising material, technical, and normative influence, they draw attention to climate issues and connect different actors in their efforts to adapt, mitigate, and build resilience to climate change. The MDBs frame climate action in terms of interventions made possible by financial investment, contributing to a narrative that exists largely within the bounds of a market-based, economistic, and quantifiable methodology.
The MDBs originally had fairly poor environmental records but have been reforming and have become influential actors in the global economic governance of sustainable development and climate change (Florini and Sovacool 2009, Käkönen and Kaisti 2012). They are only one part of complex environmental governance networks but are notable for their leadership in mainstreaming initiatives like the 2015 Paris Agreement and the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). They have introduced green and blue bonds to catalyse private investment. Indeed, ‘multilateral lending is a defining feature of international finance and the environment’ (Clapp and Dauvergne 2005, 196).
MDB climate actions are diverse. They range from project financing and resource mobilisation, including co-financing and on-lending to the public and private sectors, to building knowledge networks and communities of practice, to research and training. These actions make the MDBs climate intermediaries that draw together different actors and send signals to private capital about the feasibility of and potential rates of return on climate investments. The focus on private investment cannot be understated; the MDBs all acknowledge that public multilateral development finance (MDF) is required in the short-term but argue that successful climate interventions in the mid- to long-term need private capital. This somewhat naïve neoliberal climate mantra holds that with enough finance invested it is possible to manage climate change while promoting continuous economic growth.
The MDBs pursue climate actions to different degrees. Over the past few decades, international focus and institutional isomorphism has led almost all to adopt, at the least, corporate climate action plans to align their lending operations with sustainable best practice and participate in global climate forums. There are, however, differences due to capital availability, shareholder influence, and regional circumstance. Capital and mandate restrictions limit some MDBs from financing climate projects, but the larger MDBs are climate leaders, as are some of the smaller ones.
Shareholder influence and geography play key roles too. The Latin American MDBs are exemplars of proactive climate action as the region has been hit hard by climate-related disasters and progressive leaders have supported interventions, though regional politics has become more conservative in the last few years. Still, as an example, the Caribbean Community’s ‘early political championing of the climate change threat’ shaped the Caribbean Development Bank’s (CDB) robust environmental agenda (CDB 2012). In contrast, the Russian-led Eurasian Development Bank (EDB) and Black Sea Trade and Development Bank (BSTDB) are laggards and continue to heavily invest in petroleum, coal, and oil but the European Union (EU) has ensured that the European Investment Bank (EIB) has a strategic focus on climate change.
This chapter outlines the evolving role of the MDBs as climate intermediaries. The first section builds a history of the international climate regime, identifying key turning points in MDB climate action. Next, this chapter analyses initiatives by the MDBs to mainstream environmental and climate change objectives in their organisational activities. The third section evaluates MDB climate finance approvals, detailing both investment volumes and sectoral focuses. The final section examines climate bonds and climate finance facilities established, administered, or supported by the MDBs.

10.1 Climate history

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), the leading United Nations (UN) body debating and coordinating global climate action, proclaimed in the early 2000s that ‘there is now little doubt that human-induced climate change is happening’ (cited in Adger et al. 2003, 180). The MDBs have largely taken this warning seriously, albeit with inter-organisational differences in practical action and some gaps and questions. Multilateral climate finance has evolved with the international climate regime – defined here as the global governance framework coordinating state and non-state climate action – over the past several decades. There have been four phases in the international climate regime: (i) 1970–87: Limits to Growth and Sustainable Development; (ii) 1987–2000: Formalising the International Climate Regime; (iii) 2000–10: From Sustainability to Climate Change; and, (iv) 2010–20: Adaptation, Mitigation, and Resilience. These phases, explored briefly below, align with key turning points in global climate action, which, in turn, saw organisational, operational, and policy shifts in the MDBs.

10.1.1 Phase I: 1970–87: limits to growth and sustainable development

Connections between development and the environment were first made by the MDBs in the 1970s. In 1971, the World Bank prepared the first MDB handbook linking environmental costs to long-term economic growth as an acceptable trade-off (Gutner 2002, 51, Lee 1985, x); they pioneered analysis of environmental factors in lending operations and conceptualised ‘ways to stabilise the environment so that it could be commercialised and used to promote poverty alleviation’ (Yudelman 1976). In other words, the environment was an input to economic growth, it was only a matter of finding the right level of exploitation. In 1972, the Club of Rome published the influential Limits to Growth, which modelled the consequences of exponential economic and population growth on finite natural resources (Meadows et al. 1972). Limits to Growth sparked debate about whether economic growth and sustainability are compatible as well as a reformist discussion on how best to manage natural resources while improving economic growth. This was a factor in the Inter-American Development Bank (IADB) becoming the first MDB to adopt an environmental policy in 1979.
Global concern over environmental sustainability grew throughout the 1970s and 1980s, spurred by heavily publicised human-made disasters including the 1978 Amoco Cadiz oil spill, 1984 Bhopal gas tragedy, and 1986 Chernobyl nuclear accident (Elliott 2004, 13). Further, the dire environmental consequences of numerous MDB, particularly World Bank, projects started to emerge. One of the most infamous was the World Bank Polonoroeste project paving 1,500 km of highway through the Brazil’s Amazon, which resulted in mass deforestation and immiseration of the local Amerindian tribes (Rich 1994). Thus, environmental factors began to be incorporated into development assistance, though not very well.
Sustainable development emerged as a concept first in the 1980 UN-led The World Conservation Strategy before entering the official communiques of several MDBs, but it was not formalised in lending operations or project evaluations (Lee 1985, ix). The 1987 Our Common Future, the report drafted by the Brundtland Commission, articulated sustainable development as ‘development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs’ (Brundtland 1987, 43). The Brundtland Report embedded the sustainable development concept in both policy and public discourse, an important step in mainstreaming environmental action (Redclift 2005). It was a key driver for MDBs revising their approach to environmental action throughout the 1990s.

10.1.2 Phase II: 1987–2000: formalising the international climate regime

The decade following the 1987 Brundtland Report saw the steady formalisation of the international climate regime, with the establishment of several environmental agencies. Among the first agencies was the IPCC set up in 1988 to assess existing scientific knowledge of human-caused climate change. The international community established the Global Environment Facility (GEF) in 1992, initially as a pilot programme with the World Bank. The 1992 UN Conference on Environment and Development – called the Rio Earth Summit – led to Agenda 21 outlining a non-binding sustainable development agenda. With the adoption of the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) at the Rio Earth Summit, the GEF became the financing mechanism of both Agenda 21 and the UNFCCC (Clapp and Dauvergne 2005, 208). The Kyoto Protocol of 1997, which entered into force in 2005, enacted greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reduction targets. This was followed by the 2002 World Summit on Sustainable Development and the 2012 Rio +20 Summit.
The 1990s saw the steady formalisation of the environmental governance architecture, with the MDBs gradually aligning their operations to key international statements and agendas. Early on, the MDBs acted because of poor press and NGO pressure due to the significant environmental damage many of their projects were causing. The MDBs thus began to establish organisational departments to address sustainable development, as well as draft operational policy and guidelines. While relatively narrow in focus, the steady formalisation of the international climate regime led many MDBs to begin substantively engaging with environmental issues, framed in the 1990s in terms of sustainable development.

10.1.3 Phase III: 2000–10: from sustainability to climate change

The 2000s began with a highpoint of international collaboration – the launch of the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) – but ended in a breakdown of inter-state cooperation with the failure of the 2009 COP-15 Copenhagen Summit to set emissions reduction targets. Two significant changes for the MDBs occurred. First, as outlined in Chapter 4, the MDGs became a unifying framework for the MDBs and Goal 8 was ‘to ensure environmental sustainability,’ which institutionalised it in the development banks (Alston 2005). In 2000, to better accommodate sustainable development and the MDGs, the GEF adopted a new policy giving the regional development banks (RDBs) access to GEF funding. This later expanded to include some sub-regional and specialised development banks, so there are now eight MDBs among the GEF’s implementing agencies. The World Bank’s Country Assistance Strategies – country-level diagnostics proposing loan schedules – began to ask sustainability questions from 2001 (Shyamsundar et al. 2001, 3).
The second significant development, identifiable from the late 2000s, saw the MDBs become key players in addressing climate change. With the collapse of COP-15 from stalled inter-state cooperation compounded by the spike in protectionist policies following the 2008 Global Financial Crisis (GFC), climate action lost priority for many countries, but this created more space for non-state actors in climate leadership. 2009 was a turning point for the MDBs; they demonstrated a concerted acceleration of interest in climate change adaptation, mitigation, and resilience, taking on the idea of climate-smart development and not just sustainable development (Lipper and Zilberman 2018).

10.1.4 Phase IV: 2010–20: adaptation, mitigation, and resilience

The 2010s saw accelerated concern for climate adaptation (purposive changes to better cope with climate change), mitigation (stabilisation or reduction of GHG emissions), and resilience (building infrastructure and services to better withstand climate shocks) (Ayers et al. 2014, Henstra 2012, Janetos et al. 2012). The rhetoric turned from environmental factors are relevant to development to climate change shapes development. During the 1990s, multilateral and bilateral climate finance had tended to be short-term funding for ad hoc projects. By the 2010s, it involved longer-term and more substantial investments (Peake and Ekins 2017, 833–4). The World Bank nearly doubled annual allocations between 2012 and 2018 and other MDBs increased from the mid-decade too.
The 2015 Paris Agreement and SDGs are the lodestars of current MDB climate finance, broadly aligning their lending, policy, and normative actions. A joint Climate Change Finance Statement released by several large MDBs in September 2019 showed their common approach: commitment to increase climate finance allocation over time; catalysing private finance; supporting the Paris Agreement; strengthening climate finance transparency reporting; and, transitioning operations away from fossil fuel projects (AfDB, AsDB, AIIB, et al. 2019). The priorities are clearly for more finance and more private sector finance.
The Paris Agreement entered into force in November 2016. Its centrepiece is the Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs), the emissions reduction and adaptation commitments of countries. The Agreement outlined the need to build cooperative networks through the MDBs and climate financing mechanisms to achieve the NDCs (Peake and Ekins 2017, Sharma 2017). Many MDBs joined the NDC Partnership Agreement and built their own NDC advisory platforms: IADB launched NDC Invest in 2016, the African Development Bank (AfDB) unveiled the African HDC Hub in 2017, and the Asian Development Bank (AsDB) established NDC Advance in 2018 as a technical assistance platform. Other MDBs, such as EIB and CDB, have not developed their own platforms but incorporate measures to ensure lending operations align with recipient NDC strategies (Wright et al. 2018). Many MDBs also work with the Green Climate Fund (GCF), proposed in 2009 and established in 2011. It is the world’s largest climate fund and was mandated to limit or reduce GHG emissions in developing countries and adapt vulnerable societies to climate change. It works through national and multinational implementing bodies including 11 MDBs.
The SDGs complement the Paris Agreement and the discursive shift from sustainability to climate action. The link between them is that global temperature increases above 1.5°C would make impossible the achievement of many of the SDGs and lead to worsening inequalities and a greater likelihood of conflict and humanitarian issues (IPCC 2018). The SDGs are much more comprehensive than the MDGs in their climate awareness and even imply that ‘[e]conomic growth can no longer be attained at the cost of the environment’ (AsDB 2019, viii), but they do not go as far as suggesting that there is a trade-off between the two and they put a great deal of faith in technological fixes. While global agreements now indicate that ignoring anthropogenic climate change is no longer an option, the selfish retroliberal agenda has seen more and more states walk away from international agreements.

10.2 Climate mainstreaming

Every MDB has mainstreamed environmental actions, defined as ‘the integration of environmental and climate change objectives into non-environmental sectors and the “greening” of public policies’ (Benson et al. 2014, 606). While some have done far more and others far less, they have all acted, with the 2010s seeing a growth in mainstreaming. Only the Central American Bank for Economic Integration (CABEI), Development Bank of Latin America (CAF), European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD), and New Development Bank (NDB) explicitly refer to environmental factors in their establishing agreements; EBRD was ‘the first MDB to be born with an environmental mandate’ (Gutner 2002, 62), it being founded in the post-Brundtland milieu amidst the formalisation of the climate regime. The significance of so few MDBs formally mandated to support climate action shows that the growth of climate finance has been the result of efforts by management, member countries, and social movements to foster organisational change (Goldman 2001).
The World Bank was an early adopter of mainstreaming, albeit in somewhat limited forms. In 1987, it established an Environmental Department to set policy, planning, and research work but this was largely in response to public pressure related to environmental disasters and the Brundtland Report (Goldman 2001, Gutner 2002, Rich 1994). However, the Bank maintained a very narrow and neoliberal understanding that environmental degradation limited economic growth (Castro 2004). Mainstreaming saw the Bank become the largest environmental donor of the 1990s, providing a third of all multilateral environmental assistance, though this illustrates the lack of other resources as much as the Bank’s engagement. Further, there were pertinent criticisms of the Bank’s definition of environmental projects as it included large-scale dam projects that displaced large numbers of people including tribal communities (Zawahri and Weinthal 2014). Under pressure, the Bank established inspection panels and requirements that all projects must submit environmental impact assessments (O’Neill 2012, 155); the number of environmental staffers increased from five in 1987 to 200 by 1994. The ‘significant shift in World Bank policies… in a relatively short period’ (Clapp and Dauvergne 2005, 201) framed how other MDBs should restructure their own policies and lending operations, particularly the RDBs.
The RDBs began mainstreaming following the Brundtland Report. Prior to it, they were not aligned; earlier environmental policies were largely ad hoc, voluntary guidelines (Gutner 2002, 70). From 2000 onwards, isomorphic pressure framed by the Kyoto Protocol, MDGs, and growing public concern led the RDBs to deepen their mainstreaming activities. AsDB approved a new Environmental Policy in 2002, as did AfDB in 2004; these policies updated environmental assessment guidelines, revised country environmental analyses, and laid the groundwork to expand environmental and energy investments (Delina 2011, Posas 2011). Accelerated by the 2015 Paris Agreement and SDGs, the RDBs developed climate change action plans to increase their climate investments, establish policy platforms, and catalyse private climate finance. EBRD unveiled its Green Economy Transition (GET) framework in 2015, proposed to achieve a target of increasing climate finance to 40% of annual investments by 2020, and it became its main mechanisms to support borrower NDCs and the SDGs (EBRD 2018). Further, EIB sent strong signals to other MDBs and private capital with its announcement in July 2019 it would cease financing fossil fuel projects by the end of 2021 (Ambrose 2019).
Compared to the World Bank and RDBs, the other MDBs demonstrate greater variability in their climate actions. Various factors shape these differences, including limited capital availability constraining annual approvals and shareholder activism either promoting or demoting climate action as a strategic priority. The small scale of many of the sub-regional and specialised banks is also a factor. Climate-friendly changes include organisational pledges to carbon neutrality, such as the Council of Europe Development Bank (CEB) purchasing hybrid vehicles and CABEI maintaining a carbon neutral headquarters, and utilising renewable energy market mechanisms, with the East African Development Bank (EADB) purchasing UN-certified carbon emissions reduction credits from member countries.
Latin American and Caribbean MDBs have a strong history of climate mainstreaming. CAF, CDB, and CABEI have all developed robust policies, demonstrating what large and small MDBs are capable of with enough shareholder support. CAF started its Sustainable Development Coordination Office in 1994, which evolved to become the new Vice Presidency of Social and Environmental Development in 2004. Recently, it built a climate network with regional organisations to assemble alliances for climate finance action (CAF 2017), a move indicative of norm leadership. CDB and CABEI have similarly built strong climate frameworks, with CDB guided by its Climate Resilience Strategy 2019–24 drafted to develop its reputation and capabilities as a regional climate leader (CDB 2018a) and CABEI by its 2015–19 Institutional Strategy that recognises Central America as a disaster-prone region and therefore requires climate investments (CABEI 2015). Further, CABEI has developed strategic networks to support its climate actions – just like CAF – but has done so with non-regional actors, including the Climate Change Adaptation Fund and GCF. For smaller MDBs, accreditation with development funds and networks enables very small annual climate commitments to expand considerably.
Other MDBs have engaged less with climate mainstreaming, notably the Russian- and Arab-led MDBs. They do pursue some climate actions, but their approach is narrower and more conservative than other MDBs. To illustrate, the International Investment Bank (IIB) has an Environment and Social Policy Framework, but its conceptualisation of climate action is narrowly limited to sustainable energy and infrastructure, while making considerable allowances for fossil fuel investments. This reflects the importance of carbon-based energy to its leading shareholders and their geopolitical pursuit of resource security. Further, while the Arab Bank for Economic Development in Africa (BADEA) became more rhetorically active in the 2010s, action lagged. BADEA’s Seventh Five-Year Plan does not mention climate change, though it does discuss the importance of carbon-based and renewable energy sources (BADEA 2015), a position common across the Arab-led MDBs. The Islamic Development Bank (IsDB) has become somewhat more of a climate leader recently, publishing its 2019 Climate Change Policy that recognises its members are facing significant obstacles due to climate change (IsDB 2019). The Policy is aligned with a 10 Year Strategy that supports the achievement of the SDGs by 2030 (Sadik 2018) and has four policy pillars: mainstreaming climate action in lending operations; promoting climate change resilience; green growth and the transition to a green economy; and, leveraging resources for climate action (IsDB 2019). Yet, IsDB still allows projects to include fossil fuel elements, a reflection of the strategic importance of non-renewables to its main shareholders.
Despite achievements, troubling issues remain. Regarding environmental safeguards applied to projects, Humphrey (2016, 4–5) outlines that they are imposed over national laws and thus lead to sovereignty questions, are often one-size-fits-all, legalistic, and inflexible, and are expensive and time-consuming with costs paid by the borrower. Further, mainstreaming has revealed not a break from traditional MDB operations but attempts to integrate climate actions into the neoliberal model – emphasising market-based solutions and private sector finance to adapt, mitigate, and build resilience to climate change. As an illustration, most MDBS have climate risk management tools, including project exclusion lists prohibiting investments in non-renewables and screening tools to evaluate the climate impacts of projects. While such tools are important to minimise the negative effects of MDB operations, these tools do not fundamentally transform how development is understood or challenge the naïve faith that continuous economic growth is possible.

10.3 Climate finance

Annual sustainable development lending and climate finance by the MDBs has increased in volume and scope since the 1980s, paralleling climate mainstreaming. In absolute terms, the UN estimates global climate finance – public and private – to have been between $340 and $650 billion in 2014. However, global infrastructural, industrial, and energy investments – including carbon-based sectors – in the same year were estimated to be in the trillions (CEB 2016, Peake and Ekins 2017). This disparity is unsurprising, given the orientation of global investment patterns towards ‘traditional’ and ‘bankable’ sectors. But, despite risk aversion, over the 2010s there was increased support for climate action from both public and private capital, especially energy transition investments. Bell (2020, 59–64) identifies two phases in the gradual expansion of climate finance markets. First, state intervention prompted by popular pressure or political policy agendas encouraged a growth in renewables. Second, the expansion of state intervention signalled increasing market returns on investments, leading to greater engagement by private capital with new financial innovations emerging. This occurs even though ‘the sunk costs and older path dependencies in the fossil fuel sector remain deeply entrenched in energy systems worldwide’ (Bell 2020, 62). This analysis both provides a strong logic for MDBs to further engage in climate finance and frames the changes in MDBs. They have played an important role as ‘market makers’ for climate markets, catalysing private investment in environmental projects and setting common operational standards in project delivery and climate finance tracking (CEB 2016).
A 2019 AsDB Institute report, co-authored by Jeffrey Sachs, argues that the MDBs ‘should help finance [environmental] programs in Asia and other regions by raising vastly more long-term debt from the capital markets at the prevailing low interest rates and then lending the money to governments and public-private investment entities’ (Sachs et al. 2019, 3). The report reveals two important trends: first, the focus on public funding in the short-term to catalyse long-term private investment and, second, the increasing financialisation of climate action and use of debt. The need for public financial action is further articulated by Sachs et al. (2019, 5) because private banks ‘consider most [renewable energy] projects to be risky’ and their ‘resources come from deposits, and deposits are usually short to medium term, whereas green infrastructure projects require long-term finance, resulting in a maturity mismatch for banks.’
AfDB, AsDB, EBRD, EIB, IADB, and the World Bank began publishing joint annual climate finance reports in 2011, following joint press releases starting with COP-15 in 2009. Since 2015, the reports have outlined projected climate finance allocations, with 2020 targets being to double or triple climate finance approvals. They are also a means to improve transparency, improve cooperation, and catalyse private finance. The 2018 Joint Report demonstrates a staggered increase in climate finance approvals after the 2015 Paris Agreement (AfDB, AsDB, EBRD, et al. 2019).
Several MDBs and the International Development Finance Club (IDFC) jointly agreed to the Common Principles for Climate Adaptation Finance Tracking in March 2015. The Principles were designed to align what previously had been ad hoc environmental investments and develop more systematic methodologies to track the movement of climate finance. Between 2015 and 2017, the Principles were applied to more than 1,000 projects with adaptation finance exceeding $18.6 billion. The IsDB applied the Principles to its climate actions beginning in 2018, demonstrating a rollout of the Principles as an institutional norm. Other MDBs have built their own methodologies for mapping climate finance. In addition to reporting, the Principles have been useful in drawing attention to MDB climate action and, as an attempt to ‘crowd in’ private capital, they have led the MDBs to build their capacity to assess climate finance vulnerabilities (Climate Finance Tracking Working Group 2018).
The World Bank, RDBs, and CAF and NIB are the largest MDB climate financiers. It is difficult to isolate the climate commitments of many MDBs; some do not list it as a discrete sector, for the NIB (2018) it is a cross-cutting theme. Others, like the World Bank, do, but such allocations reflect normative choices, for example, in determining what percentage of a sanitation project might count as climate finance. The World Bank’s Environment and Natural Resources investment stream delivered 14% of all approvals between 2011 and 2015; the stream is one of eleven operational priorities. Between 2011 and 2016, the Bank Group committed $52 billion to over 900 climate projects, an annual average of $10.3 billion. In 2017, following a post-Paris Agreement spike, the percentage of climate finance increased to 31% (World Bank 2017). In 2018, the World Bank was the largest MDB climate financier, collectively approving close to 50% of all climate finance when ranked against the RDBs, followed by EIB and EBRD.
Like the World Bank, the RDBs have increased their climate finance commitments since the Paris Agreement. In 2017, AfDB had 28% of approvals as climate finance, up from 9% in 2016, AsDB listed 22% of approvals as climate interventions, and EBRD allocated 43% of OCR to its GET, up from 33% in 2016. Yet, the RDBs pursue different climate objectives. AfDB focusses on a ‘New Deal on Energy’ and policy environments attractive to private investment, AsDB on building climate-resilient infrastructure and low-carbon development, and EIB on targeting renewable energy projects and waste and wastewater reclamation. RDB climate finance focusses more on mitigation (70% in 2018) than adaptation (30% in 2018), with mitigation loans tending to be short-term responses compared to longer-term adaptation projects. In 2018, 71% of World Bank and RDB climate finance was delivered as investment projects and only 8% as policy loans, meaning that MDB climate finance is project-based. Lastly, sub-Saharan Africa (21%) and Latin America and the Caribbean (20%) are the two largest regional recipients of climate action from the World Bank and RDBs (AfDB, AsDB, EBRD, et al. 2019).
A 2018 report by E3G, an independent climate change thinktank, analysed the progress of the RDBs relative to the Paris Agreement (Wright et al. 2018). It found that while they all had aligned their practices with the Paris Agreement, the IADB and AfDB excelled as climate leaders in transitioning to low-carbon and resilient lending with their respective ratio of renewable climate finance to non-renewable finance as 13.9:1 and 5.3:1. The other RDBs performed less well: EBRD 1.2:1, EIB 1:1, and AsDB 0.9:1. These ratios are worrying. While the RDBs are committed to increasing their climate commitments and to divesting non-renewable energy investments, their critics rightly point out that they are not doing this fast enough given both the scale of the climate crisis and the role of the MDBs as market leaders.
The smaller MDBs demonstrate considerable variability in their climate commitments, reflecting their differences in climate mainstreaming as well as capital availability and shareholder influence. The Latin American and Caribbean MDBs are climate leaders, with 37% of CABEI approvals between 2015 and 2018 for climate interventions. CABEI also maintained a portfolio of $1 billion of GCF projects. Similarly, CEB allocated 30% of its annual approvals to environmental projects in 2015 and CDB committed 44% to environmental sustainability and disaster risk reduction in 2017. Other MDBs invest far less. BADEA did not approve any climate change projects in 2017, although 56% of its portfolio financed water, sanitation, and power projects, and the Eastern and Southern African Trade and Development Bank (TDB), despite policies mainstreaming sustainability, assigned 45% of its trade finance to petrochemical transactions in 2016 (BADEA 2017, TDB 2016). The BSTDB demonstrates the tension between renewable and non-renewable energy projects for smaller MDBs. In 2019, it approved several renewable energy projects, including the Syvash Wind Farm Project in Ukraine and a $65 million loan to the $970 million greenfield EFELER Geothermal Power Plant in Turkey. Yet, it also financed offshore oil and gas projects in Azerbaijan and Greece, a coal terminal in Russia, and a greenfield waste incineration plant in Turkey.
The AIIB and NDB claim to prioritise sustainable infrastructure investments. For AIIB, its sustainable energy infrastructure projects favour solar and hydro power (AIIB 2018), despite many climate experts not including hydropower as a sustainable source given its impacts on river flows and resettlement. But its lead shareholder China has made major investments in this area. NDB has a clear operational focus: sustainable infrastructure development, which is forecast to take two-thirds of total project commitments (NDB 2017). Of approved projects between 2016 and 2018, renewable energy and transportation projects were most common for NDB, typically involving highway upgrades and wind, hydro, and solar construction and storage projects. With a focus on project investment for climate action, the lending patterns of the new MDBs closely resemble those of the larger and more established MDBs, notably the presumption that infrastructural and technological reforms will solve climate challenges.

10.4 Climate bonds and facilities

MDF organisations have led the establishment and management of climate bonds and various funds and facilities to catalyse public and – especially – private investment for climate action. The rise of these funding mechanisms demonstrates the increasing financialisation of climate policy and the overreliance on global and regional financial markets (Layfield 2013). Environmental action has been a key part of the financialisation agenda of the MDBs and, as Layfield (2013, 901–2) remarks, a
financialised policy goes further than simply relying on market-based instruments to achieve reductions in carbon emissions. In this context financialisation refers to the way in which governments identified a potentially global problem in the form of anthropogenic climate change, and then permitted a new global financial market to emerge as an important means to address this problem.
Still, there are large climate financing gaps and uncertainty whether private capital will
meet the adaptation needs of the poorest and most marginalised people… The world’s least developed countries received only 1.9 per cent of global foreign direct investment flows in 2012 and little of this money is focused in areas which are adaptation priorities.
(Oxfam 2013)
Yet, the MDBs hold fast to the Maximising Finance for Development (MFD) view that ‘capital markets can provide new solutions to age-old development challenges’ (Gabor 2018, 16), be those challenges related to infrastructure, climate change, or human development.
Green bonds are the main financial instrument issued by the MDBs to mobilise private finance for climate change solutions. They were pioneered by the EIB in 2007, and their importance has increased since the 2015 Paris Agreement to catalyse green private investment (AsDB 2017). The green bond market grew considerably during the 2010s, increasing from $11 billion in 2013 to $42 billion in 2015 to $250 billion in 2018 (Chang 2019). Between 2007 and 2012, the green bond market was largely comprised of MDBs, notably the EIB, World Bank, and International Finance Corporation (IFC), with a few government funding agencies and national development banks also involved. The Paris Agreement and entrance of Chinese issuers gave the market a large boost (AsDB 2017, OECD 2017). Green bonds are structured like conventional bonds, because having the same or similar risk/return profiles and yield maturities encourages private investment (Banga 2019). The main point of difference is that proceeds must finance green projects. Blue bonds are a more recent innovation, designed to support the stable conservation and sustainable exploitation of the blue economy – sustainable economic activities directly related to oceans and seas (CDB 2018b), though it is worth pausing to ask if this is possible. Seychelles launched the world’s first sovereign blue bond in 2018 to leverage finance for the blue economy, which led the CDB to express interest in issuing them.
The green bond market is governed by the voluntary Green Bond Principles. Established in 2014 by a consortium of investment banks including Deutsche Bank, JP Morgan, and HSBC, the Principles form an important structuring regime adopted by many MDBs, demonstrating their links to banking institutions. Updated in 2018, the Principles have four core components: the use of proceeds for actions with clear environmental benefits; processes of project evaluation and selection aligned to sustainability objectives and eligibility; management of proceeds allowing tracking of investment operations; and, up-to-date reporting on proceeds use (ICMA 2018). AfDB, AsDB, CEB, EBRD, EIB, NIB, and the World Bank are all member issuers of the Green Bond Principles, and the West African Development Bank (BOAD) is an observer. Despite the structuring regime of the Principles, criticisms have been raised that the green bond market is far too broad to meaningfully identify clear environmental benefits, leading to the potential ‘greenwashing’ of non-renewable sectors (Doran and Tanner 2019). Further, while the green bond market has been effective in financialising ‘concerns’ about climate change, questions remain whether it has led to quantifiable environmental improvements (Tripathy 2017).
The EIB issued the first green bond in 2007, labelled a Climate Awareness Bond. Between 2007 and 2018, it raised €23.5 billion across 11 currencies, making it the world’s largest green bond issuer (Wright et al. 2018). In April 2018, EIB introduced Sustainability Awareness Bonds, with an initial €500 million offering. The World Bank issued its own green bonds in 2008, responding to a request from Scandinavian pension funds seeking to support climate projects. It achieved issuances of over $10 billion by 2017. EBRD commenced bond issuances in 2010 and NIB in 2011. AfDB began in 2013, raising $15 billion between 2013 and 2018, which is significant given that the African green bond market only reached $3 billion in 2017. AsDB was comparatively slow to enter the market given that the Asia-Pacific region already had a green bond market and was the second largest region for climate-aligned bonds in 2017. In regions where bond markets are underdeveloped, the MDBs have taken a leading role in cultivating green and blue bond transactions and in assisting governments to issue their own green bonds (Wright et al. 2018).
Smaller MDBs have also engaged with climate bonds, though in more marginal ways compared to the RDBs. CABEI is relatively active and in November 2019 it issued a $375 million floating-rate five-year Green Bond, listed on the Luxembourg and Taipei Stock Exchanges, demonstrating the complexity of global finances; the issuance led to an aggregate demand of $974 million, driven by Asian (75%) and European (25%) investors. The ECOWAS Bank for Investment and Development (EBID) has expressed interest in green bond issuances, but it is currently limited to policy discussions. In 2017, ECOWAS announced the intention to establish a platform for climate finance at COP-23 in Bonn. The statement was preceded by the Declaration of Abidjan in June 2017 at the ECOWAS Green Investment Catalyst Roundtable, which discussed the potential for green investment in West Africa and constraints posed by a lack of adequate financing and stable legal frameworks. The smaller MDBs recognise the utility of climate bond issuances as bolstering their limited capital to finance climate interventions. In November 2019, IsDB issued a Green Sukuk, which is a Shari’ah compliant green bond. The Islamic MDB has issued sukuks, or Islamic bonds, since 2003 and total international sukuk issuances reached $95 billion in 2017 (Sadik 2018, 72–4). Under the Sustainable Finance Framework, IsDB is authorised to issue Green Sukuk and Sustainability Sukuk, the main difference between them being that Sustainability Sukuk proceeds can be allocated to both environmental and social projects. The AIIB and NDB have also issued green bonds, following trends set by the more established MDBs. Indeed, they began issuing them almost immediately after their establishment.
There is a chaotic network of actors, funds, and financing mechanisms supporting the international climate regime; ‘the current world of climate change finance…, with its multiplicity of funds, funding sources, and new financial mechanisms for climate change, makes organising climate change finance in an orderly way a daunting task’ (Gomez-Echeverri 2013, 644), leading to coordination difficulties and donor overlap. Administered by or in collaboration with the MDBs, climate funds and facilities range from the GEF and GCF to smaller multi- donor trusts such as AsDB’s Climate Change Fund established in May 2008 and AfDB’s Africa Climate Change Fund founded in April 2014. Despite governance complexity, the growth of these funds and facilities has increased access to climate finance and made possible more robust pre-investment and technical assistance project support. A range of examples follow.
Financial mechanisms such as the GEF and GCF are beneficial for the smaller MDBs in accessing climate finance. As an example, BOAD is limited in its annual approvals and only recently engaged substantively with climate action. After receiving accreditation with GEF in 2015 and GCF in 2016, BOAD developed a pipeline of projects for WAEMU member countries, receiving over $23 million from GEF and $255 million from GCF. Bowman and Minas (2019, 351) regard GCF favourably, arguing that it ‘could be a key interlocutor between law- and policy-makers and private sector financiers,’ thereby connecting finance with action. In contrast, Kumar (2015) identifies criticisms of GCF, including concerns that it does not adequately consult indigenous communities, its processes lack transparency, and it funnels money via large MDBs and private banks instead of directly to local institutions in the countries where projects take place.
The MDBs have developed and engaged with numerous other small-scale climate funds and facilities. AsDB manages its Climate Change Fund and the Clean Energy Financing Partnerships Facility launched in 2007, NIB runs Climate Change, Energy Efficiency, and Renewable Energy lending facility founded in 2008 endowed with €1 billion, and EIB operates three facilities established in 2004. The creation of these funds and facilities illustrates not only the growing demand for climate investments but also the limitations of traditional OCR and concessional lending for climate interventions.
To demonstrate the multitude of climate funds and facilities interacting with one MDB, consider the AfDB. The African RDB works with the Climate Investment Funds (CIFs), is accredited with GEF, GCF, and the Climate Change Adaptation Fund, and established several regional bodies including the Africa Climate Change Fund, ClimDev-Africa Special Fund, Congo Basin Forest Fund, Sustainable Energy Fund for Africa, and African Water Facility. The CIFs, announced in February 2008 by Japan, the UK, and the US, provide grants, concessional loans, risk mitigation instruments, and equity to over 70 developing countries. A third of CIF funding goes to sub-Saharan Africa and well over half of that goes to the AfDB (AfDB 2017). The complex arrangement of funds and facilities engaged by even one MDB demonstrates the problems in donor coordination, at the same time the system enables MDBs to access different climate finance sources.
Climate bonds and funds and facilities are important instruments to catalyse investment in a sector that suffers considerable public and private financing gaps. The growth of green and blue bonds and alternate financing mechanisms has not been without risks, however. While making capital available that previously was not, the risks include the duplication of scarce resources and expertise resulting in high transaction costs, weak visibility and high complexity for recipients, resource competition at the expense of demand-driven projects, and poor strategic coordination (Brouder and Garreta 2009).

10.5 Conclusion

Since the 1987 Brundtland Report, there have been concerted efforts to financialise climate action and catalyse private investment as argued necessary for long-term climate adaptation, mitigation, and resilience. Writing on the World Bank’s environmental lending, Nielson and Tierney (2003, FN120) contended that critics
are wrong when they conclude that the Bank is conducting business as usual when it comes to the environment. Environmental lending has increased significantly since 1987, the Bank has institutionalized checks on the dysfunctional behavior of its staff, the Bank has dramatically decreased lending for traditional ‘dirty’ loans, and member governments have significantly increased their ability to monitor and sanction behavior that is not consistent with official Bank lending practices.
Though this comment was written almost two decades ago, the fact remains that the MDBs have changed significantly since the 1980s and in fact the 2010s saw further improvements.
While environmentally questionable lending practices remain, the evidence reveals that through climate mainstreaming, increased climate finance levels, and the emergence of green and blue bonds and new financing windows, the MDF architecture has institutionalised sustainable development and climate actions as legitimate and expected organisational behaviours, with the MDBs exercising influence as climate intermediaries. The business-as-usual of the MDBs in 2020 is that climate change is an existential threat to 21st-century development. Still, the major limitation that must be laid bare is that the MDBs plan to address environmental challenges through a market-based capitalist system that still regards growth and sustainability as compatible. The MDBs also remain strongly influenced by their shareholders and the retroliberal era has seen many countries back or even walk away from meaningful climate action; the Spring 2020 Meetings of the World Bank and IMF did not discuss the role of climate action in the COVID-19 recovery. Further, the mechanisms the MDBs favour to address climate change – green bonds and related financialisation – may not be suited to achieve a just and equitable transition to a sustainable future. Still, the fondness of the MDBs and private capital for new bonds may be a sustaining force for the multilateral promotion of climate action.

11 Human development

The multilateral development banks (MDBs) have long used the rhetoric of human development and social progress to shape their operational strategies. For some, such as the World Bank, ending extreme poverty and promoting shared prosperity guide their current missions, while for others like the African Development Bank (AfDB) their revised establishing agreements explicitly include social progress as an organisational purpose. This chapter analyses MDB financing for human development, defined here broadly as investing in people to improve quality and duration of life (Lind 2019, 422), with a focus on education, health, and gender.
This chapter arrives at two significant conclusions. First, although finance for education and health can be dated to the 1960s, the MDBs only began investing in people in earnest in the late 1980s and some only since the early 2000s. With operational histories prioritising infrastructural investment and private sector growth, human development is often a secondary consideration, if considered substantively at all. This takeaway is important, given that so many MDBs use the rhetoric of human development and social progress to defend their investment priorities. Second, human development financing has generally favoured investing in people to improve economic growth rather than improve quality of life. Given that the MDBs first articulated their education, health, and gender policies in the Washington Consensus era, revising those policies time and again during the post-Washington Consensus and retroliberal eras, the resultant legacy has been to classify people as economic units; human development has come to mean human capital development, which focusses on the utility of people as per their productive capacity. This is problematic, as a neoliberal ‘common sense’ understanding of human capital necessarily constricts the breadth of options available that the MDBs can pursue in promoting human development.
There is, nevertheless, considerable variation between the MDBs in their human development financing. Some have engaged substantively. For example, ‘social services’ was the fourth largest investment sector of the Caribbean Development Bank (CDB) between 1970 and 2000. Similarly, ‘social development’ was one of three strategic axes of the Central American Bank for Economic Integration (CABEI) in 2015, accounting for 38.7% of annual approvals. In contrast, other MDBs, including the International Investment Bank (IIB) and ECO Trade and Development Bank (ETDB), barely reference the concept outside public statements affirming the importance of investing in people for economic growth. Further, a handful of MDBs such as the Development Bank of Central Africa States (BDEAC) and West African Development Bank (BOAD) do not disclose sufficient public information to identify lending patterns, although the limited data suggests very low engagement. A few reasons explain the poor commitment of select MDBs to human development, low engagement of select MDBs with human development, including limits on annual lending capacity, difficulties in assuring loan repayments for social sector investments compared to infrastructural investments, and a lack of borrower interest. Instead of focussing on why certain MDBs do not invest substantively in education, health, and gender, this chapter analyses those that do, evaluating how they have done so and the policies that have guided their actions. The remainder of this chapter is divided into four sections. After first reviewing the literature and MDB financing for human development, the chapter turns to education, health, and then gender.

11.1 Human development

Human development entered the lexicon of development in the early 1990s. While initiatives to invest in people first emerged in the 1970s in discussions about poverty and basic needs (Javed Burki and Ul Haq 1981, Streeten 1979), targeted interest in human development was a late Cold War construct (Cammack 2017). Human development posits that people are the ends as well as the means of development, challenging the conventional view of development understood as per capita economic growth (Ponzio and Ghosh 2016). It combines a multidimensional conceptualisation of human well-being and poverty through a focus on the social, cultural, political, and economic institutions which enhance or curtail human capabilities. The works of Streeten (1981), Ul Haq (1995), Sen (1990) were foundational to building the concept. The UNDP’s Human Development Reports, published annually since 1990, were effective in mobilising support from governments, MDBs, and civil society organisations, though they did maintain an understanding of people as units in economic growth (Cammack 2017).
The World Bank played a leading role in shaping human development in multilateral development finance (MDF), comparable to its role in other debates about development. During the Bank’s early decades, it minimised social sector financing, prioritising a growth-only agenda via macroeconomic reforms and capital inputs to achieve economic growth (Ponzio and Ghosh 2016). Between the 1950s and 1960s, the Bank and others argued that investment in social development, including education and healthcare, was counterproductive and ‘would only squander limited national resources’ (Kapur, Webb, and Lewis 1997, 115). Robert Cavanaugh, the Bank’s liaison between the MDB and Wall Street during the 1950s and 1960s, said,
If we got into the social field… then the bond market would definitely feel that we were not acting prudently from a financial standpoint… If you start financing schools and hospitals and water works, and so forth, these things don’t normally and directly increase the ability of a country to repay a borrowing.
(cited in Kapur, Webb, and Lewis 1997, 119–20)
It was not until 1968 and the appointment of President Robert McNamara that the Bank shifted from a financial institution to a ‘development agency’ (Bazbauers 2018). McNamara, supported by the Bank’s first Chief Economist, Keynesian development economist Hollis Chenery, argued that in the absence of redistribution of growth, socioeconomic development would be stymied by inequality (Ponzio and Ghosh 2016). From McNamara onwards, three broad anti-poverty initiatives emerged from the Bank: anti-poverty as welfare, as protection, and as social development.
Anti-poverty as welfare came in the form of investments in education, health, population planning, and nutrition, and they occupied a third of the World Bank’s loan portfolio between 1968 and 1981. The rationale behind these investments was that enhancing human capital – or the ability of people to perform labour – would increase economic growth, a basic needs tenet (Hall 2007, Hunter and Brown 2000). With the arrival of President Tom Clausen in 1981, the Bank altered its a poverty strategy by aligning it with the nascent Washington Consensus. As a result, it became a model to limit welfare budgets and encourage private sector participation in and delivery of welfare services. There was thus a shift to short-term welfare projects with a productivity objective, rationalised by the human capital principle that investments in education and health would enhance productivity and lead to higher economic growth (Hall 2007).
Yet, the severe social consequences of neoliberal structural adjustment exposed problems in the neoliberal poverty strategy and, buttressed by UNICEF’s 1987 call for ‘adjustment with a human face’ (Jolly 1991), the Bank under President Barber Conable introduced moderate pro-welfare, anti-poverty measures and built minimalist safety nets using social emergency programmes and social action funds (Hall 2007). The Bank’s World Development Report 1990: Poverty and World Development Report 2000: Attacking Poverty highlight the operational shift, with the former focussed on poverty reduction through enhanced productivity and economic growth and the latter on empowerment via pro-poor institutions and the introduction of insurance mechanisms such as cash transfers to protect against risks and shocks (World Bank 2000), which informed the Bank’s anti-poverty as protection initiative.
Anti-poverty as protection was a response to criticisms posed of neoliberal adjustment. In many developing countries, welfare provision services bore the brunt of public spending cutbacks, exacerbating poverty and vulnerability (Thomson, Kentikelenis, and Stubbs 2017). In belated response, the World Bank drafted programmes to support safety net mechanisms, coming in the forms of conditional income transfers, food programmes, price subsidies, microcredits, and fee waivers for health services. These fell under the responsibility of the Social Protection Unit launched in the Bank’s Human Development Network in 2001. Conditional cash transfer (CCT) programmes, as a social protection measure, started in Latin America in the late 1990s and became popular with the MDBs only in the late 2000s once their poverty reduction impacts could not be ignored. They preferred minimalist programmes that still posited development as an individual responsibility before that, in particular the very problematic intervention of microfinance (Bateman 2010, Engel and Pedersen 2019, Mader 2015). The main objectives of CCTs are short-term poverty alleviation via cash transfers and long-term alleviation through human capital development (Barham, Maluccio, and Stampini 2018). The core difference between anti-poverty as welfare and as protection is in the more targeted approach of the latter (Fiszbein 2009). Nonetheless, both approaches align on the premise that enhanced human capital will lead to increased economic growth.
Anti-poverty as social development dates to social components integrated in World Bank projects approved in the 1970s but became increasingly institutionalised from the late 1990s following the advent of the post-Washington Consensus. In 1996, Bank President James Wolfensohn established a Social Development Task Force to measure the social impact of the Bank’s lending operations. This was a reply to the ‘Fifty Years is Enough’ campaign as well as Wolfensohn’s personal commitment to enhancing participatory development (Hall 2007). It provided an important context for the adoption of Poverty Reduction Strategy Papers as a Bank tool as well as new conditional lending modalities, such as development policy loans replacing structural adjustment loans in 2004. Whereas anti-poverty as welfare and protection were temporary solutions pursued through emergency financing, anti-poverty as social development focussed on social analysis inputs to support such initiatives as community-driven development, participation and civic engagement, culture, indigenous peoples, conflict prevention, youth, and social accountability during the post-Washington Consensus era. This was a more holistic approach than pure neoliberalism, though still a largely instrumentalist and constrained approach to human welfare (Cammack 2012, Carroll 2010). Whereas Bank social development lending accounted for 5.1% of annual lending in 1980, human development lending totalled 14% in 2015. While still a rather modest commitment, the elevated importance of human development is visible.
The current MDB discourse on human development prioritises human capital development. Entering the mainstream of development assistance in the 1990s (Gundlach 1996), the concept of human capital rose to prominence in the 2000s and 2010s as part of a reconfiguration of welfare, poverty, employment, and market competition. The Asian Development Bank (AsDB), Council of Europe Development Bank (CEB), European Investment Bank (EIB), Inter-American Development Bank (IADB), Nordic Investment Bank (NIB), and World Bank all promote human capital growth as human development. EIB regards education and health investments as necessary to building a competitive and diversified workforce, CEB in 2005 allocated 13.4% of sectoral approvals to human capital development, and IADB identifies labour market insertion – or measures to improve employability and reduce barriers to labour market entry – as a human development sub-sector. For NIB, ‘human capital and equal economic opportunities’ are one of four institutional drivers, arguing that the ‘quality of labour inputs and accumulation of knowledge stock in an economy boost productivity in a similar manner as accumulation and improved quality of physical capital’ (Normann 2019). For AsDB, human capital investments have ‘intrinsic value,’ being ‘regarded at the macro level as a key factor of production in the economywide production function’ (Son 2010, 2). Lastly, the World Bank established the Human Capital Project in 2017 as a research and policy toolkit and the World Development Report 2019: The Changing Nature of Work argues that enhanced human capital is a prerequisite for sustained, inclusive economic growth (World Bank 2019). Thus, a persistent focus on human development remains, but it is pursued as investments in human capital; a means to invest in people to ensure long-term economic growth (Rossi 2018).

11.2 Education

The right to education was accepted as a fundamental human right under the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in 1948. Yet, in international development assistance, education as a sectoral investment stream has a rocky history, with the definition of education fluidly evolving over time and annual financial commitments fluctuating greatly. During the 1950s and 1960s, education investments prioritised technical and vocational education – skills-based approaches then deemed necessary to supplement rapid infrastructural growth (Kenneth 2015). Education policy, development, and funding in low-income countries were aligned under the modernisation paradigm (Cox 1968). The newly formed discipline of the economics of education emerged and ushered in a wave of optimism about the role of education in facilitating economic growth. The optimism for skills training and development was short-lived, however, when target-driven plans for education in Africa did not generate expected economic outcomes (Kenneth 2015). By the 1980s, education priorities shifted for many MDBs following the neoliberal turn, as deregulation and privatisation eroded public education programmes. It was not until the 1990 World Conference on Education for All that the rationale for education development reverted to its human rights origins, reinforced by the 2000 launch of the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) and the 2015 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). A key part of this change was the turn away from user fees for primary education. However, even after the MDGs, MDB education investments remained small. Further, sectoral investments have largely aligned with human capital understandings, with education positioned as a contributing factor to improving workforces, employability, and competitive economic growth as opposed to improving quality of life.
The World Bank’s education programme began in 1962, with a project approved to Tunisia to finance middle and secondary school building construction. Over the next two decades, education lending comprised a very small percentage of annual approvals – in 1970, of $2.2 billion approved, the Bank allocated $79.9 million to education projects, prioritising construction and equipment financing for secondary and vocational schools as well as curriculum studies. Education was viewed instrumentally, in terms of relevance to economic growth: ‘further education and training beyond the basic level should be provided selectively to improve the knowledge and skills needed to carry out economic, social, and other developmental roles’ (World Bank 1975, 18).
From the Washington Consensus era onwards, World Bank education lending increased but remained a small portion of annual approvals, 6.3% in 1985 and 7.25% in 1990. By 1995, the Bank’s education portfolio grew to include initiatives to promote equitable access to education for girls and indigenous children, while maintaining a strong focus on vocational skills training. The post-Washington Consensus and retroliberal eras under the MDGs and SDGs saw little change though emphasised the public savings generated by contracting out textbook and supply services to private providers in another example of the marketisation agenda. Throughout the 2000s and 2010s, annual education approvals averaged 8% of total annual approvals and committed to ‘interventions to help ensure that education builds relevant skills to meet labour market demands’ (World Bank 2010, 16); a human capital agenda.
The regional development banks (RDBs) largely followed the World Bank’s lead, although the two European RDBs – the EIB and European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD) – have been far less active. This reflects the fact that in most European countries the state plays an active role in education and provides the bulk of primary and secondary education – the MDBs do not need to encourage investment. The EIB, after decades of little engagement with the sector, approved projects in the 2000s and 2010s for primary, secondary, tertiary, and vocational education, but at a small percentage of annual approvals; 1.3% in 2000 and 5.5% in 2015. Despite comprising only 5.5% of 2015 approvals, that figure was €4.3 billion, larger as a net amount compared to the other RDBs. Building on the European Union’s 2020 Strategy, EIB’s current education portfolio prioritises those areas that promote market competitiveness and economic sustainability.
IADB and AsDB have financed education projects far longer than their European counterparts. Between 1960 and 1995, IADB approved 153 education loans totalling $2.8 billion, just 3.6% of the $78 billion allocated across 2,248 loans. This was maintained in 2005 but increased to 7.2% in 2010; it has since maintained an average comparable to the World Bank. Its approach to education sees a balance between human development and human capital development, with projects over the 2010s supporting equitable education access and adequate school supplies as well as building labour market skills. For AsDB, vocational and higher education loans totalled just over 1% of annual approvals between 1966 and 1975, delivered mostly as small loan components rather than stand-alone projects. By 1990, however, AsDB (1990, 33–4) shifted its approach ‘from a rather narrow human capital rationale, typified by an almost exclusive emphasis on technical and vocational education, to an approach that embraces the entire sector, including primary, non-formal and environmental education.’ During the 2000s, its approach to education drew heavily on new communication technologies and private service delivery. By 2010, education investments amounted to 10% of annual approvals, with AsDB arguing that projects should be oriented to skills training and employability, a return to its human capital approach of the 1970s.
Of the sub-regional development bank (SRDBs), only three devote significant resources to education: CABEI, CDB, and the Development Bank of Latin America (CAF). FONPLATA also finances education projects, but publicly available information is limited and, in 2019, health and education projects collectively totalled 4% of new commitments, compared to 63% for logistics and transportation. Thus, the SRDBs most active in education financing all operate in Latin America. Yet, the sector still comprises a small percentage of annual and cumulative approvals. CABEI approved $24.8 million in education investments in 2018 of $2.44 billion in new approvals and, between 1970 and 2018, CDB allocated $707.7 million to the sector of a total of $5.2 billion, around 13.6%. CDB’s education portfolio covers a broad agenda, ranging from human development projects that improve access to quality early childhood development and teacher effectiveness to human capital projects that ensure training is relevant to increased workforce participation and promote gender-equitable access to non-traditional occupational areas. CAF, while also a relatively small education lender, launched a $140 million education bond in July 2019, reflecting the financialisation agenda. It is guided by its 2018–22 Educational Agenda that details three strategic guidelines: increased access to education, improved education quality, and strengthened education relevance, drawing connections between education providers, skills development, and employability (CAF 2018); again demonstrating an instrumental focus around human capital development.
Education investments by the specialised development banks also exist on a spectrum: (i) not financing education projects, as is the case with the newly formed New Development Bank (NDB) and Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB) – the closest connection made by the NDB has been in noting that transportation projects enable greater access to health, education, and social services; (ii) financing small-scale education projects, such as NIB supporting the construction of a multipurpose education facility in Rovaniemi, northern Finland, in 2015; and, (iii) relatively active lenders, such as the CEB and Arab-led MDBs. To illustrate, 8.77% of CEB’s approvals between 1989 and 1997 were for education sector projects, a considerable portion given that its two largest sectors were social housing (15.9%) and rural modernisation (14.4%). In 2015, however, the sector comprised only 1.5% of new approvals. With the refugee crisis taking off in that year, it appears other investments were prioritised. Overall, CEB mirrors other MDBs in that agreed projects are informed by the human capital tenet that an educated and skilled workforce improves a country’s market competitiveness.
The four Arab-led MDBs have all financed education projects, though there are differences between them. Between 1974 and 2018, the Arab Fund for Economic and Social Development (AFESD) and Arab Bank for Economic Development in Africa (BADEA) allocated 7% and 10.4% of the cumulative approvals to the social sector, which is a broad field including the health, education, housing, and microfinance sub-sectors. To illustrate priorities, BADEA in 2018 approved a school construction education project, indicative of its infrastructure-focussed portfolio. For the OPEC Fund for International Development (OFID), new education sector approvals amounted to 2% of annual approvals in 2010 and 2.9% in 2018, with projects financing school facility upgrades, again suggesting an infrastructure-based approach, like AFESD and BADEA. The approach of the Islamic Development Bank (IsDB) has been more comprehensive. While only 3.2% of its total approvals between 1975 and 2019 were for education, its core education priorities in the 2010s were universal basic education, science and mathematics education for technological advancement, vocational training to enhance youth employability, and non-formal education through vocational literacy programmes for employability (IsDB Group 2016); a combination of human development and human capital investments.
In summary, statistical studies of the MDBs reveal them to be bit players in the education field (Morozkina and Sabelnikova 2017), prioritising human capital development by financing infrastructural upgrades and mechanisms to improve employability. While sectoral investments have expanded in recent decades, causes for concern remain, particularly since the MDBs tend to finance ad hoc projects that do not adequately address systemic issues related to educational inequalities and ineffective service provision.

11.3 Health

Health was an early arena of international cooperation, predating MDF. International Sanitary Conferences started in 1851 and the League of Nations had a Health Organisation. Since 1948, the World Health Organization (WHO) has been the main international health body promoting and protecting health globally (McInnes 2015). During the Keynesian, welfare, modernisation era, there was intense advocacy for strong government intervention to overcome systemic health impediments, including poverty, unequal access to health services, and assuring high quality care (Zwi and Mills 1995). However, once health became a sectoral focus for the MDBs, its conceptualisation – as with education – fluctuated between a rights-based focus on improving quality of life for the poor and a human capital-focus on ensuring a healthy, working population to bolster economic growth (Sastry and Dutta 2013). In evaluating World Bank health projects, Noy (2018, 17) identified that project rationales vary greatly, from prioritising market efficiencies to equity and social development, and that they are shaped in part by the strategies and goals of borrowers.
Like education, the health sector has remained at the periphery of MDB action, particularly when compared to infrastructure investments. The World Bank did not adopt a formal health policy until 1974. Prior to that, the Bank’s focus was limited to financing health components in projects addressing other sectors and the odd project on population or family planning. By the end of the Keynesian era, the Bank’s (1980) engagement in the sector was limited to addressing life expectancy, mortality rates, and temporary illnesses that impacted worker output; its understanding of health was technical and instrumental, a factor potentially impeding economic growth.
With the neoliberal turn, the World Bank maintained its instrumental outlook but added in the privatisation and decentralisation of healthcare. A Bank (1987) policy paper argued that the main problems facing the health sector in developing countries were poor resource allocation, wasteful(!) public health programmes, and inequitable service distribution. Solutions included introducing user fees and private insurance as well as privatising public health facilities. The Bank did envisage a residual public system for the poor, though circumscribed by a very limited definition of public health goods: basic preventative healthcare, infectious and easily transmittable diseases, and basic maternal and child health services; essential, low-cost, high-return interventions. The focus on efficiency and limiting state expenditures had little consideration of the catastrophic effect of degrading healthcare, which was to be greatly exacerbated by the emerging HIV/AIDS crisis in the 1980s (Birn 2009, Thomson, Kentikelenis, and Stubbs 2017). Other MDBs such as the AfDB and AsDB followed the Bank’s lead, adopting similar health policy prescriptions (Coburn, Restivo, and Shandra 2015, Larmour 2002). By 1990, Bank health sector financing equalled just 4.5% of annual approvals, with sector priorities being healthcare modernisation, immunisation programmes, and construction, equipment, and training.
The graphic consequences of a lack of healthcare support in developing countries was part of what drove the shift to the post-Washington Consensus, which promised a slightly friendlier approach to public health but delivered little. Even the MDGs did not stop the push for the further privatisation of health services. The MDGs listed three health-related goals: reduce child mortality, improve maternal health, and combat HIV/AIDS, malaria, and other diseases (Komatsu, Low-Beer, and Schwartländer 2007). Influential in setting MDF targets, the World Bank and other MDBs aligned their health sector investments behind the MDGs (Roth et al. 2018). Alongside the MDGs, the 2002 establishment of the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis, and Malaria helped centre HIV/AIDS as the priority of global health investments during the 2000s (Lisk 2009). Funding commitments for HIV/AIDS increased from $300 million in 1996 to $8.8 billion in 2011. The focus on HIV/AIDS transformed the narrative from health-to-development to AIDS-to-development, leading to a seismic shift in investment priorities away from broader health issues to narrower, target-led goals (Dennis and Zuckerman 2008, Woodling, Williams, and Rushton 2012). Further, the HIV/AIDS epidemic was narrowly framed as a loss of labour potential, fitting neoliberal the ‘common sense’ of viewing health as a factor in economic productivity, and ‘limiting the range of articulations and actions in response to HIV/AIDS’ (Sastry and Dutta 2013, 36). Particularly relevant is that HIV/AIDS programmes have been criticised as inadequately addressing gender dynamics, with ‘fundamentally patriarchal’ programmes placing ‘women in the global South at increased risk of HIV/AIDS’ (Sastry and Dutta 2013, 36) and often focussing on maternal health issues above ‘reproductive and sexual health rights more broadly’ (Dennis and Zuckerman 2008, 298).
The RDBs all finance health projects, except for the EBRD, which does not have a health sector investment stream but rather references ‘public health benefits’ as components of loan appraisals. Annual RDB health sector approvals are low, however. In 2019, the World Bank allocated 7.6% of annual approvals ($3.41 billion) to health sector projects, EIB allocated 2.3%, AfDB 2.4%, and IADB and AsDB 3%. These percentages fluctuate, with IADB health sector approvals at 8% in 2015 and 0.2% in 2000. Cumulative EIB health sector investments were €30 billion over its several-decade history, a relatively small amount considering that it approved €69 billion across all sectors in 2018. Thus, although a sector promoted by the World Bank and RDBs as vital to human development, financing levels are low. The AfDB, AsDB, and IADB prioritise investments for communicable diseases, rural and urban health, hospital management, and public-private healthcare delivery. Following a pattern, EIB ‘has implicitly taken the view that health is mostly equivalent to healthcare. Indeed, it has concentrated its financing mainly on the creation or improvement of hospital infrastructure’ (Schmidt, Moreno, and Goodwin 2007, ii); an infrastructure-based human development approach.
In the sub-regional and specialised development banks, project data and annual approvals reveal the health sector to be a low priority, with many not investing at all. There are, however, exceptions, notably IsDB and CEB. IsDB invested 2.9% of its cumulative lending portfolio in the health sector between 1975 and 2019, with health projects approved during the 2010s prioritising two areas: prevention and control of communicable and non-communicable diseases and health system strengthening to improve healthcare quality and access (IsDB Group 2016). These areas reflect the post-Washington consensus agenda, with the specific diseases used to limit expenditures and the governance focus to ensure technocratic efficiencies. CEB allocated 12.1% of its 2015 approvals to the health sector, predominantly co-financing projects for modernising and increasing accessibility to medical infrastructure. Other MDBs that invest only marginally in the sector include BADEA, which approved only one health project in 2018 – financing the construction of buildings at a hospital site in Mauritius. In 2018, OFID invested 5% of new approvals in the health sector ($140.5 million), focussing on high-burden diseases (HIV/AIDS) and non-communicable diseases (cancer and diabetes), again demonstrating a disease-specific focus rather than a comprehensive approach to public services.
The initial response of the MDBs to COVID-19 between March and May 2020 was quick and large. Most MDBs devised COVID-19 response plans, ranging from short-term financing during the initial outbreak to long-term programmes. The World Bank Group allocated $160 billion to address health, economic, and social shocks experienced over 2020 and 2021 and the RDBs provided a further $80 billion. Priorities were medical supplies, transmission surveillance, and clinical care facilities, as well as direct cash transfers to the poor and vulnerable. A few also utilised previously established pandemic financing mechanisms, such as the World Bank’s Pandemic Emergency Financing Facility founded in July 2017 to address to the 2014–15 West Africa Ebola outbreak and EIB’s Infectious Disease Financing Facility that held a €5 billion project pipeline in mid-2020. Emerging financing arrangements were also approved by many sub-regional and specialised MDBs, including CABEI, CAF, CDB, IsDB, NIB, and the Black Sea Trade and Development Bank (BSTDB). Though smaller in size than the World Bank and RDBs, they demonstrated the capacity of MDBs to quickly allocate emergency finance. Even MDBs that would not normally finance the health sector, like AIIB, drafted pandemic response plans.
COVID-19 revealed the dominance of the retroliberal development agenda. Even with a global pandemic and many countries facing unmanageable levels of debt, there was no expansion of grant resources for the Global South. Indeed, the US cut funding to WHO. The MDBs, development finance institutions (DFIs), and some Chinese funds were the main financing mechanisms available to many countries. Indeed, there was a very large outflow of private capital from the Global South. Further, MDB finance was used to plug holes in health systems that had been eroded over decades. Public systems are underfunded, have not focussed sufficiently on pandemic preparedness, and have no spare capacity thanks to the relentless neoliberal focus on efficiency. We expect to see MDB funds propping up private health providers in developing countries as they are facing financial stress and crisis (Williams 2020). This is because many of their most profitable procedures are either being delayed by patients or governments or because insurance schemes are struggling due to the level of claims and the associated economic disruptions meaning many people cannot pay their insurance premiums.

11.4 Gender

The nexus between women, gender, and development has long been a point of contest. Until very recently, the MDBs have largely sidestepped substantive recognition of the significance of women to and gendered inequalities in development. This is not to suggest that they have not engaged with gender, but rather that such issues have been treated as incidental. There are serious questions about whether MDB gender actions genuinely address inequalities or whether they are more rhetorical responses to expectations that they should be part of an international organisation’s operations (Aasen 2009).
Many MDBs over the 2010s published reports discussing gender dynamics in their organisations, including attempts to better manage corporate diversity. The AfDB released an internal gender equality strategy in 2014 that detailed male to female employment ratios and promotion opportunities as well as guidelines for an inclusive workplace. In 2018, CEB enacted a gender equality strategy for organisational reform. Such reforms are important, for they expose and attempt to redress gendered biases. To illustrate, EIB has a severe gender imbalance for internal promotions, with ‘numerous complaints by female employees in recent years about the lack of gender diversity and career advancement for women and a generally misogynistic culture at the bank’ (De La Baume 2017). The EIB’s lack of gender diversity is not unique, with most MDBs disproportionately staffed by men, particularly in management positions, from presidents to senior leadership to members of boards of governors and executive directors. This topic is important and would benefit from detailed future research, but our focus in this chapter is on MDB lending operations.
Since the 1970s, three primary perspectives on women, gender, and development have evolved: women in development (WID), women and development (WAD), and gender and development (GAD). Each of the gender-based paradigms occupies a different space in development discourse, offering alternate accounts of the impacts of and solutions to reduce inequalities for women (and men), while being unified in encouraging greater participation and empowerment of women, and stressing the harm of gender exclusion to progressive societal change (Momsen 2004). For the MDBs, actions to address gendered inequalities began with the adoption of WID policies in the 1980s but more recently shifted towards a neoliberal variant of GAD; WAD has not entered the policy documents or lending operations of the MDBs.
WID was the first academic paradigm drawing women into development discourse, led by Ester Boerup’s (1970) Women’s Role in Economic Development. Adopting a liberal feminist lens, WID challenged modernisation theory’s exclusion of women from development. The paradigm was criticised, however, for its oversimplification of women’s work and role in society and its argument that legal and administrative reforms were sufficient to address systemic gendered inequalities (Pearson and Jackson 1998, Rathgeber 1990, see Benería and Sen 1981, Tinker 1990). Next, WAD came from neo-Marxist feminist analyses in the mid-1970s to address WID’s limitations. It asserts that women have always been important economic actors and their work inside and outside the household has been critical to the maintenance of (capitalist) society (Pala 1977). WAD regards the perpetuation of dependency and global inequalities as the key determinant facing developing countries and women in those societies (Rathgeber 1990), and attention thus focusses on the unequal gendered impact of modernisation (Griffin 2009). It has been criticised for inadequate attention to social and patriarchal divisions between men and women and for discounting the intersectionality of other identities which perpetuate inequalities between women themselves (Parpart 1993). Lastly, GAD emerged in the 1980s as a response to silences in WID and WAD. It represents a more holistic approach and ‘is not concerned with women per se but with the social construction of gender and the assignment of specific roles, responsibilities, and expectations to women and to men’ (Rathgeber 1990, 494); the focus turns to the relationships between women and men and the impact different gendered roles have on development. For GAD, development is multidimensional and is not merely the process of integrating women into productive labour but to ensuring women and men benefit from development.
Beginning in the 1980s, the MDBs slowly began to incorporate ‘gender’ into their operations. Unlike education and health, ‘women’ and ‘gender’ for the MDBs are not investment sectors but rather issues that are taken into consideration when drafting loan agreements. While gender mainstreaming has made progress over the past few decades, it remains a less than important issue for most MDBs; common statements appear as ‘it is our aim to embed gender issues in all that we do’ as opposed to committing substantive resources to addressing gendered inequalities. Since the 1980s, three phases demarcate MDB gender strategies: mid-1980s to mid-1990s and WID, mid-1990s to mid-2000s and GAD, and 2010s (OVE 2018). The 2010s are particularly significant not only because they have seen an integration of WID and GAD approaches but also because this was when many of the smaller MDBs began engaging with gender issues.
Between 1984 and 1990, the World Bank, IADB, AfDB, and AsDB introduced their initial WID policies, prioritising actions that increased women’s access to employment, markets, education, and material resources (OVE 2018). These policies predated initiatives by other MDBs by at least a decade. Still, projects were often small. For example, under its 1984 WID Gender Directive, the World Bank approved a $7.5 million project to the Gambia to improve women’s economic productivity and income earning potential. IADB pursued a WID agenda in projects as early as 1978, but it published its first WID policy in 1987 that focussed on economic growth and poverty alleviation for women through increased access to credit, microenterprise loans, and institutional support for women entrepreneurs. Microfinance, as noted earlier, is very problematic as a poverty reduction programme and its construction of gender relations and women’s empowerment has been the subject of powerful critiques (Karim 2011, Yeboah et al. 2015). Still, it remains a preferred policy for many MDBs. Turning to AfDB, in 1987 it established a WID Unit designed to evaluate WID components in the project cycle. AsDB’s approach mirrored the World Bank and other RDBs, with an economistic and growth-oriented WID model. Reflecting Japanese government hostility to gender equality action, between 1992 and 1995 the Asian RDB only approved two WID projects – it had a policy but little substantive action.
By the mid-1990s, the WID policies of the larger MDBs were replaced by new strategies aligned more with a neoliberal GAD paradigm; it was more holistic in addressing social, political, and economic structures but favoured market-centric responses. The World Bank (2000) outlined a revised Gender Sector Strategy with measures addressing barriers to women and men gaining equal access to credit, education, and healthcare. For IADB, in 1995 then Executive Vice President Nancy Birdsall outlined an initiative to ensure that half of projects had gender components by 2000, which was followed in 2003 by a Gender Mainstreaming Action Plan to increase the volume of loans, country strategies, and advisory services targeting gender issues. AfDB turned towards GAD in its 2001 Gender Policy that sought to embed gender as a crosscutting issue alongside the environment, civil society participation, and institutional development and AsDB’s 1998 Policy on Gender and Development proposed a GAD-oriented roadmap, broadly focussing on mainstreaming gender in lending operations as well as setting the foundations for a multi-donor Gender and Development Cooperation Fund launched in 2003.
The World Bank, IADB, AfDB, and AsDB again drafted new gender policy documents in the 2010s, merging WID and GAD into ‘twin-track strategies’ (OVE 2018, 8). The World Bank Group’s (2015) current 2016–23 Gender Equality Strategy prioritises closing education and health inequalities, removing employment and land ownership obstacles for women, and enhancing women’s voice and agency. IADB’s (2019) 2019–21 Diversity Action Plan for Operations lists five action lines: agenda setting and policy development, operational mainstreaming, direct investment, knowledge and dissemination, and technological innovation. In 2010, AfDB unveiled a Gender and Social Development Monitoring Division, followed by a new Gender Strategy in 2014 and a Gender Marker System in 2018 to track gender mainstreaming in its operations. Prügl (2017, 47) describes such initiatives as
the reconstruction of neoliberalism so that it dons a feminist face. This reconstruction preserves the privileges of private enterprise, individual responsibility, and the wisdom of the market while recognising the inequality-producing force of institutional biases, informal, norms, unequal endowments, and power relations in the household and society.
In 2010, EBRD implemented the first phase of its Gender Action Plan, which was approved in May 2009, drafted to support equal opportunity and to promote women entrepreneurship. The Gender Action Plan was replaced in 2015 by the Strategy for the Promotion of Gender Equality 2016–20, which Shields and Wallin (2015) and Shields (2020) regard as proposing a neoliberal ‘business case’ for gender mainstreaming, framing women as individualistic workers operating in a market economy; a clear articulation of human capital development as opposed to human development.
For the SRDBs and specialised development banks, available data reveals considerably less substantive gender interventions than the World Bank and RDBs. There are, without question, a few MDBs with robust diversity policies that have been translated into action, including CDB and IFAD, while others have not developed clear gender policies, such as the Russian- and Arab-led MDBs, with the notable exception of IsDB. The NDB and AIIB to date have not financed gender projects, although they have disseminated boilerplate press releases stating the importance of women to development. Other MDBs have only very recently engaged with gender and therefore details remain scant, such as IsDB and its Women’s Empowerment Policy published in February 2019, which is designed to mainstream gender in its lending, analytical work, and organisational practices. Similarly, CAF created a Gender Equity and Inclusion Unit in 2015 to provide gender oversight in projects, but only began applying gender safeguards to its entire loan portfolio in 2019.
As a gender leader, CDB introduced gender mainstreaming in 2005, with initiatives to incorporate gender analyses into lending operations, develop gender analytical frameworks and indicators as part of country surveys, and improve gender equality awareness. In December 2008, it approved a Gender Equality Policy, followed by its 2015 Gender Equality Policy and Operational Strategy. In 2015, 57% of its projects included gender components and between 2012 and 2015 it conducted ten Country Gender Assessments of member countries. CDB thus appears to be one of the most progressive MDBs, prioritising economic empowerment for women. Similarly, IFAD has a long gender history, almost rivalling the World Bank and RDBs. This history dates to 1992 when it led the Summit on the Economic Advancement of Rural Women that argued WID approaches to be ineffectual in addressing systemic issues leading to gendered inequalities in development (IFAD 2018). In 1998, IFAD published its Lending Policies and Criteria that directed operational resources to poor rural women and in 2012 it approved the Gender Equality and Women’s Empowerment Policy. This policy focussed on economic empowerment for rural women, equal voice for women and men in rural organisations, and more equitable workloads between women and men, pursued through country programmes and projects, advocacy initiatives, and public and private sector capacity building. In 2016, IFAD’s Strategic Framework 2016–25 identified gender equality as one of five guiding principles, shaping not only lending operations but also internal organisational practices. Like CDB, IFAD is a very progressive MDB.
The approach taken by the MDBs to women and gender in development is largely one that regards gendered inequalities as objects that can be ‘fixed’ through the application of the right resources. While attempts to mainstream gender actions and support women’s empowerment and inclusion are essential pursuits by the MDBs, the broad approach taken is one that focusses on economic opportunities through resource investment, without significantly addressing entrenched cultural values and norms that maintain structural gender imbalances;
failure to acknowledge the role of neoliberal economics – privatization and deregulation – and depoliticization in consolidating social inequalities leads to the World Bank advocating a developmental paradigm that will perpetuate poverty and thus gender inequality.
(Power 2020, 29)

11.5 Conclusion

There has been a lot written about the World Bank’s role in human development interventions, and a little about some of the other MDBs. We cannot do justice to the literature in this chapter. There are many important qualitative analyses of MDB-funded development interventions that shed light on the nature of human development, capabilities, and participation. There is important work on the rights of indigenous people, minority groups, the disabled, and more that we do not have the space to engage with here. Our focus was to provide an overview of the ideas and policies that shaped MDB actions in a few key areas. We demonstrated that human development is a difficult space for the MDBs, as they were conceived to fund profitable projects with clear quantitative returns on investment. As they shifted from their banking focus towards development, they could not ignore the field and did not want to as it gave them a whole new arena to finance. Still, their engagement with human development is restrained, despite influencing global discourse on education, health, and gender. Ultimately, their approach to human development is framed by neoliberalism. It is thus not surprising that, in education, the human capital approach of creating productive workers trumps cultivating educated citizens with rights. In health, the MDBs still extoll market forces despite decades of failures and inefficiencies associated with private healthcare provision. And, in gender, we see the agenda of ‘fixing women’ to ensure that they are more efficient economic units. Still, the chapter highlighted some differences between the MDBs, and such subtleties matter, particularly when considering whether to invest in people to improve their productive capacity or to improve quality of life.

12 Conclusion

We set ourselves a difficult task in writing a single volume about 30 multilateral development banks (MDBs) operating in every corner of the globe over the past 80 years. Undoubtedly, in this breadth we have missed important details and writing a simple conclusion about the complex and interdependent architecture of MDF is daunting. And, yet, the breadth of the volume has, we think, been useful in unearthing many questions, particularly those highlighted by the title of the book. Are the MDBs a system? What is the relationship between the MDBs, financial markets, and debt? And what kind of vision of development do the MDBs promote? After we respond to these three questions, this concluding chapter briefly considers a future research agenda for the MDBs before turning to how COVID-19 is demonstrating the continued significance of the development banks and what we need to be reviewing about them in the context of a global pandemic.

12.1 The MDBs as a system

The book demonstrates that the 30 MDBs largely have the same lineage. They evolved from the post-Second World War Keynesian, modernisation, welfare project that sought to tame capitalism but relied on capitalist tools to do so, such as banks in the form of the MDBs. Mandate, structure, and governance similarities illustrated in Chapters 3–5 makes it evident that the MDBs can be regarded as a system. The establishment of the IDA in the World Bank and not in the UN was a key turning point in the history of development finance, promoting a banking model for the MDBs over a cooperative focus (Shaw 2005). Still, between 1946 and 1979, the MDBs followed the Keynesian formula of promoting balanced investment and economic development, with an expansion of that agenda to include social, regional, and sub-regional development. Even MDBs created in and by the Global South, which emerged from an anti-colonial and generally developmentalist agenda, largely followed the same model. With the rise of neoliberalism, there came the first neoliberal MDB, the EBRD, with its focus on creating market-oriented economies and multi-party democracies. Many other MDBs became partners in the neoliberal paradigm or had to admit new shareholders to accommodate the changing exigencies of the global financial system.
Chapter 5 demonstrated that all the MDBs basically follow the organisational blueprint laid down by the World Bank, with variations emerging due to differences in capital, shareholders, capabilities, and mandates. There is good evidence that the World Bank, RDBs, and a handful other MDBs have followed each other in establishing accountability mechanisms, safeguard policies, and anti-corruption mechanisms (Komori 2016, Park 2014, 2017). Several international development initiatives also align the MDBs, including the 2005 Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative and a few OECD Development Assistance Committee (DAC) proposals, for example, the Aid Effectiveness Agenda, which involved 13 of the then 28 MDBs. The MDBs are thus networked in a myriad of ways, though the larger MDBs are more networked than most of the smaller ones.
One main networking and cooperation forum for the MDBs is the Annual and Spring IMF and World Bank Group Meetings. There are also regular Heads of MDBs and IMF Meetings, which include the AfDB, AsDB, EBRD, EIB, IADB, IsDB, World Bank, and IMF. In 2016, the BRICS-led NDB and China-led AIIB joined these meetings. Further, the MDBs have issued joint statements on a range of issues over the past two decades, particularly on the Millennium and Sustainable Development Goals and the 2015 Paris Agreement. And there have been and are a range of formal and informal working groups on specific topics, for example:
  • Heads of procurement from at least ten MDBs have been meeting since 2000 to enhance their work;
  • The Evaluation Cooperation Group was established between the World Bank and AfDB, AsDB, EBRD, and IADB in 1996 and since then the evaluation groups of the BSTDB, EIB, IFAD, IMF, and IsDB have joined;
  • Several MDBs and the International Development Finance Club (IDFC) jointly agreed to the Common Principles for Climate Adaptation Finance Tracking in March 2015; and,
  • The Global Infrastructure Forum was launched in 2016, as was a platform to coordinate on economic migration and forced displacement which included seven MDBs.
The World Federation of Development Finance Institutions was established in 1979 to promote more efficient development banking, increase opportunities for development banks, and expand coordination. It is made up of four regional groupings, which all have RDBs and SRDBs as special members and other DFIs (including national development banks and export-import banks) as ordinary members. The African association includes the five functional African SRDBs as special members and the IBRD and IFC as honorary members. The Asia Pacific grouping’s 117 members include AsDB, PIDB, the IsDB Group’s Islamic Corporation for the Development of the Private Sector (ICD), and the IIB. The Latin American group’s 80 members include IADB, CABEI, CAF, FONPLATA Development Bank, and EIB. There is also a special grouping for IsDB members.
The IDFC is another coordinating body for the MDBs. It was established in 2011 to help DFIs set agendas on development finance and share know-how. Today it has 26 members including six SRDBs – BOAD, BSTDB, CABEI, CAF, IIB, and TDB – and IsDB through the ICD, along with national development banks from both the Global North and South and one export-import bank; this demonstrates the connections between the MDBs and between the MDBs and other DFIs. It claims to have $4 trillion in combined assets and annual commitments over $850 billion. The IDFC and World Federation of Development Finance Institutions sponsored a joint summit in November 2020 called Finance in Common, which brought together 450 global development institutions.
The MDBs also co-finance each other’s projects; EIB’s first loan in 1958 was co-financed by the IBRD. They in addition coordinate their lending with each other in different ways, a major one being country-level donor coordination groups. Finally, there have been numerous reviews of the MDB system by US and international agencies indicating that the global community regards the MDBs as a networked system, though they do not always cover the full suite of MDBs.

12.2 The MDBs, financial markets, and debt

The first IFIs were created in the wake of a series of crises in the 19th and 20th centuries culminating in the 1929 Great Depression. They were the product of an increasingly globalised network of money and debt built up with the state system. International financial cooperation emerged slowly from the mid-19th century and Chapter 2 identified a range of precedents for MDBs, most specifically a Mexican proposal for a development finance element to be added to the inter-American bank idea, which was discussed on-and-off from the late 1800s. Still, formal IFIs only emerged at the end of the Second World War and they were designed to promote financial openness while controlling the problems that international finance markets had already brought.
The MDBs were designed to ensure that states received the investment flows they needed to promote development but, in setting up the IBRD as a ‘bank,’ Harry Dexter White and John Maynard Keynes inadvertently created an organisation that would not overcome ‘the somewhat violent and not too enlightened motives of capital’ (White cited in Oliver 1975, 97). Or, equally to the point, the MDBs could not overcome the violence of capital in the absence of an orderly system of default for nation-states. Indeed, the MDBs have and continue to provide loans directly – and facilitate private lending for – what the Committee for the Abolition of Illegitimate Debts calls illegitimate, odious, and unsustainable debts. That is to say, the MDBs lend to regimes simply because they control a territory; they loan regardless of a regime’s legitimacy, respect for human rights, or whether the loan benefits the local population (Toussaint 2019). Safeguard policies have been established by the MDBs to limit the latter situation, but the literature is still littered with far too many examples of project-linked human rights failings, which is only likely to increase with the greater infrastructure focus discussed in Chapter 9.
The MDBs also helped financialisation in the 1970s thanks to their promotion of lending to the Global South and, once the 1982 Debt Crisis hit, the World Bank became a major debt-collector for the Global North. After the 1980s, most MDBs became proponents of a neoliberal approach to development, which led to a dramatic increase in financialisation. But this financialised capitalism has not proved to be as super-efficient as its proponents claimed. Rather, it has been prone to crisis, with major debt crises in 1997 and 2008. As outlined in Chapter 4, 2010–20 saw the ‘largest, fastest and most broad-based increase’ in EMDE debt of the past 50 years, even once the impacts of China’s corporate debt bubble were excluded (Kose et al. 2020, 111). This has occurred along with low interest rates and the growth of new financial ‘innovations,’ which were present in the previous three debt crises (Kose et al. 2020). Earlier debt crises tended to be triggered by banking or financial crises. This time the trigger may be the current global pandemic.
Regardless, the book has demonstrated how the MDB system is entwined with debt networks from other DFIs and private finance and that the MDBs play a pivotal role in this system as direct lenders, signallers, facilitators, creators of ‘common sense’ policy frameworks, financial innovators, de-riskers, and more. With the shift to the retroliberal development paradigm, states have been encouraged to take on more and more debt for development, but the development model is a shadow of its former self, mostly just recycling old ideas in a way that sets them on a problematic detour when many should have gone straight to landfill.

12.3 The MDBs and framing development discourse

Most MDBs emerged and grew up with the Keynesian, modernisation, welfare model, but their banking DNA meant that they tended to focus on the modernisation element and, within that, on bankable projects that demonstrated returns to the market. This drive did not disappear even with greater lending for basic needs from the 1970s and certainly not with the emergence of the Washington Consensus in the 1980s. When the human cost became too high, the post-Washington Consensus arrived to renew the palatability of neoliberalism. What followed was a brief progressive interlude, with the Millennium and Sustainable Development Goals targeting poverty alleviation and human development. But the penny-pinching returned with the Beyond Aid initiative, and this fed into the rise of the current retroliberal development agenda. The influence of China can be seen in an old-school focus on infrastructure as facilitating economic growth and an increased emphasis on state sovereignty and concurrent dismantling of human rights-based approaches to development.
Chapters 9 through 11 explored three main areas of MDB operations: infrastructure, climate finance, and human development. Our choice in selecting these areas reflect the fact that Engen and Prizzon (2018) found that in 2015 infrastructure was the largest MDB sector in terms of disbursements, while the World Bank and CDB had the social sector as their largest area of support, with BADEA and IADB also being quite active. Importantly, as revealed by their human development actions and climate interventions, the MDBs tend to finance social and environmental programmes that promote increased productivity as opposed to ‘solving’ climate change or improving the lives of people. The three chapters demonstrated how most MDBs follow an instrumentalist approach in each of these areas, and that the larger MDBs play a key role in setting the normative agenda.
Lastly, the two new MDBs led by the BRICS and China not only replicated the MDB model, they opted for a donor-recipient blueprint, which is less development-friendly than the member-owned, financial cooperative agenda envisaged by some the founders of the IBRD. Still, member-led MDBs remain and there are examples of innovative, growing banks in the Global South, most notably CAF.

12.4 Some thoughts on a future research agenda

Turning very briefly to academic research on the MDBs, it is clear from the book that this is a huge field and there is much left unexplored. Historical research on almost all the MDBs, apart from the World Bank and a few RDBs, remains very limited. Archival access would be a fruitful avenue for expanding understandings of development priorities, challenges, and drivers. Equally, an historical analysis of the relationship between states and the MDBs both as recipients and donors would reveal much, especially since this has only been done for a handful of borrowing states and most research on donor countries focusses on the US. For recipient states, questions and topics could include why they have engaged with MDBs, differences in borrowing country portfolios, long-term studies of project outcomes, comparative studies of conditionalities and safeguards, the relationships between political and business interests in MDB lending (see Bond 2016, 2020), and the political economy and practices of MDB procurement (Bracking 2009, Williams-Elegbe 2017). There has also been limited research on the impact of the MDBs on labour markets (Murphy 2014) and, while there are many important project studies, we think there is space for additional comparative project analyses at the meso or macro level.
The complex relationships between the MDBs could use further investigation. This could focus on the World Bank and RDBs, or the RDBs and SRDBs in their regions. And the ideas and programmes of the RDBs and SRDBs could give historical and contemporary insights into the role of regionalism in development thinking and practice (Bruszt and Palestini 2016). Equally there is more scope for research on the RDBs and some of the sub-regional and specialised banks as intellectual actors, as Shields (2015) has done on the EBRD. Organisational research on MDB leadership would be interesting, from issues of poor gender representation in most MDBs to the professional and personal backgrounds linking MDB staff.
There is very little research on the SRDBs, which is peculiar given that they are fascinating and have diverse histories and outcomes. Humphrey (2019) has provided some excellent research with a focus on MDB banking imperatives but there are other avenues, for example: the pros and cons of the quirks of the SRDBs, such as having other DFIs and commercial banks as shareholders; the story of why and how non-regional members joined, like Belarus in the TDB or Barclays Bank and the Consortium of Yugoslav Institutions in the EADB; or, why the CDB provides a very high level of budgetary support lending. Research on the SRDBs could be useful for IR and IPE to better understand cooperative relations between smaller or less powerful nation-states. Similarly, the dominance of hegemons in the SRDBs would be quite illustrative; for example, Russia’s role in the IBEC, IIB, and EDB could shed light on the financial dimension of Russia’s relations with its neighbours and former Soviet states and the relative degrees of coercion and consent involved. Research on the extent to which the SRDBs follow mainstream development discourse is very scarce and would be illuminating in shedding light on policy diffusion from a constructivist perspective or on neoliberal hegemony from a Gramscian one. Finally, research on the SRDBs could be useful for developing a model about what kinds of innovations are possible for states genuinely wanting more progressive and equal relationships between members in an MDB.

12.5 COVID-19

Amid a global pandemic, it would be wrong not to close this book with a few thoughts on how the MDBs are responding to COVID-19. Indeed, it is vital as the MDBs will be one of the main sources of funding for the Global South, if not the main source, given that the Beyond Aid Agenda has seen decreases in grant financing and increases in loans. The World Bank has so far committed to providing $160 billion to help states address the crisis and a handful of others will provide a further $80 billion. The World Bank is also frontloading grants through its COVID-19 Fast Track Facility with more lending to follow. Some of the funding is of course supporting healthcare. Yet, as Chapter 11 briefly demonstrated, historical MDB financing to the health sector has been limited and what they have provided has tended to support the privatisation and deregulation of public health services. The resultant mixed public-private systems in developing countries are often poorly coordinated and do not have the capacity to respond to public health emergencies let along a pandemic. The private sector provides most of the hospital beds in many developing countries, but many private operators are unwilling to treat COVID-19 patients and others are using the crisis as an opportunity to price gouge and some from both these groups are struggling financially because of the pandemic’s impacts. Yet, the MDB response to date appears to be a doubling-down on support for the private sector; public sector healthcare remains underfunded. This support is unlikely to assist the poorest, most vulnerable, and people in rural and remote areas. Indeed, much of it may just bail-out urban-based private medical centres. Hence, MDB health financing needs close monitoring and critical scrutiny.
The COVID-19 response of the MDBs is not limited to healthcare concerns. They have allocated significant financial allotments to budgetary support, emergency liquidity, and investment loans for economic recovery in infrastructure and other areas. Loans to financial intermediaries are proving popular for the World Bank, IFC, AIIB, CEB, and others, which is one of the least transparent forms of MDB lending. For example, the AIIB COVID-19 Crisis Recovery Facility is set to provide $10 billion to public and private organisations from April 2020 to October 2021, with considerable support directed to on-lending facilities and credit lines to financial institutions and immediate fiscal and budgetary support, including a $500 million credit line to Turkey to alleviate capital shortages. Just as in the healthcare sector, close monitoring of loans will be vital to ensure that their benefits are not mostly captured by elites, with the middle class and poor ultimately left to pay the bill. The likelihood of misappropriation and outright corruption is high across the globe, but higher in states in the Global South dominated by predatory domestic power configurations. When COVID-19 hit, rapidly increasing debt was already a concern. Thus, it may not be long before the MDBs switch from being vital sources of funds in a pandemic to debt-collectors and austerity promoters.
