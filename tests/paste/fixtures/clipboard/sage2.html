Hybrid democratic innovation as assemblage: Probing and extending an emerging concept for democratic-innovations research

Abstract

The assemblage concept has recently been applied to the field of democratic innovations. Although ‘democratic assemblage’ as a sensitising concept can sharpen awareness to the mixing and mingling of divergent components that is going on in practices of hybrid democratic innovation (HDI), the current usage of the concept also has its limitations. We argue that to move forward with meaningful theoretical understanding of democratic assemblage, additional steps are required. In this article, we forge connections between assemblage theory and governance scholarship, more notably institutional and cultural theory, to offer a more comprehensive framework for analysing democratic innovations. This expanded approach enhances the analytical and explanatory power of the democratic-assemblage concept. The case of the recurrent reshuffling of PB in Antwerp, Belgium, is used in this article as a test case and grindstone for conceptual argumentation.

Introduction

This article explores hybrid democratic innovation (HDI) – designs that combine diverging modes of citizen involvement – with the theoretical lens of democratic assemblage. Our central concern is to understand theoretically how such complex innovations ‘hold together’ and generate meaningful impact. We use and extend the concept of democratic assemblage – a term that foregrounds the heterogeneous, dynamic, and contingent nature of heterogenous democratic practices (see also Bussu et al., 2025a, previously in this journal’s Special Issue ‘Democratic innovations beyond the deliberative paradigm’).
In this article, we use the term hybrid democratic innovation (HDI) to describe the combination of formats in democratic innovations, namely, the combination of deliberative (talk-centric) and plebiscitary (vote-centric) modes of citizen involvement in connection to representative democracy (Chambers and Warren, 2023; Elstub and Escobar, 2019; Ettlinger and Michels, 2025; Hendriks, 2023; Saward, 2001, 2021; Witting et al., 2023). Our empirical focus here is on a case within a particular subtype of HDI: Participatory Budgeting-new style (PB-new style), which combines larger-scale plebiscitary voting enabled by digital tools with small-scale deliberation rooted in earlier PB practices (Hendriks and Michels, 2024: 4; cf. Cabannes, 2004; Goel et al., 2019; Iasulaitis et al., 2019; Lehtonen, 2022; Wampler, 2000; Sintomer et al., 2008). We understand assemblage as a broader ontological and relational theoretical lens that captures the planned and emergent processes through which such hybrid democratic forms are composed, stabilised, and sometimes destabilised through human and non-human interactions.
Democratic assemblage theory so far challenges systemic thinking by conceptualising democracy not as a stable structure, but as a process of becoming: an arrangement of human and non-human components that is at once contingent, heterogeneous, and layered (Felicetti, 2021). Felicetti (2021: 1598) suggests that ‘democracy is neither a set of predetermined parts (. . .) nor a random collection of things’. Asenbaum (2022a, 2022b, 2025) further advances this thinking, arguing that democratic innovation emerges through both planned processes and emergent, unforeseen constellations of actors and elements, human and non-human alike. Asenbaum thus pushes the concept of democratic assemblage further than a description of a heterogenous institutional arrangement, to a means of understanding the (un)planned process of assembling human and non-human components into an assemblage that ‘holds’ – for example, including not only citizens and facilitators but also non-human actants such as digital platforms, tables, or urban infrastructure used during participatory processes (cf. Asenbaum and Bussu, 2025; Kaithwar, 2025). Bussu et al. (2025a, 2025b) show how an assemblage perspective provides a useful lens to recognise and analyse participatory processes as non-linear and situated in a complex network of interactions. Assemblage theory so far thus emphasises contingent configurations of human and non-human actants,1 challenging the stability assumptions of systemic approaches.
Yet, democratic assemblage theory remains underdeveloped in two critical respects. First, there is limited understanding of upstream drivers: what initiates and shapes democratic assemblages in different contexts. While Durose et al. (2022) underscore the role of vision and agency, they offer limited insight into conditions for durability. Second, downstream consequences, how assemblages translate into democratic outcomes, are insufficiently theorised. This article thus addresses the question: how can the emerging theory on democratic assemblage be applied and extended in order to help us understand why certain hybrid democratic innovations form assemblages that ‘hold’ (upstream power) and realise meaningful outcomes (downstream power)?
All in all, this article offers a refined, multifocal lens to analyse how and why hybrid processes hold (upstream power) and generate meaningful democratic outcomes (downstream power).
In what follows, we first examine the case of Antwerp, Belgium, which we approach as an exemplary case of HDI due to its sustained evolution over multiple cycles, deliberate hybridisation of participatory logics, and well-documented shifts in design and practice (section ‘Hybrid innovation in Antwerp’s case of PB-new style’). The Antwerp case is especially suitable for probing assemblage thinking because it combines institutional structure with emergent dynamics, allowing us to trace both upstream drivers and downstream consequences within a single setting. As such, the case functions as a conceptual grindstone: allowing us to refine theoretical insights that may be extended or adapted to other hybrid democratic innovations with similarly complex configurations. We then probe the democratic assemblage concept as a lens (section ‘Probing the scope of the democratic-assemblage concept’), focusing on what power the concept in current formulation holds, and on where it is in need of reinforcement.2
We show that the democratic assemblage concept provides valuable insights into the precarious and layered nature of hybrid democratic innovations, but requires both further exploration of what assemblage theory has to offer, as well as reinforcement from other theories. We then elaborate on how a conceptual extension with institutional and cultural theory (Douglas, 1966, 2006; Ostrom, 1982, 1990) can offer a more comprehensive understanding of democratic innovations as both planned and emergent assemblages (section ‘Connecting democratic assemblage to institutional and cultural theory’). Following this, we take the expanded theorical lens to the Antwerp case again, summarising how this can help to understand how hybrid democratic innovations come into being and realise outcomes in urban practice (section ‘Hybrid democratic innovation through an expanded theoretical lens’). In the final section, we discuss how by advancing theoretical assemblage, we can contribute to a richer understanding of real-life democratic assemblage, including upstream drivers and downstream consequences (section ‘Conclusion and discussion: The case for theoretical assemblage’).

Hybrid innovation in Antwerp’s case of PB-new style

Participatory Budgeting (PB)-new style, exemplified by Antwerp’s Citizens’ Budget (Burgerbegroting in local Flemish), represents a hybrid democratic innovation blending plebiscitary, vote-centric elements with deliberative, talk-centric approaches within the framework of representative (local) democracy. Originating in Porto Alegre, Brazil, in 1989, PB has evolved globally, with cities like Paris, Madrid, Berlin, and Reykjavik leading in integrating digital tools into participatory processes (Cabannes, 2004; Goel et al., 2019; Iasulaitis et al., 2019; Lehtonen, 2022; Sintomer et al., 2008; Wampler, 2000). Antwerp stands out as a model for examining the concept of democratic assemblage due to its deliberate and evolving hybridisation of deliberative and plebiscitary elements, supported by digital advancements.
Since its inception in 2014, Antwerp’s Citizens’ Budget has transitioned from a primarily face-to-face deliberative process to one embracing large-scale online participation and voting, achieving a significant transformation in 2018. This shift introduced what some evaluators described as an ‘online voting battle’, revealing both opportunities for broader engagement and challenges like unequal resources and undesirable behaviours, such as recruiting tactics (Hofman et al., 2021). These dynamics necessitated rebalancing strategies, culminating in a redesigned participatory framework as of 2022 (Hofman et al., 2021).
To better appreciate Antwerp’s Citizens’ Budget as democratic assemblage, we need to take a closer look at the assembled components of the full process. A certain degree of descriptive detail is necessary to recognise the dynamic and heterogenous quality of assemblage in this.
Antwerp’s Citizens’ Budget exemplifies a dynamic assemblage of heterogeneous components – planned and unplanned – within a multi-step institutional design. The process consists of four interlinked stages, each assembling diverse social and physical elements (see Figure 1). The entire process depends on political goodwill and assumes the existence of local demands and popular energy, which feed into the budgetary process – a process dispersed over 2 years, with year 1 of each new cycle overlapping with year 2 of the previous cycle. Each new cycle works towards an accepted Burgerbegroting, a final list of selected and budgetarily awarded projects, to be actually implemented in the urban realm.

Step 1: Selecting themes and allocating budget

Citizens participate offline and online in selecting priority themes and distributing funds, blending deliberative group activities and individual votes. Offline deliberation is weighted more heavily to maintain a balance with the more numerous online contributions. Assembled components in step one are profoundly heterogenous, and include multiple things – social and physical – that assemble in partially pre-structured and partially novel configurations: online and offline participants, organisers and facilitators, meeting rooms, tables of five, themes, preferences, factual information, poker chips, deliberations, votes, scores, principles, aggregation formulas, and more depending on how deep one cares to zoom in on the social and physical world. For present purposes, ascending to an even more detailed, microscopic level of assembled components is not deemed necessary here, nor in the following three sub-steps. The current description suffices to illuminate the key point: the dynamic and heterogenous nature of democratic assemblage in Antwerp’s case of hybrid democratic innovation.3

Step 2: Submitting and tweaking projects

Citizens and groups submit project proposals within selected themes, supported and screened by municipal professionals. This step focuses on enhancing rough ideas into feasible plans, ensuring inclusivity and adherence to guidelines. Assembled components in step two are also quite heterogenous and include multiple things – social and physical – that assemble in partially novel, partially pre-structured configurations: local individuals and civil-society groups working on rough ideas, proposals, budget statements, facilitation resources, professionals, principles, screening moments, negative list items, budget rules, information, specifications, labels, visuals, interactions, forms, meeting venues, et cetera.

Step 3: Choosing projects and allocating budget

Citizens select projects through a combination of deliberative exchanges, offline and online,4 and plebiscitary voting. Aggregation rules prioritise deliberative contributions, striving for a balanced influence of both modes. Step three assembles heterogenous – social and physical – components that closely hang together in this fateful (go/no go) step of the process: online and offline participants, organisers, facilitators, tables of five at a Budget Festival, online groups of five, interactive exchanges in rounds, theme areas, project submissions, votes with and without group exchange (‘deliberative’ and ‘non-deliberative’ scores), compulsory multiple voting, aggregation rules, rankings, budget subtractions, selected projects, win/lose emotions, cooling down elements. Again, the components assemble in partially novel, partially pre-structured configurations.

Step 4: Realisation of selected projects

Finalised projects, ranging from neighbourhood initiatives to public infrastructure, are implemented by either municipal staff or the submitting groups, showcasing diverse operational assemblages. Compared to the previous steps, components assembled in step four of the process are qualitatively different but still rather heterogenous. One might say that it all comes down to operational action, but in this operational action many novel interactions and exchanges emerge. The realisation of after-school activities by civil-society groups displays a very different assemblage of components than, say, the production of a music festival.

In sum: Hybrid assemblage in democratic practice

Looking up from such very specific constellations of collective actions made possible in the context of an innovative set-up called the Citizens’ Budget, we see an interlaced succession of ‘democratic assemblage within democratic assemblage’, ranging from more micro-assemblage (e.g. in the intermingling of deliberative and plebiscitary mechanisms around tables of the yearly Budget Festival) to more macro-assemblage (e.g. in the reconstruction of the heterogenous four-step Budgetary Process).
Antwerp’s PB-new style thus highlights an intricate interplay of deliberative and plebiscitary components, often assembled laterally rather than sequentially, fostering dynamic democratic interactions. The process balances three democratic norms – deliberation (reasoned interaction), reach (inclusion), and impact (political decisiveness) – within a constantly evolving framework (Hofman, 2021). This hybrid assemblage underscores the adaptive and contingent nature of participatory governance, where design elements guide but do not fully determine outcomes.

Probing the scope of the democratic-assemblage concept

So, to what extent and in which way does the democratic-assemblage concept assist and strengthen our understanding of the dynamics of new blends of democratic innovations such as PB-new style, and where and when is it in need of reinforcement? While the concept is promising for capturing the heterogeneity, contingency, and fluidity of democratic practices, it also faces important theoretical limitations. Notably, Buchanan (2020) rightly cautions that assemblage theory risks ignoring structural power and agency. This critique is particularly relevant when examining real-world democratic innovations that combine both planned and emergent elements, and which unfold within existing political-institutional contexts. Latour (1999) and Callon (1984) have countered this critique, noting that the flat ontology in assemblage thinking does not deny power but rather situates it in relational dynamics: as emergent from an assemblage rather than being possessed a priori by discrete entities. In any case, we argue that assemblage theory is a useful analytical starting point, especially when it is complemented with perspectives that attend more explicitly to institutional structuring (such as Ostrom’s). In what follows, we use the Antwerp case as a grindstone to assess the analytical reach of democratic assemblage theory.
The democratic assemblage concept in some way responds to the systemic approach to democracy (e.g. Asenbaum, 2022a; Felicetti, 2021). Our focus on an individual democratic innovation such as PB-new style shifts the analytical emphasis from overarching configurations to situated practices. This allows for more fine-grained tracing of how heterogeneous elements come together, stabilise, or fragment in specific institutional and cultural contexts. Overall, we argue that while the assemblage concept yields valuable insights into the layered nature of hybrid democratic innovation, it requires reinforcement to account more fully for both upstream drivers and downstream consequences.
As indicated, the core essence of democratic assemblage – heterogenous democratic components that somehow stick together based on dispersed agency – comes indeed close to what can be observed in the set-up of Antwerp’s Citizens’ Budget and its underlying process steps. In each of the four steps, we found diverse – social and material – components assembled in situated, interactively developed configurations. The emerging democratic-assemblage literature provides helpful words to describe and distinguish what is happening here: democratic assemblage as ‘a formation that realizes democratic core values, but also the very process by which such a constellation is brought about’ (Asenbaum, 2022a: 251) and ‘actors on the ground (that) not only mix practices belonging to different conceptions of democracy’ but ‘also constantly deal with the challenges that this heterogeneity involves’ (Felicetti, 2021: 1595) are apposite descriptions of PB-new style in Antwerp.
However, some things remain to be desired. The current use of the democratic assemblage concept has a deeply descriptive focus, which has its values but also its limitations (cf. Kaithwar, 2025: 28–29). Current conceptualisations help to sense and grasp the qualitative richness of democratic assemblage in great phenomenological detail – a quality not habitually offered in standard depictions of democracy and innovations therein. However, they do not assist very specifically in connecting the phenomenon to downstream political and social consequences – why are some democratic assemblages more consequential than others in realising public and democratic values? (cf. Hendriks, 2022; Smith, 2009) – nor to upstream explanatory factors – what explains the emergence, composition and dynamics of democratic assemblages in different contexts? (cf. Cain et al., 2003; Zieman, 2013). The focus of attention is first and foremost on the phenomenon itself, on assemblage and its detailed heterogenous qualities.
To be fair, Asenbaum (2022a: 258–259) mentions ‘at least three forces that bring and hold assemblage together’. which is to some extent an exploration upstream, albeit of an extremely general sort. First, he mentions natural forces, such as gravity that ‘keeps every material object, including sentient bodies from drifting into space’. The second force he calls affect, the ‘often sudden, visceral response to some occurrence’ that attracts and arranges components of the assemblage. And the third force he mentions is cognition, ‘making sense of things and establishing connections between them’.
Relevant and fundamental as they are, these forces are also highly abstract and far from explaining more specifically why, for instance, the democratic assemblage of PB-new style in Antwerp has emerged differently from the one found in Amsterdam, or the one that characterised PB-old style as originally emerged in Porto Alegre. In fact, the three forces do not so much offer a clarifying explanation of the workings of assemblage, rather a deeper description of democratic assemblage as a phenomenon – which is something else, however valuable it may be in and of itself.
Thus, a pressing question is now to what extent assemblage thinking can help us to understand the process of becoming in hybrid democratic innovations (as in the Antwerp case), and related to this to help us to understand the conditions under which such assemblages, precariously, ‘hold’ together. This process of becoming can be seen as both-emergent-and-planned. So far scholars have emphasised the emergent qualities of assemblages (Durose et al., 2022: 2132): ‘the composition of diverse elements into emergent and provisional socio-spatial formations’ (. . .) ‘heterogenous, contingent, unstable, partial, and situated’. The value of the assemblage concept for them is its ability to shift from studying reified entities (democratic innovations in the context of this article) to better examining the ongoing sociomaterial processes that constitute, reproduce, or change these entities. They link assemblage with practice theory, stressing the practices and agency of situated human agents, the acts of assembling done by brokers, and the everyday work at the frontlines of urban transformation (cf. Bussu et al., 2025a, 2025b; Eadson and Van Veelen, 2021; McFarlane, 2011; Monteiro and Nicolini, 2015).
Where Durose et al. (2022), along with other previously mentioned urban and policy scholars, stress human agency and practices, Jane Bennett (2020) adds a case for the vitality and agency of non-human elements. Her concept of ‘vital materialism’ stresses that non-human components have their own capacity to act and affect the world, often in ways that humans cannot control or predict. For instance, consider the role that air pressure, temperature, and ambient odour can play in face-to-face meetings such as the Antwerp Budget Festival.
However, this focus on materiality and emergent, unplanned agency has been met with criticism within assemblage scholarship. Ian Buchanan (2020), in particular, argues that certain interpretations of assemblage theory tend to diminish the political agency and intentionality of human actors, overlooking crucial power dynamics and the influence of social structures. In line with this, Koster and Van Leynseele (2018) emphasise the role of brokers in assemblages to bridge gaps between populations, usually disadvantaged, and power-holders. Latour (1999) and others (such as Callon, 1984; Law, 2002; MacKenzie, 2008; Mol, 2002) have dismissed this critique on materialism as a misunderstanding. Their argument is that a flat ontology does not presuppose an absence of power, but rather recognises that power and agency are not properties possessed by discrete entities. Instead, agency emerges through the relational dynamics between human and non-human actants that together stabilise or destabilise an assemblage, whether in planned or unplanned ways (Latour, 1999). Further on, the point made in this article is that the planned, and more institutionalised, elements of assemblages along with their extant power structures should not be ignored when transposing the concept to the democratic innovations literature.
This debate is highly relevant to the Antwerp case as a specimen of HDI. On the one hand, it is true that novel democratic assemblages emerge at the various tables and in the various digital groups, in the interactions between proponents of projects and between them and municipal staff, in the dynamics of deliberation and voting on submissions, in the mesh of expressions, bodies and objects (meeting tables, information boards, poker chips and much more), and in the interactive realisation of awarded grants.
On the other hand, it would be a misunderstanding to view what happens and comes together in the successive steps of the process as entirely unforeseen, provisional or unstable. As indicated, there is a significant measure of predetermined design, conscious planning, and institutionalised choreography inserted into Antwerp’s PB-new style and its four-step process. At the same time, there is plenty of room for ever-novel, unforeseen and voluntaristic ‘micro-assemblage’ within the still dynamic but significantly patterned and institutionalised ‘macro-assemblage’ of Antwerp’s mixed model. To acknowledge only the former (the unforeseen) and neglect the latter (the predetermined) would not serve our understanding.
In the emerging, still fledgling literature on democratic-assemblage its conceptualisation is primarily geared to expressing and understanding democratic assemblage in a radically bottom-up and egalitarian way, applying a narrowly understood ‘everything-is- -important’ flat ontology, which is in part but not in whole consonant with real-life democratic assemblage as evidenced in the Antwerp case. Although there is much more to this process than a simple flow-chart can capture, not everything that we see assembled here is bottom-up emerging, nor equally important.
This echoes Buchanan’s (2020) criticisms that the flattening of agency may obscure power dynamics or reduce the capacity of human agents to forge political change. Buchanan’s critique is also grounded in his interpretation of Deleuze and Guattari’s (1987) original ideas. He believes that DeLanda (2016) and Bennett’s (2020) versions of assemblage theory diverge too far from the original intentions of Deleuze and Guattari, particularly in their overemphasis on materialism. Instead, Buchanan (2020) suggests that an adequate theory of assemblage must include a focus on totality – how assemblages are part of larger systems and how these systems exert structural constraints on their components.
The democratic assemblage concept has so far been used by scholars to describe the composite nature of democratic innovations, and to acknowledge the both-planned-and-emergent interplay of assemblages (though emphasising the emergent qualities). However, as said, this literature does not yet push the concept to further an explanation of the upstream and downstream power of assemblages. While Deleuze and Guattari’s (1987) seminal work touches on processes of territorialisation and deterritorialisation that shape assemblages, and Asenbaum’s (2022a) and Bussu et al.’s (2025a) builds on this in the context of democratic spaces, it is still unclear why some (democratic) assemblages are more consequential than others, and why some assemblages ‘hold’ and others disintegrate. Enriching the upcoming democratic-assemblage concept with earlier established cultural and institutional theory can help filling the gaps.

Connecting democratic assemblage to institutional and cultural theory

To further develop explicatory power, both upstream and downstream, additional and complementary theoretical approaches would be needed. In a constructive spirit, we would suggest to seek a mutually enhancing connection between democratic assemblage as an emerging theoretical concept and earlier developed institutional and cultural theory. We believe that this would shed light also on the previously observed distinction of micro-level and macro-level assemblage. For present purposes, and readily acknowledging that more could be taken from the rich fund of institutional and cultural theory, we would suggest at least two additional frameworks, pioneered by, respectively, the institutional theorist Elinor Ostrom and the cultural theorist Mary Douglas.

From micro to macro democratic assemblage: Elinor Ostrom’s worlds of action

Elinor Ostrom’s Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework was originally developed in the 1980s and has informed her Nobel prize-winning research on governing the commons in subsequent decades. Central to the IAD framework are institutional rules (in use), which coalesce with wider social and physical factors to create worlds of action and situations of choice; see Figure 2 (Bushouse et al., 2016; Ostrom, 1982, 1990).
Of particular relevance here is the distinction of different levels or ‘worlds’ of action and choice. Moving from micro to macro, Ostrom distinguished the ‘operational’, the ‘collective’, and the ‘constitutional’ levels of action and choice. Translated to PB-new style in Antwerp, and staying close to Ostrom’s conceptualisation, one could say that the practical realisation of awarded projects in the city happens at the operational level; deliberation and voting on priorities and projects at the collective-choice level; and the (re)configuration of the encompassing model of Citizens’ Budgeting in Antwerp at the constitutional level.
Ostrom’s research spanning the various levels of aggregation was primarily focused on understanding public choice, particularly on common pool resources, but what matters here is that her levels of aggregation could just as well be seen as layers or ‘worlds’ of democratic assemblage. Refining the rough distinction between macro- and micro-assemblage made earlier, we might distinguish between democratic assemblage at the operational level, the collective choice level, and the constitutional level. This is all democratic assemblage in the sense of ‘mixing practices belonging to different conceptions of democracy’ and ‘dealing with the challenges that this heterogeneity involves’, referring to Felicetti (2021: 1595). But the meaning of this changes from level to level, as we can see in Antwerp’s case of PB-new style.
On one end, in the operational world, democratic assemblage in the Antwerp case involves the mixing of bottom-up and voluntary citizen initiative with public administration under democratic-political responsibility – the assemblage of different people, organisations, energies, resources in and around specific projects. At the other end, at the constitutional level, democratic assemblage in the Antwerp case involves the mixing of different democratic principles in one encompassing, heterogenous configuration – the assemblage of representative, participatory, deliberative and plebiscitary notions of democracy.
At the level of specific projects, democratic assemblage is more voluntaristic and unforeseen (though far from entirely). At the level of the encompassing configuration, democratic assemblage is more patterned and stabilised (though again far from entirely). Ostrom’s insights are particularly helpful in clarifying this layering within assemblages. In addition, Bennett’s (2020) work, which underscores the importance of robust interactions in assemblages, can be connected to this. The Antwerp case exemplifies an effective upstream and downstream assemblage precisely because it features layered interactions that reinforce one another.
Seeing democratic assemblage at different levels of aggregation, inspired by Ostrom’s conceptualisation, means seeing that one level embraces and impacts the next – with assemblages existing within assemblages. Democratic assemblage at the constitutional level is not a priori ‘more democratic’ or ‘more assemblage’. Rather, it is democratic assemblage at a different – more general, more elevated, more encompassing – level. Constructions formed and reformed at this level, such as the elaboration of four heterogenous steps from theme areas to project implementation, affects democratic assemblage at the collective-choice level, which in turn affects assemblage at the operational level. To be sure, ‘affecting’ is far from ‘determining’. But seeing democratic assemblage as an entirely emergent, free-floating, voluntaristic affair would also deny the reality of hybrid democratic innovation in a case such as Antwerp’s PB-new style. The distinction between affecting and determining can also be productively read through Deleuze and Guattari’s notions of deterritorialisation and reterritorialisation. Rather than seeing agency as exerted in linear or hierarchical terms, assemblage theory emphasises how configurations are constantly unmade and remade through shifting relations. Deterritorialisation refers to processes that disrupt or displace existing patterns of interaction (e.g. through unexpected uses of digital voting provisions), while reterritorialisation describes efforts to stabilise or re-anchor those patterns (e.g. by rebalancing deliberative and plebiscitary elements in response to disruption). This lens helps us see how assemblages evolve through affective disruptions and structural recalibrations without implying deterministic causality (cf. Asenbaum and Bussu, 2025; Eadson and Van Veelen, 2021; Kaithwar, 2025).
Does this contradict the core essence of democratic assemblage as the coupling of highly heterogenous components that somehow stick together based on dispersed agency? To the contrary, it sharpens the sensitivity of the democratic-assemblage concept to interactions between the molar and molecular layers of assemblages à la Deleuze and Guattari (1987). These authors posit that molar structures represent stable, large-scale organisations, while molecular forces are fluid, smaller-scale processes that can destabilise or transform the larger system. Ostrom’s three worlds of action and choice can be seen as a public-governance elaboration of such layered thinking, providing a defined way to understand the interactions between layers (see Figure 2).
Moreover, Ostrom’s work can be linked to Asenbaum’s (2022a) force of ‘cognition’ mentioned earlier. Cognition means ‘making sense of things and establishing connections between them’ (Asenbaum, 2022a: 259). At the operational level, cognition is crucial for making practical decisions about resource use. At the collective-choice level cognition involves more complex and deliberative processes. Participants must engage in reasoning and debate to set rules that will guide operational decisions. At the constitutional level, cognition involves high-level, abstract thinking about the structure of governance itself. This includes determining who gets to participate in decision-making, how power is distributed, and how rules can be changed. In this way, Ostrom’s work also extends beyond cognition to reveal the critical importance of related power dynamics, access to competencies, and the material resources necessary for governance. Her emphasis on how rules are devised and enforced by individuals embedded in social and ecological systems brings to light the role of asymmetries in influence and capacity.
Differentiating worlds of action and choice à la Ostrom does not only help to better understand how democratic assemblages work and interlink, but also how they come about and make a difference. The success of the Antwerp PB-new style case as a democratic assemblage, can in part be explained by the presence of these layered interactions at the molar and molecular level that reinforce each other upstream (creating a stable and durable assemblage), allowing for downstream political and social consequences (‘outcomes’ at the ‘operational level’). To be sure, we acknowledge that Ostrom’s identification of distinct ‘levels’ of action may seem to diverge from assemblage theory’s commitment to a flat ontology. However, in our use, these levels are not understood as fixed strata or hierarchical tiers, but rather as institutionally divergent ‘worlds’ of action; analytical entry points through which different assemblage dynamics can be observed, enacted, and interconnected.
Ostrom’s institutional ‘rules in use’ impact action and choice at different levels, and work primarily through extrinsic regulation: specific boundary rules, scope rules, authority rules, procedural rules, and aggregation rules regulate individuals and communities in a way not dissimilar from the rules of tennis, which set the basic framework within which players play the game, but do not regulate the motivations, views and inclinations that actors take to the (democratic) playing field. These latter aspects relate more to Asenbaum’s (2022a) force of affect, for a deeper understanding of which Mary Douglas’s version of cultural theory proves helpful.

Handling matter in/out of place: Mary Douglas’s take on cultural dynamics

Why and how do heterogenous components move together and drift apart in democratic assemblages at different levels of action? What are the cultural attractors and rejectors at work? For instance, in the democratic assemblages, we find in Antwerp’s PB-new style and elsewhere.
Here, we do not have the book-length used elsewhere to explain this (Hendriks, 2023), so we will confine ourselves to the main argument, starting with Douglas’s original thesis on cultural cleaning and anomaly handling, condensed into the famous quip ‘dirt is matter out of place’. Matter out of place is matter – social and symbolic, but also physical and concrete – that does not square with some cherished order, which is culturally defined as impure and in need of cleaning, understood as maintenance of some cherished order. This is what Douglas (1966) flagged in her seminal work ‘Purity and Danger’, elaborated on in a string of later work (a.o. Douglas, 1970, 1986, 1996; Richards and Perri 6, 2023).
The essence of culture according to Douglas is keeping things (‘matter’) in order – some culturally cherished order. What corresponds with this cherished order – defined as ‘in order’, proper, wholesome, or even holy – will be maintained and ‘polished up’ as much as possible by way of positive feedback. What deviates from it will be perceived as ‘out of order’, impure, improper, misfitting and will be distanced and ‘brushed away’ as much as possible by way of negative feedback . In this sense, every culture, in one way or another, is engaged in cultural cleaning, with divergent feedback mechanisms interacting – alternately colliding and colluding – with each other (Douglas, 2006; cf. Perri 6, 2003; Thompson, 2006).
Research on ‘Purity and Danger’ had focused on rules of avoidance in cultures that Douglas would later describe as deeply hierarchical. In later work, she developed a more diverse cultural typology, the so-called grid-group typology distinguishing four elementary ways of life – besides hierarchy also individualism, egalitarianism, and atomism – which act for themselves and react to each other through positive and negative feedback (Douglas, 2006).5 The four types are ideal types, which relate to real-world cultural manifestations as primary colours do to real-world colour varieties – in other words, as mixtures or hybrids. The same pertains to the four types of democracy that in Frank Hendriks’s theorising display elective affinity with the cultural types à la Douglas.
Building on Douglas, Hendriks (2010, 2023) distinguished four ideal types of democratic life, which imply different notions of proper versus improper democracy, and which inspire different attempts at polishing up (through positive feedback) or brushing away (through negative feedback) varieties of democratic ‘matter’: pendulum democracy, consensus democracy, voter democracy, and participatory democracy. In line with these ideal types, Table 1 shows what would be defined as democratically ‘in order’ and ‘out of place’, and what would generally be embraced as democratic betterment in pendulum, consensus, voter and participatory democracy (Hendriks, 2023: 31).
In participatory democracy, for example, traces of direct and integrative democracy signify democratic matter ‘in place’ while traces of indirect and aggregative democracy signify democratic matter ‘out of place’. In cleaning language, the latter calls for brushing away, the former for polishing up. Comparable feedback mechanisms – negative and positive – are theoretically connected to the other types of democracy; what differs is what these other types hold in order and out of place (Table 1). For instance, direct-majoritarian voting is essentially out of place in consensus democracy, while in voter democracy it is fundamentally in order; empowered participation strikes a chord in both voter and participatory democracy, but is drawn in different (aggregative vs integrative) directions therein. The mesh of culturally inspired feedback mechanisms explains a great deal of the drawing together and the moving apart – the (dis)assembling – that we find in the worlds of democracy and democratic innovation (Felicetti, 2021: 1597); and a great deal of the cultural blends and biases revealed in the outcomes of democratic decision-making.
We would argue that such feedback mechanisms pertain to various levels of democratic life, and of democratic assemblage in terms probed here: from the national level, where varieties of pendulum and consensus democracy tend to be the more prominent (but not exclusive) forgers of the modern mixed regime as Rosanvallon (2008) calls it, to the local level, where varieties of voter and participatory democracy are coming to the fore in hybrid democratic innovations such as PB-new style in Antwerp. When in democratic life various things are desired and aspired at the same time, mixed and dynamic assemblages appeal, while pure and fixed designs are distanced as a result of underlying cultural dynamics.
Adding this perspective further contributes to our understanding of assemblage in a hybrid democratic innovation such as Antwerp’s Burgerbegroting. Rather than insisting on a fixed and pure design, the democratic assemblage in Antwerp demonstrates a capacity to accommodate conflicting notions of democratic order, balancing deliberative and plebiscitary interpretations of citizen-directed democracy, attuning these to the institutions of local representative democracy. Taking a Douglasian perspective, one might say that Antwerp’s democratic innovation stays in place and is consequential in doing so not by eliminating conflicting notions of democratic order but by continuously managing it, forging a hybrid democratic assemblage.
This capacity for ongoing negotiation is key to understanding democratic assemblages not as static configurations but as dynamic and negotiated constellations. These assemblages are to a large extent provisional and always relational, animated by what Asenbaum (2022a) calls affect. Unlike Deleuze and Guattari’s (1987) more open-ended celebration of multiplicity, Douglas’s perspective reminds us that assemblages are not merely shaped by a free flow of matter, but also by cultural feedback mechanism that amount to checks and balances that are both constitutive and consequential.
Bringing these perspectives together allows us to view democratic assemblages shaped not only by the agency and creativity of those who assemble them, but also by the inherited structures of meaning that determine what counts as legitimate or clean within a given context. The democratic assemblage is thus a site of both composition and contestation: held together by affect and vision, but also constrained by layered institutional rules-in-use (Ostrom, 4.1) and cultural feedback mechanisms (Douglas, 4.2). Understanding the democratic assemblages in this fuller sense, beyond spontaneity and openness (Durose et al., 2022), helps to explain why certain democratic innovations, like PB-new style in Antwerp, are resilient and consequential, while others are not.

Hybrid democratic innovation through an expanded theoretical lens

In the previous two sections, observations from Antwerp’s PB-new style, its Citizens’ Budget, as a case of hybrid innovation (HDI) were scattered across a dominant theoretical line of reasoning moving from assemblage to institutional to cultural theory. In this section, we return to the Antwerp case in a more condensed way, viewing democratic assemblage therein through our expanded theoretical lens. We connect components of democratic assemblage described earlier to institutional layering (Ostrom) and cultural feedback (Douglas) mechanisms that shape, constrain, and enable hybrid democratic innovation. The analytical response to our research question – how can the emerging theory on democratic assemblage be applied and extended in order to help us understand why certain hybrid democratic innovations form assemblages that ‘hold’ (upstream power) and produce meaningful outcomes (downstream power)? – is summarised in Figure 3.
To reiterate briefly, democratic assemblage theory offers us a basic understanding of upstream drivers, pointing out that HDI’s are situated in a complex network of (non-)human interactions, held together through natural forces, cognition and affect (Asenbaum, 2022a). Downstream, democratic assemblage theory contends that meaningful outcomes emerge at the intersection of structure and contingency, planned and unplanned (Buchanan, 2020).
As specified, Ostrom adds to the understanding of upstream power by showing us how HDI assemblages are held together through a layering of ‘worlds’ of action; at the constitutional, collective-choice, and operational levels. In our case, this helps to see how Antwerp’s process of compiling and implementing its Burgerbegroting is embedded in a broader institutional assemblage. At the constitutional level, the four-step participatory architecture was deliberately constituted and periodically revised by municipal actors. The weighting of deliberative scores over online votes, the compulsory multiple voting system, and the aggregation rules are all examples of constitutional-level choices. These rules shape the space of possible actions and outcomes in subsequent stages and reflect an attempt to create institutional conditions for inclusive and reasoned participation. At the collective-choice level, participants in Antwerp’s hybrid-democratic assemblage engage in deliberation and voting to prioritise themes and select projects. The Budget Festival and digital discussion forums serve as collective-choice arenas where citizen preferences are deliberated, aggregated and filtered. Here, the interplay between planned design (e.g. structured rounds, table formats, and facilitation protocols) and emergent dynamics (e.g. informal influence, spontaneous coalitions, and participant interpretations) becomes visible. These assemblages-within-assemblages are neither entirely stable nor random but held together through recursive interactions among actants, including rules, facilitators, digital platforms, and citizens. At the operational level of Antwerp’s Burgerbegroting, selected projects are implemented and concretised by municipal actors or citizen groups. This stage is characterised by highly differentiated assemblages depending on the nature of the awarded projects (e.g. a neighbourhood playground vs a community-run festival). Such assemblages at the operational level in Antwerp display a high degree of contingency and context-sensitivity, but they are not decoupled from the more encompassing levels. Arrangements for constitutional and collective-choice in Antwerp’s HDI, including established eligibility criteria and administrative support facilities, influence what is and what is not acceptable and doable at the operational level (e.g. assembling a local festival: yes, an international exchange: no). In this way, Ostrom’s IAD framework helps us understand downstream consequences as well. Concrete actions and outcomes at the operational level, are facilitated and constrained by rules and arrangements at the constitutional and collective level. Antwerp’s HDI case shows that democratic assemblage is not only about assembling diverse elements but also about layered reinforcement across institutional worlds, which contributes to the stability (upstream) and outcome (downstream) of the process (of Participatory Budgeting-new style in this case).
As elaborated, Douglas’s cultural theory adds a second analytic lens that sharpens our understanding of upstream drivers and downstream consequences. Her concepts of positive and negative feedback helps to understand how HDI assemblages are held together by cultural checks and balances that negotiate conflicting notions of the (im)proper in democracy; continuously including and excluding ‘matter’ deemed in/out of place within the evolving assemblage. For example, in Antwerp’s Burgerbegroting the 2018 shift towards large-scale online voting introduced what some described as an ‘online voting battle’, which was perceived by parts of the community and by evaluators as out of sync with the deliberative ambitions of the Citizens’ Budget. This triggered negative feedback in the form of critique, resistance, and ultimately redesign. In Douglasian terms, the hyper-plebiscitary mobilisation became ‘matter out of place’ within the assemblage’s cultural frame, which partly leaned towards deliberative-democracy values. The 2022 redesign, which reinforced deliberative elements in Antwerps’ HDI and moderated the impact of online votes, could thus be understood as a combination of positive and negative feedback, recalibrating the desired democratic order. The cultural hybridity of Antwerp’s PB, bringing together elements of deliberative, plebiscitary, and (in the background, as a shadow of hierarchy) representative democracy, produces ongoing tension between different ways of seeing and doing democracy. Yet rather than attempting to purify or eliminate these tensions, Antwerp’s Burgerbegroting as a democratic assemblage evolves by continuously renegotiating them, attempting to make inevitable tensions productive. The system remains dynamic but not unstable or impotent because of its capacity to absorb, process, and selectively incorporate competing democratic logics. This also holds downstream consequences: for instance, giving votes for projects acquired after group deliberation more numerical weight than those achieved without group deliberation pushes some projects higher than others on the list of projects selected and ultimately implemented in Antwerp’s quarters. Douglas helps us understand that outcomes of HDI are inherently mixed, and subject to constant reappraisal. Her lens explains why certain elements of hybrid democratic innovations are embraced more readily than others in particular cultural contexts. For instance, in Antwerp’s Citizens’ Budgetary process deliberative exchanges among ‘tables of five’ resonate with participatory and consensual cosmologies, in which digital mass voting, though efficient and attractive to many citizens, may be culturally coded as individualist-consumerist or techno-populist. The interplay of these elements reflects deeper cultural scripts about what counts as legitimate democratic practice and what can result in meaningful democratic consequences.

Conclusion and discussion: The case for theoretical assemblage

In previous sections, we have attempted to understand an emerging empirical practice – hybrid democratic innovation – with an emerging theoretical concept – democratic assemblage –, aspiring to find out to what extent and in which way this concept assists our understanding in this realm, and where and when it is in need of reinforcement.
For this purpose, we have revisited and reinterpreted a particular variety of hybrid democratic innovation emerging in various places: Participatory Budgeting-new style, of which District Antwerp presents a good case. In general terms, democratic assemblage is a fairly apt concept to capture what happens in Antwerp’s PB-new style, both macro and micro. Clearly heterogenous democratic principles and varieties of innovation are assembled, based on more or less dispersed agency, in Antwerp’s Citizens’ Budget in general and its process steps in particular. As a sensitising concept, democratic assemblage thus helps to see the profoundly heterogenous, chequered, detailed qualities of democratic practice in the making that are not habitually acknowledged in studies of democratic innovation. This is valuable.
However, the current use of the democratic assemblage concept has a deeply descriptive and phenomenological focus, which does not assist very specifically in connecting the complex phenomenon to downstream consequences, nor to upstream drivers. To strengthen its analytical power, we proposed a theoretical assemblage: combining the lens of democratic assemblage with institutional theory (Ostrom) and cultural theory (Douglas). Connecting with institutional theory à la Elinor Ostrom can help to refine and extend in particular the element of cognition in Asenbaum’s (2022a) explication of democratic assemblage. It helps us to distinguish meaningfully between different institutional levels of assemblage, the rules-in-use that guide and instruct actors and actions at these levels, and to understand the institutional impact from one level to the next (assemblages working within assemblages). Moreover, this perspective allows us to better trace downstream consequences, outcomes at the operational level in Ostrom’s terms (Figure 2). Connecting with cultural theory à la Mary Douglas can help to better understand the element of affect, the culturally inspired feedback mechanisms that impact on democratic assemblage, and that contribute to it being never done or finished. It sheds sharper light on the culturally-induced likes and allergies that come to the fore in democratic innovations (Table 1). Relatedly, it helps to grasp the upstream drivers that shape the mesh of democratic modes in democratic assemblages.
While this article has focused on extending the concept of democratic assemblage, it remains important to position this approach in relation to other approaches that, without always or necessarily using the term ‘assemblage’, investigate the negotiated and messy consequences and drivers of democratic innovations (Escobar and Bua, 2025; Ettlinger and Michels, 2025; Font et al., 2016; Jacquet et al., 2023). The expanded concept of democratic assemblage adds value to these debates by offering a vocabulary that explicitly integrates materiality, emergent dynamics, and multi-scalar layering. We see strong potential for dialogue between an expanded approach to democratic assemblage and adjacent views of heterogeneity in democratic innovation, and encourage future work to bridge these bodies of literature more systematically.
Ultimately, we advance three general takeaways. First, we suggest that the strength and sustainability of HDI lies not in achieving coherence or purity, but in the ongoing negotiation of difference across institutional and cultural dimensions. This is not unique to Antwerp. Other strands of HDI, like citizens’ assemblies linked to referendums (Farrell et al., 2023; Hendriks and Wagenaar, 2023) also involve tensions among competing democratic logics. These can be fruitfully understood as democratic assemblages operating in layered, context-sensitive ways.
Second, by understanding HDI in the broader light of democratic assemblage, researchers gain a vocabulary and conceptual structure to compare across cases without flattening diversity. The framework encourages attention to how democratic practices are composed, stabilised, and contested in everyday settings. This enables more nuanced evaluations of democratic innovation, beyond effectiveness or legitimacy metrics, to include institutional durability, cultural resonance, and adaptive capacity (cf. Asenbaum, 2025).
Third, the augmented democratic-assemblage concept has practical relevance for practitioners and policymakers. It suggests that the challenge of innovation is not just to select the ‘best’ participatory method, but to construct and maintain flexible architectures that can accommodate heterogeneity. The concept encourages reflexivity in design, helping to anticipate where friction might occur and where reinforcing interactions can be cultivated.
While the Antwerp case is context-specific, its analytic value lies in revealing the layered and negotiated dynamics that characterise many hybrid democratic innovations. Rather than serving as a prototype, it offers a transferable lens for recognising how institutional structures, cultural logics, and material arrangements interact to shape such innovations. Future research could apply the enriched framework to other variants of democratic innovation, to further refine its explanatory power, identify recurring assemblage patterns, and explore the limits of its applicability.
Overall, this article advocates for theoretical assemblage not only as an interpretive tool for understanding democratic heterogeneity, but also as a generative framework for democratic experimentation. It invites further research across different geographies, policy domains, and institutional arrangements to test and refine its scope.

Biographies

Frank Hendriks is full professor of comparative governance at Tilburg University, and also a member of the Dutch Council for Public Governance, an advisory council. His research focuses on the design and quality of democratic governance, democratic leadership and active citizenship, reform and innovation in democratic institutions. He is the (co)author of numerous articles and books on these topics; his most recent monograph is Rethinking Democratic Innovation: Cultural Clashes and the Reform of Democracy, Oxford University Press, 2023; a sequel to Vital Democracy: A Theory of Democracy in Action, Oxford University Press, 2010.
Shirley Kempeneer is assistant professor of digital government at Tilburg University. Her work contributes to the interdisciplinary field of Science, Technology and Society (STS) and investigates issues of public sector digital transformation and citizen-state relationships. She is the author of Breaking the Bank: The regulatory implications of knowledge production through indicators, 2021, Eleven International Publishing. Her work has appeared in journals such as Government Information Quarterly, Data Science & Society, and Information Polity.
