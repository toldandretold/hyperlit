Peer Review 2027: Scenarios for Academic Publishing in the Age of AI

Kevin Munger, Bert N. Bakker, Adam J. Berinsky,

Nathalie Giger, Andy Guess, Natascha Just, Regina Lawrence,

Keren Tenenboim-Weinblatt, and Arnout van de Rijt

Abstract

The practices of peer review and the broader landscape of academic publishing are under strain. Submission rates are rising, placing greater demands on editorial and reviewer capacity. The use of LLMs in the production of academic papers threatens to accelerate these trends beyond the breaking point. This article is the product of a meeting of editors of academic journals across political science, sociology, and communication science to discuss the issue of LLMs in academic publishing. We argue that peer review is an essential and irreplaceably human component of social science: if elements of the research process traditionally done by humans are substituted by AI, humans should increase their involvement with the evaluation of research. We present a scenario-casting exercise illustrating four possible equilibria for the incorporation of LLMs into the publication ecosystem, and we discuss the various levers that academic journals have at their disposal to navigate the changing landscape. We emphasize that adaptive policies with built-in evaluation mechanisms, feedback loops, and a capacity for revision are required, along with new streams of metascientific data to remain up to date as AI and its adoption continue to evolve.

1 Introduction

An important function of our journals and the review process on which we rely is the allocation of scholarly attention to research that we think is important. AI may prove to have the potential to dissolve current structures of knowledge production, leading to more post-publication review and less reliance on the imprimatur of traditional arbiters like journals. As an author team of social-science journal editors, we assert the continued importance of the attention-allocating function of peer-reviewed journals. Judgment should not be offloaded to AI, nor should curation be offloaded to third-party platforms.

The practices of peer review and the broader landscape of academic publishing are under strain. Submission rates are rising [Thelwall and Sud, 2022], while acceptance rates at reputable, high-ranking journals remain broadly unchanged (e.g., Dolan and Lawless, 2024; Tripp and Dion, 2024), thereby placing greater demands on editorial and reviewer capacity. The presence of LLM-produced text in academic papers is increasing (Liang et al., 2025; Hao et al., 2026; Shmatko et al., 2026), threatening to accelerate these trends beyond the breaking point. To date, however, there has been limited coordinated or systematic responses from journal editors and publishers.

This article is the product of a meeting of editors (and associate editors) of academic journals across the disciplines of political science, sociology and communication science to discuss the issue of LLMs in academic publishing. In our opinion, and based on our experience, peer review is an essential and irreplaceably human component of the scientific process. We argue that as elements of the research production process traditionally done by humans are increasingly substituted by AI, humans should increase their involvement with the evaluation of research. In short: increase AI production, increase human evaluation. Assigning value to new knowledge – on scientific, practical, and ethical grounds – is inherently a human responsibility. Trained and trusted scientists remain the individuals best suited to carry out this task.

We emphasize that the scope of our discussion is social science. Computer science (and perhaps some natural sciences) may provide an alternative institutional setup, relying on benchmarks and other objective performance to evaluate knowledge. Contributions to social science (mostly) cannot be flattened in this way; expertise is required to holistically evaluate how the disparate elements of a manuscript (theory, data, conclusions) relate both to each other and to previous work.

There is not a single LLM “policy” that will fix peer review. Peer review will never return to the status quo circa 2023, before the release of ChatGPT, nor would this static return be desirable. There remains considerable uncertainty in how LLMs will continue to develop (Bengio et al., 2025; Berti et al., 2025; Villalobos et al., 2024). Therefore, adaptive policies with built-in evaluation mechanisms, feedback loops, and a capacity for revision are required; prerequisite for such adaptiveness are new and continuously updated sources of information about how academics are using LLMs, a point which we address in detail below.

And to be blunt: it’s not like peer review was perfect before (Tennant and Ross-Hellauer, 2020; Smith, 2006), or even that it necessarily made sense. Nobody designed the current system, nobody was evaluating its performance on any outcome-based metric or subjective criterion, let alone calculating the trade-offs between the various goals that peer review has been understood to accomplish. This is an organically grown ecosystem with a strong built-in path dependency.

Consequently, the effects of LLMs cannot be assessed in isolation but depend on system-wide responses and interactions. We therefore present a series of different scenarios for the incorporation of LLMs into the academic publication landscape. This process has been used by other actors trying to understand how AI will reshape their environments (Tegmark, 2018; Korinek, 2023). Rather than starting where we are and deriving specific steps to take, we envision possible futures and consider the set of conditions that would enable those futures, as well as the desirability and especially stability of those futures.

We have noticed a general trend in the AI policies that actors in sectors from education to the workplace seem to favor: “I get to use AI, and nobody else gets to use AI.” For example, teachers want to be able to use AI for generating and grading assignments, but only if students aren’t able to use AI to complete those assignments [Edutopia, 2025, Vice, 2025]. The game-theoretic logic is clear: people are aware of the efficiency benefits that using LLMs offers them, but wary of degraded performance if others use LLMs. More existentially, the extensive use of LLMs by all actors in the system calls into question the value of those actors in the first place.

Some journals, including some of the ones we edit, have already adopted some kind of “AI policy,” though this adoption has been tentative. Many journals have buried their policy in the increasingly long list of journal policies spurred by various science reforms. Some academic publishers have adopted AI policies for their journals without much fanfare–indeed, some of us editors only learned about the policies of their journal (or publisher) in preparation for this workshop. We agree with the commentary by the editor of Political Communication that it is essential ”to grapple with undisciplined uses of AI and related tools while we lean into their possibilities for enhancing the scope and creativity of our research” [Lawrence, 2025]. Meeting this challenge will require negotiated norms, coordinated practices, and incentive structures across journals and institutions.

We begin by outlining the four motivations that guide our investigation of the different scenarios for how to adapt peer review to the age of LLMs, each of which is discussed as a section of the manuscript below:

- The Goals of Peer Review. Peer review has evolved in an ad hoc fashion. The bundle of goals addressed by peer review is contingent; further, various journals even in the same discipline put different emphases on these goals. We clarify these different goals and discuss the way in which they can be addressed.

- The status quo is both undesirable and unsustainable. The unstable status quo produces a sense of “Fear of Missing Out” (FOMO), the sense that others are using AI to get ahead. This fear corrodes our trust in each other and our epistemic authority vis-à-vis society. Uncertainty about what kinds of AI use is acceptable can give short-term advantages to the least conscientious actors in the system. Eventually, AI usage will be evenly distributed across the actors and actions for which it is institutionally approved; we hope to shorten the current period of uncertainty.

- Think about where we want to end up. The impact of LLMs is far too large to paper over; and purely reactive approaches to incorporating them may struggle to keep pace. Before discussing individual journal policies designed to address isolated problems, we think it is essential to think about potential future states for academic publishing. We engage in a scenario-casting exercise to illustrate four possible equilibria; our aim is to push our academic community to take an active stance in deciding which equilibrium we would like to end up in, and to accelerate progress towards that equilibrium.

- Peer Review in the AI Age. Rather than treating AI solely as a problem to be contained, we should understand it as a set of capabilities that, when guided by appropriate norms and institutional design, can help us innovate peer review in ways that better serve scientific communities. The goal is to integrate it in ways that enhance quality, innovation, trust, and capacity, rather than eroding them. This will require both institutional reform and the development of new streams of diagnostic data to remain up to date as AI and its adoption continue to evolve.

2 The Goals of Peer Review

The practice of soliciting written commentary on scientific manuscripts is old; the Royal Society started soliciting independent written assessments of submissions in 1831 [Baldwin, 2018]. But adoption remained slow and uneven well into the twentieth century. In 1936, Albert Einstein was famously offended when the Physical Review sent his submitted paper to an external referee, withdrawing the manuscript with a terse note protesting that he had not authorized such treatment [Baldwin, 2015, Kennefick, 2005]. More strikingly, the prestigious journal Nature did not require external peer review for publication until 1973; previous editors could publish papers they found interesting based solely on editorial judgment [Baldwin, 2015]. The notion that external refereeing constitutes the “lynchpin of science” is thus a product of the late twentieth century, not a timeless feature of scientific practice. Indeed, the term “peer review” only entered widespread usage in the early 1970s [Moxham and Fyfe, 2018, Baldwin, 2018].

The elevation of peer review to its current status coincided with the massive post-war expansion of federal funding for science. The establishment of the National Science Foundation in 1950 and the rapid growth of the National Institutes of Health institutionalized competitive, peer-reviewed project grants as the dominant mechanism for distributing research funds [Wolfe, 2018, Guston and Keniston, 1994]. This system served multiple purposes: it justified scientific autonomy in the allocation of public money, it fit the ideological imperatives of the Cold War by rewarding individual excellence, and it provided a veneer of meritocratic legitimacy to funding decisions [Wolfe, 2018]. Peer review for grant funding developed largely independently from editorial peer review, yet the two became conceptually intertwined, jointly constituting what Guston and Keniston [1994] called the “social contract for science.”

This potted history illustrates a point central to our analysis: peer review as we know it is not the product of deliberate institutional design oriented toward clear epistemic goals [Hug, 2022]. It is an accretion of practices adopted at different times, by different actors, for different reasons; and most of these reasons were outward-facing rather than intrinsic to the goals of science. The bundle of functions now attributed to peer review was assembled piecemeal, and the relative emphasis placed on each function varies considerably across fields and journals.

We summarize our understanding of the components of this bundle:

- Certify contributions and deter fraud

- Archive knowledge

- Improve manuscripts

- Maintain legitimacy with society

- Remain up to date

- Allocate attention

- Assign academic credit

- Develop community

The first three goals are straightforward and widely understood: peer review serves to ensure that the methods used and conclusions reached are justifiable and in line with legitimate academic practice. The comments from peer reviewers also helps to improve the quality of the published manuscript. Journals are tasked with maintaining the academic record; while peer review cannot catch all errors or fraud in advance, journals have the function of correcting or retracting publications that are later found to be importantly flawed.

This connects to the next goal, of legitimating the knowledge produced from the perspective of society as a whole. Collins and Evans [2007] argue that science requires both substantive expertise (the ability to contribute to a field) and interactional expertise (the ability to engage meaningfully with specialists). Peer review operationalizes this distinction: it certifies that claims have been vetted by those with substantive expertise, rendering them trustworthy for non-experts who lack the capacity to evaluate the underlying evidence directly. Science is a messy process, so it is necessary to present outputs in a form that is consistent and legitimate. The “peer-reviewed” scientific study is now widely understood to be a vessel of high epistemic confidence.

Peer review takes time, but it is important that it not take too much time: the knowledge being published should remain up to date. This is particularly relevant for the social sciences, cursed as we are to be studying phenomena which can be affected by changes in the social, political and technological world.

Within an academic discipline, even within a given subfield, there are more scientists doing more work than their peers can possibly keep abreast of. Journals have different levels of prestige, serving as a mechanism to collectively assign more attention to the best or most important new findings.

Journal prestige also serves as a mechanism to assign credit within academic careers. University administrators, deans, department chairs, and hiring/tenure/promotion committees all treat journal publications as a crucial signal of the quality of an academic’s contribution. The fact that this is happening within a group of scholars who can expect to be writing manuscripts that are reviewed by people whose manuscripts they might in turn review helps to develop a community of scholars; by participating in this practice, researchers internalize and modify the norms of scientific practice. This implicit pedagogy maintains coherence within fields even as they evolve.

We re-iterate that we are all journal editors. It would be unreasonable to expect us to be totally indifferent to the current institutional arrangement; rather, we believe in the importance of academic peer review and publication, and we want to make it better.

Publishers have a very clear incentive to have more papers published. Individual authors have a very clear incentive to have more papers published. Universities and departments want to hire and promote the “best people,” but ultimately it requires a larger community of scholars to define “best.” Journals are the primary institutions dedicated to evaluating and ultimately deciding on what is considered “good” and “best” within a scientific community. We think that journals have a collective responsibility to provide leadership in this unsettled technosocial environment.

3 The Status Quo

3.1 What Scholars Say about Their Use of AI

Four large surveys recently queried researchers’ usage of large language models (LLMs) and their attitudes toward such usage. In early 2023, a global survey of about 2,000 asked corresponding authors of recent PubMed-indexed biomedical papers about their familiarity with, use cases for, and concerns regarding AI chatbots [Ng et al., 2025]. In February 2025 a survey of approximately 5,000 authors who had previously published in Nature asked respondents to evaluate the acceptability of different forms of LLM-assisted research and publishing [Kwon, 2025]. A third survey of authors and collaborators publishing in fifty sociology journals asked respondents between January and June 2025 about their GenAI usage practices, motivations, and broader disciplinary concerns [Alvero et al., 2025]. Finally, a paid survey of social scientists from a random sample of 30 of the 100 top-ranked US universities randomly asked about their actual and perceived acceptability of AI use [Chakravorti et al., 2025].

Given generally low response rates and the opt-in nature of these surveys, we need to be careful to generalize the conclusions to the whole population of researchers. With that caution in mind, we proceed in discussing the results of these projects.

There are large differences in reported use of AI in paper or grant proposal writing across the surveys. 28% of Nature authors report having used AI for writing, and 10-30% of sociologists say they use GenAI for various writing tasks, while 54% and 71% of biomedical researchers across two of these studies report having used AI chatbots for writing or editing grants or research papers. These percentages are likely to increase in the coming years, because the percentage of Nature authors who report being willing to use AI for writing currently lies much higher, at 71% (though only 28% report using it so far).

Assistance in academic writing is the most often used application of AI, but other uses are fairly common as well. Among biomedical researchers, more than half use LLMs for general administrative tasks, such as writing emails, for translating research material to another language, for literature search, and for understanding a research methodology. Fewer than half use it for social media engagement, generating presentation material such as slideshow decks, and analysing or interpreting data. Among sociologists, 15-20% use LLMs for literature search and data analysis. While only about 10% of Nature authors claim to have used LLMs for other research tasks, such as writing summaries of other papers for inserting into the literature review of one’s own paper, about 40% would be willing to do so.

AI in peer review has relatively low levels of reported use and acceptance among survey respondents. Among biomedical researchers who reported the highest use of AI in writing, only 33% claimed to have ever used LLMs in peer review. Of Nature authors, only 5% reported using AI to do the initial peer review of a paper. 5% found AI use in peer review appropriate if undisclosed, 15% if disclosed, and 34% if only a locally installed AI tool was used. These numbers are higher if AI is only used to answer questions about a manuscript – an important caveat to the low numbers above that speaks to the need to more carefully delineate constructive uses of LLMs, like we do below.

The results from Chakravorti et al. (2025) are particularly relevant for projecting how AI adoption will evolve over time, since they identify a significant gradient in terms of career stage: PhD students reported using AI significantly more frequently than tenured professors and were also more likely to report accepting AI usage in their research domain and finding it useful.

3.2 How we have noticed academics using AI so far

Some journals have not seen much of a difference in LLM usage over the past year. However, some of our journals have seen qualitatively noticeable changes in the submissions in the LLM era. Some authors are clearly using AI to write their papers and/or to conduct their research. This shows up in four ways, two of which we think are desirable, another two we think are undesirable.

- More low-quality papers. Some journals are seeing submissions that appear to be generated with minimal human oversight. These manuscripts have generic literature reviews, superficial engagement with prior work, and conclusions that don’t follow from the analysis. This influx functions as a kind of spam, consuming editorial resources without contributing to scientific discourse.

- More low-quality papers that superficially appear to be high-quality papers. As the use of off-the-shelf LLMs spreads, some journals are seeing an increase in manuscripts that use these tools in a relatively unsophisticated and atheoretical way. Methodologically, these commonly take the form of loosely formed RQs that are then “answered” with the kinds of variables one can easily measure with these tools, often without clear or precise reference to extant literature

- Fewer high-quality papers that superficially appear to be low-quality papers. AI being used in high-quality papers, especially by junior scholars and non-native English speakers, in ways that reduce language barriers and strengthen the contribution.

- More high-quality papers. Large-scale data collection and analysis is possible for a wider set of researchers.

The first is obviously bad, though journals have always received low-quality submissions. The difference now is volume and the reduced cost of producing such submissions. What previously required some baseline of effort and engagement with the research process can now be generated in minutes. The second result in what we call the “OOOTP effect”: submissions falling into the category of “Oh One Of Those Papers.” This is an inductive categorization emerging from a bunching of the methods being used for straightforward applications of well-trod paradigms. But outside of egregious cases like unintelligible figures, text copied straight from ChatGPT (“would you like me to…”), and hallucinated citations (Walters and Wilder, 2023; Linardon et al., 2025), there is not really a “smoking gun” of irresponsible AI usage we can point to. This increase in problematic submissions is making the initial vetting process more onerous for editorial and support teams and is increasing the rate of desk rejects while eating up time and energy for the more important and satisfying work of shepherding promising manuscripts through the editorial process. Some journals are responding by designing more streamlined desk reject workflows.

On the positive side, we are seeing AI lower barriers for researchers who previously faced disadvantages in the publication process. Non-native English speakers can now produce prose that meets journal standards without expensive editing services. The barrier to entry for coding-intensive projects has fallen for early-career researchers. And experienced researchers are able to increase the scale and speed of data collection and analysis, increasing the robustness of their results. These cases suggest that thoughtful AI integration can lower barriers to publication while maintaining or even improving research quality.

3.3 AI Disclosures

One policy on which journals seem to be headed towards consensus is mandatory “AI disclosures” included by authors as part of their manuscripts. Many journals have a “soft” requirement in the form of an AI policy listed on a general “information for authors” page, but enforcement is unclear and some editors may even be unaware of the existence of these policies, since authors encounter them as an automatic feature of the submission process. Others, including the American Journal of Political Science that one of us edits, have introduced a checkbox as part of the article submission flow, mandating that authors click the box if they used AI and that they include a separate section at the end of the manuscript explaining the details of that AI use. In this case, the decision was made by the editors, but the difference between editor-imposed and publisher-imposed rules or interface options is not always clear to authors.

One long-term vision for AI disclosures is that they be treated similarly to other parts of the “methods” sections – that they provide readers, editors and reviewers with a transparent accounting of, exactly, what AI was used for and how. But in any case, “disclosure” should proceed from a principled understanding of what we (as a field) hope to get out of it. One important such consideration is the short-term effects of its adoption. In particular, disclosure should not function as a stigma signal, its value lies in improving transparency, accountability, and collective learning about emerging practices.

Along these lines, it is important to “normalize” disclosure of the use of AI – if disclosure is spotty, then people might be afraid to disclose real use. That is, if disclosed AI is the exception, it might cause reviewers and editors to evaluate the submission differently or more critically. But if we make it something normal (like a statement of funding) then it could be useful for everyone. Another unambiguously useful contribution of the mandatory disclosure box is that it is empirical data about AI use in the academic publishing process, from which changes over time can be measured. It is impossible to go back in time to have collected baseline data, so we encourage all editors to begin and then continue recording some kind of data.

Regardless of any specific policy like mandatory disclosure, regardless even of the overall institutional landscape, one essential non-institutional component of transition to LLMs is that everyone be on an equal playing field. Ambiguity over the rules means that the least scrupulous scholars rush to gain a competitive advantage while the more cautious wait for the judgment of the field as to which applications are acceptable.

There are also equity implications of the current ambiguity: men are considerably more likely to report using generative AI, in a variety of applications (Chakravorti et al., 2025; Otis et al., 2024). Paradoxically, given the stereotype of young people as more enthusiastic adopters of new technology, it might also be the case that senior (tenured) professors are more likely to admit to maximally incorporating AI since they’ve already established their scientific credentials, while younger scholars are more concerned about hiring committees discounting their work if they admit to AI use. The open science agenda was, perhaps, the most disruptive change in (social) science in the last decade. Here, survey research on the adoption of open science research practices – such as preregistration, replication, data sharing, etc. – has not found much indication for career stage differences in the adoption of these practices (Bakker et al., 2021).

4 Forecasting Scenarios

We turn to presenting a set of possible future scenarios for the incorporation of LLMs into the publication ecosystem. They are designed to provide structured vantage points from which to consider how this ecosystem might evolve under different approaches, clarify trade-offs, and help identify AI integration pathways that can sustain the core purposes of peer review and academic publishing.

4.1 Let it Rip

This is the AI-optimist, peer-review uncritical scenario, and the direction in which we are headed by default. AI is a powerful tool for enhancing academic work, and should be incorporated by all of the relevant actors for whatever tasks they find it useful. Authors use AI to carve out research questions, refine theories, conduct literature reviews, develop research designs, write and test code, and even to flesh out the manuscript. Reviewers use AI to produce reviews which are then automatically forwarded to editors with recommendations they can simply accept or reject.

One key goal of academic publishing is degraded in this scenario, although not immediately. The peer-reviewed pdf is not the only output of the academic publishing process; we are also producing scientists when we conduct science, and scientists play other important roles in society, as teachers and as experts. Widespread, uncritical use of AI for both production and review of academic research would undermine the role of substantive expertise in knowledge production.

To acknowledge the objection, yes, it’s clear that we scientists stand to lose if our function is reduced to that of coal-stokers for the furnace of the great Knowledge Engine, producing and “peer reviewing” pdfs at a faster rate than we could ever hope to; a cynical read is that we are arguing for a kind of technological protectionism to defend academic employment. But a society without scientists is one in which there is no one with the epistemic capacity to notice that the Knowledge Engine is overheating (the planet), and no one with the epistemic authority to explain to citizens and governments how we might adapt to the various challenges that will doubtlessly emerge from the progress in pdf production. The promise of this direction is efficiency: faster writing, reviewing, and publishing. But as automated evaluation becomes easier, incentives may shift toward optimizing for machine-readable signals of quality. Over time, the system may produce more publishable text while weakening the substantive expertise that peer review is meant to mobilize, including methodological judgment, domain knowledge, and interpretive skill.

Our insistence on mentioning pdfs points to another problem with this scenario: it is “off equilibrium,” in the sense that the constituent components are unstable and unlikely to persist. If we’re using machines to read, write and review our academic pdfs, what’s the point of using pdfs at all? The academic paper is formatted to be read by humans; much of the text is irrelevant for machines with huge memories that can, for example, directly access the content of cited papers. Furthermore, machines might be better able to work with raw data or model outputs rather than summary statistics or visualizations designed to render statistical tendencies legible to humans.

Our point is that the scenario in which LLMs are used in every part of the peer review and publication process is actually insufficiently radical. If we decide to go in this direction, it does not make sense not to go even farther in this direction. If the machines are going to be doing the bulk of the epistemic work, it is more efficient for them to do so in a modality that is more natural to their architecture, whatever form that ultimately takes. We submit that it is possible that this is where social science ends up, but we believe that we are nowhere near that point; for now, at least, this is not a viable direction for social science.

4.2 Shut It Down

The alternative extreme scenario is that we collectively decide to ban the use of LLMs and all successor epistemic technologies from any part of the academic research and peer review process. Arguments in favor of this position come from a range of viewpoints. First, research should be conducted by humans: scientific inquiry is not merely the production of text (and pdfs), but a cognitive, interpretive, and ethical practice that requires accountability, reflexivity, and professional judgment. Second, and related to the first argument, some argue that we should not become dependent on LLMs for the research process. Reliance on automated tools may erode essential scholarly competencies—such as writing, critical thinking, and methodological reasoning—and could eventually deskill entire academic fields. Third, right now LLMs are relatively cheap – for Western standards – but one might worry that once we are totally dependent on LLMs, the prices will increase. Fourth, banning LLMs is seen as a way to safeguard the integrity of the scientific record. If LLM outputs remain difficult to audit, reproduce, or attribute responsibility for, then excluding them entirely becomes a precautionary strategy. Fifth, and coming from the value of preserving digital sovereignty, one could argue that science should not be dependent on the technology of big corporations that might, or might not, be compliant with national legislation.

When it comes to peer review, there are a related set of objections for using LLMs. Editors should ensure that all assessments, recommendations, and written reports are the result of human expertise rather than AI-generated language. Proponents of this scenario argue that peer review is fundamentally a judgment of quality, novelty, and rigor—dimensions that cannot be outsourced without undermining accountability. A full ban would also prevent covert uses of AI that might introduce biases, hallucinated claims, or untraceable reasoning into editorial decisions. In this model, the peer review system becomes a protected human-centric space: reviewers must rely solely on their own reading, disciplinary knowledge, and professional experience, and editors must certify that no AI assistance played a role in the evaluation of manuscripts.

A few critical questions arise when it comes to banning AI entirely. Are

LLMs the only help we get during the research process? And is it any different than older, human forms of assistance? When designing a study, for example, colleagues and dissertation advisors (in case of PhD students) often provide fundamental input on the research design of a study. When disclosed, we tend to see this as a sign of showing appreciation for intellectual collaboration. From this perspective, one might wonder whether LLMs cannot also be good sparring partners for testing and reflecting on new research ideas.

Also, in the research process, authors tend to get help from graduate or undergraduate student assistants that collect our data and other online resources that help us overcome problems in coding or writing. Similarly, when it comes to writing it is well known that scholars, especially for whom English is not their native language, turn towards language editing services (in fact some journals stipulate their use). Yet, this kind of help is not consistently disclosed. From this perspective, does it matter when we use LLM to polish our language or whether this is done by a human?

There are compelling reasons to prohibit the use of large language models in the peer review process. By agreeing to review a manuscript or grant proposal, reviewers explicitly commit to a duty of confidentiality: the content of the submission may not be shared with third parties. Using an LLM to assist in drafting a review violates this obligation, as the manuscript’s content is effectively transferred to an external system. Importantly, authors of the manuscript that was send out for peer review have not consented to this form of data sharing. Their unpublished ideas, analyses, and theoretical contributions are submitted for confidential evaluation by human peers—not for processing by opaque, proprietary models. Given the limited transparency about how LLMs handle, store, or potentially reuse input data, reviewers cannot credibly guarantee that confidentiality and author consent are safeguarded.

Beyond confidentiality, there are substantive reasons to object to the use of LLMs in peer review. First, editors invite human experts to provide their own scholarly judgment. They do not invite a tool that generates text. Delegating parts of the review process to an LLM therefore undermines the premise that a review reflects the reviewer’s personal assessment, reasoning, and responsibility. Peer review is not merely about producing evaluative prose; it is about accountable judgment by identifiable scholars.

Second, LLMs are likely to reinforce bias and promote conservatism. Trained primarily on existing literature, these models are structurally inclined toward dominant theories [Hao et al., 2026], mainstream methods, and established paradigms. As a result, innovative, interdisciplinary, or genuinely novel contributions may be less likely to be recognized or valued. While peer review already carries the risk of gatekeeping, reliance on systems that systematically privilege the status quo further exacerbates this tendency.

Third, there is no clear or enforceable boundary between limited assistance and substantive delegation of evaluative work. What begins as support with wording or structure can easily extend to summarizing arguments, generating critiques, or shaping overall judgments. In the absence of transparent and enforceable norms, the use of LLMs risks a gradual shift from support to sub stitution. This erosion undermines the idea of peer review as a meaningful intellectual contribution by scholars and risks reducing it to a largely procedural exercise.

However, just as universities and other educators are discovering, “banning” AI is not enforceable. AI detection tools are not yet reliable (Sadasivan et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023), and may never be. AI adoption could be spreading so rapidly that the “shut it down” approach is equivalent to the proverbial plugging the dyke with our digits.

4.3 Only Editors and Reviewers Get To Use AI

As editors, this is of course the solution that saves us the most time and effort: “I get to use AI, and nobody else gets to use AI.” Sadly for us, but perhaps thankfully for the legitimacy and accountability at the core of academic publication process, this is unenforceable for the same reasons as the scenario listed above.

4.4 Increase AI Production, Increase Human Evaluation

We can see this next scenario approaching as authors use AI for a broader range of tasks. For example, AI tools can improve efficiency in the research process by replacing traditional research assistant tasks, automating code generation, and streamlining analysis pipelines. Beyond this, as most scholars can attest, writing is a chief bottleneck for the completion of already-conducted research. If LLM use becomes normalized for fundamental tasks such as drafting and proofreading manuscripts, the quantity of papers in circulation could increase dramatically.

In this way, increased AI use among academic researchers is likely to result in an acceleration of ongoing trends in publishing, with skyrocketing submissions threatening to overwhelm the existing peer review system. One might be tempted to respond that the solution — per the “Let it Rip” scenario above — should be for journals to continue the arms race, employing AI as well to screen submissions for desk rejection and even to generate synthetic referee reports. A better solution, from the perspective of maintaining the critical importance of substantive expertise, would be to take advantage of the efficiency gains in the production of knowledge while maintaining human control over the review process.

How could this work? Begin by observing that for each individual researcher, the production of research papers becomes more efficient. As this fundamental part of scholarly work becomes potentially less time-consuming, so might other routine parts of the job. One might conclude from current trends that reviewing papers might be one of those tasks increasingly streamlined by LLMs. But that is not inevitable. The academic community can choose to decide that if we accept AI as part of the standard toolbox of research development and the communication of scientific results, humans must be kept in the loop of evaluating, judging, and curating high-quality and important research. This implies that a larger, not smaller, share of each individual scholar’s time should be invested in a reinvigorated peer review system.

Such a division of labor would need to be enforced by new rules and norms. Human peer review would need to be elevated to its proper role as a central task of scholarly work, with accompanying gains in status and recognition. Journal editors have always used the tools at their disposal to recruit reviewers, reciprocity and appeals to professionalism among them, but these powers are insufficient for the large-scale shift that will be needed to adapt to the coming storm. Universities and individual departments must more directly incentivize the quantity and quality of peer review contributions, for example by formally incorporating this work into service requirements for promotion and tenure (and elevating the relative weight given to these requirements). Professional associations could create and maintain public databases of review completions, obligate journals to contribute to them, and organize award competitions. Individual faculty can help inculcate these norms by training their students and involving them early in the process.

In such a system, AI tools can still play a role. For example, LLM-powered “suggested reviewer” tools could leverage citation and content information, as well as review counts from the databases, to improve editorial management efficiency. But we suspect that as AI use reduces variance in the superficial quality of papers, more human effort will be needed to filter submissions at the initial review stage — not less. Trends toward higher desk reject rates will continue, but automating this process would be risky. Increased emphasis on human evaluation in the peer review process means a partial rethinking of the overall division of labor in academia and the relative importance we assign to its various components.

5 Adapting Peer Review to the Age of AI

Having outlined these scenarios, we now discuss the various “levers” that academic journals have at their disposal to navigate the changing landscape. This overview is necessarily provisional; we are laying out what we think are promising options, but the reality is that there is little concrete evidence about how peer review will function as AI continues to develop. In particular, we lack the metascientific data necessary to evaluate the performance of these potential reforms, a point we return to at the end of this section.

Regardless of which scenario we decide to prioritize, it is important to emphasize that it is not necessary to have a single AI-use policy for all actors in the system.

5.1 On the Author Side

Our suggestion is that authors should be allowed and indeed encouraged to use AI responsibly, in ways that enhance the research process. Responsibly here is not to be understood as a mere synonym for “well.” Authors seek credit for their publications, so they must also remain responsible for them. If authors submit manuscripts with AI-generated falsehoods, misrepresentations of data, or hallucinated citations, they should be sanctioned for such behavior by, for example, limiting further manuscript submissions.

The use of AI for proofreading, especially for non-native English speakers, should broadly be a boon for social science. But this is also a tricky case, because it can involve the introduction of text that authors below a degree of English proficiency cannot double-check. We are for example hesitant to endorse manuscripts fully written in another language and then translated wholesale into English. The reasonable middle ground seems to be for authors to retain the original document submitted to an LLM for proofreading as evidence that any serious error was not introduced by them.

The situation with statistical coding is similar: there seems to be little reason not to accept AI assistance in writing statistical code. The crucial skill for researchers shifts from being able to write complicated code from scratch to having the capacity to check each step of the process and make sure that the data processing and modeling steps are done in theoretically appropriate and methodologically sound way.

The desirability of AI generating large portions of a manuscript remains an open and evolving question. The views of the authors of this manuscript vary somewhat in this regard. As AI systems continue to improve, policies will inevitably need to be revisited to reflect changing technological realities as well as enforceability. We do not think that it is a good idea to fully automate the production of literature reviews, for example, because the production of a literature review is not simply a box-checking exercise but should result from serious engagement with existing work. An LLM might usefully serve a similar role to a human research assistant, helping discover and categorize existing work – but where exactly to draw this line will continue to be revisited and contested.

At a minimum, authors must take full ownership of, and responsibility for, the words they submit for publication, regardless of how those words were produced. Accountability ultimately rests with the human author, not with the means by which the text was generated.

5.2 On the Reviewer Side

One upshot of the scenario-mapping exercise above is that it is essential that we retain human judgment in the peer review process. Reviewers are the backstop. But they are also, already, the actors in the publication process who are most under strain by increasing submission rates. The ad hoc, informal model of peer review is not sustainable.

Thus, it is critical to raise the prestige and time commitment involved in conducting peer reviews. There are several mechanisms for this, but all of them require more formal commitment from journals, academic associations, and promotion committees.

More specifically, we argue for a cautious stance against the use of AI assistance in generating peer reviews. It is crucial to retain human judgment in our processes of peer evaluation and curation, and not to automate away these critical tasks. First, insisting that human reviewers generate the core ideas in any assessment of scholarly work is not unreasonable. Based on this, some may then defend the practice of asking an LLM to take one’s human-authored notes and use them to generate a full review. We think it’s important to resist this impulse, since the process of writing is itself a form of thought, perhaps the most essential and irreducible step in the process of reasoning and judgment.

It may become necessary to give clearer advice to reviewers about the expected length and scope of their reviews, perhaps setting reasonable and enforceable limits. Standardization could go hand in hand with clearer norms about the central importance of peer review for maintaining humans in the loop of scholarly knowledge production.

To make the overall package more palatable to reviewers being asked to commit more time and effort to each manuscript they review, it is essential to limit the number of reviews they conduct, and to ensure greater equality in terms of reviewer burden. This requires re-structuring of journal practices and increased coordination across journals, which we now discuss.

5.3 On the Journal Side

The peer review workflow in social science is broadly standardized: authors submit a manuscript, a research assistant checks that it has the necessary components, an editor or team of editors decides whether to desk reject, if not they send the manuscript out to 2-4 reviewers, at which point the editor either rejects the manuscript or invites revisions to be submitted, then the authors revise and either the editor makes a decision or sends it back out to peer reviewers (usually the same ones) and then accepts, rejects, or goes for another round of peer review.

Something about this process has to be changed in light of the proliferation of AI, and especially if we adopt changes to the subprocess of external reviewer reports. The most obvious place where AI can increase efficiency without inhibiting substantive human evaluation is the first step. Papers must meet minimal requirements in terms of formatting, anonymization, and disclosures; this check can be done by AI, either one controlled by the journal or a public-facing LLM in which prospective authors can have their manuscript approved without ever interacting with a submission manager. For reasons of copyright and reproducibility, it is essential that these LLMs not be proprietary models but rather open-source, locally run models that journals fine-tune for this purpose [Palmer et al., 2024].

These tools can expand the scope of these technical checks to include things like computational reproducibility of quantitative analysis, alignment of analysis with pre-registration, and consistency in substantive conclusions throughout the manuscript. Authors could seek to have these mechanical features approved by journal-controlled LLMs before submission, with the option to explain if the LLM makes an error and requires a human check. These sub-components only exist for some types of manuscripts, but given the increased workload required for these open science practices (Hostler, 2023), this is a noteworthy place to seek efficiency gains from AI.

Let us highlight a few concrete examples of how such journal-controlled AI tools could be used in practice. First, AI could assist in detecting incorrect or hallucinated references. AI-based reference validation tools can be used by authors prior to submission and by journals during editorial screening to verify whether cited sources exist and are correctly attributed. This addresses a problem that has become more salient with the increased use of AI-generated text, namely the inclusion of fabricated or inaccurate citations. As recent audits of accepted conference papers have shown, such errors are not hypothetical but already occur in published work (Shmatko et al., 2023). Automating this check at an early stage could reduce reviewer burden and help journals prevent avoidable errors that undermine credibility.

Second, AI could support journals in evaluating compliance with Open Science and transparency criteria. Social science research increasingly relies on preregistration, data and code sharing, and transparent reporting, yet checking adherence to these standards remains labor-intensive and inconsistently applied. AI tools could assist by systematically verifying whether required materials are present, whether preregistered analyses are referenced appropriately, and whether key transparency statements are included. Such tools could also generate structured, criterion-based feedback for authors, thereby increasing consistency across manuscripts while leaving substantive evaluation firmly in human hands. Initial efforts in this direction already exist (e.g., Bekkers et al., 2023; Cummins et al., 2023), suggesting that this is a feasible and promising area for AI-assisted editorial workflows.

Finally, AI could contribute to reproducibility checks at later stages of the review process Some journals already require computational reproducibility prior to acceptance, but executing and verifying analysis scripts is time-consuming and often falls on editors or reviewers. Under carefully specified conditions, AI systems could assist by running code, checking whether reported results can be reproduced, and flagging discrepancies for human inspection. While such applications are necessarily limited to certain methodological approaches, they align closely with existing journal policies and represent another area where AI could increase efficiency without substituting for substantive scholarly judgment.

Larger-scale institutional reforms are likely necessary in light of the changes discussed above. For example, journals could establish larger editorial boards with an obligation for members to actively participate in an expanded, more robust desk-reject process, a practice which is already mostly in place at the Journal of Communication, which two of us edit. Editors and associate editors are the front line of the review process, but this workflow could be expanded to a larger team with more buy-in. A more ambitious reform would involve explicit and perhaps even public record of the reviews done by every reviewer, but this creates issues around interdisciplinarity and could also decrease review quality; we think that expanding and formalizing the role of the editorial board is more incentive-compatible. Journals could also experiment with adapting models from disciplines like computer science, such as archival conferences with time-constrained review and meta-review.

More controversially, we should seriously consider decisively adopting reforms such as submission fees and caps on submissions by authors. As AI reduces frictions in the production of manuscripts, and insofar as we insist on a human role for the evaluation and curation of knowledge, it is essential to introduce some frictions to avoid a flood of submissions that overwhelms our institutions. Modest submission fees (around $50) are already in place in a journal one of us edits (Sociological Science), and can be waived for junior scholars or scholars in other disadvantaged financial contexts. These fees are a modest funding source for the journal but primarily serve to encourage authors to pre-filter their work in terms of fit and quality.

Author-level caps on submissions are even more extreme – for a recent call to write less papers, see. Certainly, we understand the risk of restricting the contribution of particularly productive scholars, and perhaps this is one area where journals are not the best actors to impose structure on the academic system; rather, hiring/tenure/promotion committees could elect to evaluate only a fixed number of publications, prioritizing quality over quantity. If certain authors decide to use AI to produce an implausible number of manuscripts (a decision which we would oppose), some kind of formal submission cap might be necessary.

Finally, journals should coordinate on developing policies and definitions around AI reporting standards, following the broadly standardized expectations around reporting conflicts of interest. This is one of the data flows we see as essential for continuously updating peer review as AI develops, the topic to which we now turn.

5.4 What Kind of Data Would We Want?

The reforms discussed above cannot be evaluated in the abstract. To know whether any given policy is working, we need systematic data that currently does not exist. The academic publishing system has operated for decades with remarkably little empirical self-examination. If we are serious about adapting peer review to the age of AI, we must also be serious about building the informational infrastructure to monitor that adaptation.

First, journals that implement mandatory AI disclosure checkboxes should archive and analyze this data longitudinally. Crucially, this data collection should be coordinated across journals to allow for comparative analysis.

Second, systematic researcher surveys, ideally coordinated across multiple journals and disciplines, are a key tool. These surveys should distinguish between different use cases and should probe both adoption and normative perceptions of what is acceptable. Panel surveys would be even more informative, though likely only possible with significant effort and resources.

Third, qualitative reports of the experiences by reviewers and editors. We know very little about how the peer review process feels to the participants. The workshop prompting this manuscript was a unique experience for many of us; we simply lack the institutions to promote qualitative knowledge sharing among editors, to say nothing of reviews. Open-response surveys and focus groups could surface problems and even propose solutions.

Fourth, editors and journals have the institutional capacity to conduct experiments on the peer review process. This is costly and thus rare, though the examples that do exist are quite informative (Tomkins et al., 2017; Cortes and Lawrence, 2021). The larger institutional reforms discussed above should be tested with coordinated experiments across journals; for smaller reforms, the experiments should be repeated across time.

Finally, quantitative streams of data about submission, desk reject, and acceptance rates is a straightforward but important source of data about how AI has impacted the core functioning of the peer review process. Many journals already collect and report this data, but the value of this effort would be enhanced if reporting standards were synchronized across journals.

6 Conclusion

We are at a pivotal moment for academic publishing. The decisions made by journals, professional associations, and individual scholars over the next few years may shape the trajectory of peer review for decades. It is essential that we take advantage of AI to enhance and streamline some procedural parts of the peer review process in order to preserve the social and epistemic components that cannot be replaced: the cultivation of expertise, the exercise of scholarly judgment, the development of scientific community.

The scenarios we have outlined are not predictions but structured ways of thinking about the choices before us. We believe that the “Increase AI Production, Increase Human Evaluation” scenario best preserves what is valuable about peer review while acknowledging that AI will inevitably transform how research is conducted and communicated. But reaching this equilibrium will require coordinated action across institutions that have historically operated in isolation.

The reforms we have sketched are provisional. We lack the empirical foundation to know which policies will work as AI capabilities continue to evolve. This is why we have emphasized the need for new streams of metascientific data: disclosure tracking, researcher surveys, qualitative accounts from editors and reviewers, and coordinated experiments on the peer review process itself. Without this infrastructure, we will be flying blind, reacting to crises rather than shaping outcomes. Journals are uniquely positioned to generate this data, and we call on our fellow editors to begin collecting it systematically so that future decisions can be informed by evidence rather than intuition alone.

Finally, we recognize that younger scholars face a particular burden. They must navigate an ambiguous landscape in which the rules are unclear and the pressures to publish intense. Senior scholars and journal editors have a responsibility to clarify norms, to model responsible AI use, and to ensure that the transition to new practices does not advantage the least scrupulous or most risk-tolerant at the expense of those who act in good faith and with greater caution. The peer review system belongs to the social science community as a whole; it is ours to reform, and now is the time to do it.
